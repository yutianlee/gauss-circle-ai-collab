## Detailed reasoning

### 1. H4 -- Finite Vaaler approximation with floor-compatible residual

The repo currently writes, for $H\ge1$,

$$
\psi_F(t)=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}\,e(ht)+R_H^F(t),
\qquad
\alpha_{h,H}= -\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
$$
$$
|R_H^F(t)|\le\frac1{2H+2}K_H(t),
\qquad
K_H(t)=\sum_{|k|\le H}\Bigl(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\Bigr)e(kt).
$$

**Source anchors** (from Vaaler, *Bull. Amer. Math. Soc.* **12**(2), 183--216, 1985):

* **Theorem 6**, printed p. 192, equation (2.28):
  $\widehat J(t)=\pi t(1-|t|)\cot(\pi t)+|t|$ for $0<|t|<1$.
  This matches our $\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u$ when $u=|h|/(H+1)$.
* **Section 7**, printed p. 207, equations (7.1)--(7.3): definition of the functions $i_N,j_N,k_N$; the convolution $\psi*j_N$ produces a trigonometric polynomial, and the kernel $k_N$ is a non-negative Fejer-type kernel.
* **Theorem 18**, printed p. 210, equations (7.13)--(7.17), especially (7.14):
  $|\psi*j_N(x)-\psi(x)|\le (2N+2)^{-1}k_N(x)$.

**Translation** (already established in earlier rounds):

* Vaaler's $N$ becomes our $H$.
* Vaaler's $k_N$ becomes our $K_H$; the explicit Fejer form is
$$
  K_H(t)=\frac1{H+1}\Bigl(\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}\Bigr)^2,
$$
  and the residual majorant in Vaaler is exactly $(2H+2)^{-1}K_H(t)$.
* The coefficient $\alpha_{h,H}$ is obtained from the Fourier transform of Vaaler's $J$. Under $e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}$ the sign is negative; the repo form with $\Phi$ is consistent with Theorem 6.

**Integer jump convention**. Vaaler's centered sawtooth vanishes at integers. The floor-compatible $\psi_F$ satisfies $\psi_F(n)=-\tfrac12$. The discrepancy is covered because
$$
K_H(0)=H+1,\qquad\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12.
$$
Thus the residual $R_H^F$ absorbs the half-jump. This must remain flagged in the proof draft.

**Coefficient conjugacy**.
$$
\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}
$$
because $\Phi(|h|/(H+1))$ is real and the denominator changes sign and conjugates:
$$
\alpha_{-h,H}= -\frac{\Phi(|{-h}|/(H+1))}{2\pi i(-h)}
= \frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}
= \overline{-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}}
= \overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

**Special values of $\Phi$** (for code validation):
$$
\Phi(1/2)=\tfrac12,\quad
\Phi(1/4)=\frac{3\pi}{16}+\frac14,\quad
\Phi(3/4)=\frac{3}{4}-\frac{3\pi}{16}.
$$
The identity $\Phi(u)+\Phi(1-u)=1$ is correct; hence $\Phi(1/4)\neq\Phi(3/4)$ and no false symmetry may be imposed.

**Remaining task**: The proof draft should quote exact printed page/equation numbers from a rendered Vaaler PDF; the above labels are consistent with earlier rounds' source anchors, but a final check against a facsimile is prudent before marking H4 "fully source-normalized".

### 2. R5-Full -- Fejer residual product-count bound

The proof uses, for $H\asymp D X^{-1/4}$ and $\Delta=D/H\asymp X^{1/4}$, the pointwise Fejer bound

$$
K_H(t)\ll\min\!\Bigl(H,\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\Bigr),
$$
so that

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)\ll\min\!\Bigl(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\Bigr).
$$

**First residual leg**. Pick $m$ nearest to $X/d$; because $d\asymp D$,

$$
\Bigl\|\frac Xd\Bigr\|=\frac{|X-md|}{d}\asymp\frac{|X-md|}{D}.
$$

Define the piecewise weight

$$
W_\Delta(u)=\begin{cases}1,&u=0,\\\min(1,\Delta^2/u^2),&u\neq0.\end{cases}
$$

Then

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)\ll W_\Delta(X-md).
$$

Grouping by $n=md$ yields multiplicity at most $\tau(n)$; hence

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

The final estimate uses the divisor bound $\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon$ and the elementary summation

$$
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}W_\Delta(X-n)\ll\Delta+1\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

**Second residual legs**. The kernel is $K_H((X/d+\rho)/4)$ with $\rho\in\{1,3\}$. Near-integrality of this argument is equivalent to $X\approx d(4m-\rho)$. Writing $\ell=4m-\rho$ gives $n=d\ell$ with the congruence $\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4$, and the congruence only restricts divisor multiplicity. The same product-count bound follows.

**Exact-resonance safety**. The cap $W_\Delta(0)=1$ correctly handles the Fejer spike when $X/d$ or $(X/d+\rho)/4$ is exactly an integer, because then $K_H(0)=H+1$ and $(1/H)K_H(0)=1+1/H\le2$. A singular expression like "$\max(1,\Delta^2/|X-md|^2)$" would be wrong; the approved version uses $\min(1,\Delta^2/u^2)$ with a separate case for $u=0$.

**Small-$X$ and short-block handling**. Blocks with $D<X^{1/4}$ are handled before Vaaler expansion by $|\psi_F|\le1/2$ and contribute $O(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$. The final proof draft must record this.

### 3. Raw-vs-paired M9 regression

The raw two-sided definitions are

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)=-4\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}
\sum_{d\asymp D}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),
$$

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)=4\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}
\bigl(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\bigr)\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

If $w_D$ is real-valued, then

$$
A_h:=\sum_{d}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d)
$$

satisfies $A_{-h}=\overline{A_h}$; similarly $B_{-h}=\overline{B_h}$. Using $\alpha_{-h,H_D}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H_D}}$ and grouping the terms for $h$ and $-h$ gives the paired real formulas:

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
= \frac4\pi\sum_{h=1}^{H_D}\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}\,\Im A_h,
$$

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
= -\frac8\pi\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}\,\chi_4(h)\,\Re B_h.
$$

These are **proved algebraic identities** under the real-weight hypothesis. A numerical regression must verify agreement with the raw complex sums to within round-off.

**Complex-weight failure test**. If $w_D$ is not real, the conjugations $A_{-h}=\overline{A_h}$ fail, and the paired formulas are generally false. A deliberate test with a non-real weight should exhibit a discrepancy larger than machine precision.

### 4. Fixed-coefficient stress comparison

To gauge whether the specific Vaaler coefficients $\alpha_{h,H_D}$ carry special cancellation, one should compare the actual $|\mathcal M_i|$ with

* **Random-phase stress**: replace each $\alpha_{h,H_D}$ by $|\alpha_{h,H_D}|e^{2\pi i\theta_h}$ with independent uniform random $\theta_h$.
* **Adversarial sign stress**: choose signs $\pm1$ heuristically (e.g. all positive for one leg) as an upper-bound stress.
* **Termwise $L^1$ stress**: bound $|\mathcal M_i|$ by the product of the absolute sums, i.e. replace $\alpha_{h,H_D}$ by its absolute value.

These comparisons are diagnostic only; they do **not** prove or disprove M9.

### 5. M2 Cauchy kernel diagnostics

Recall

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)=4\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h B_h,
\qquad C_h=e(h/4)-e(3h/4).
$$

**(a) Unweighted $h$-Cauchy**:
$$
|\mathcal M_2|^2\le 16\,H_D\sum_h|C_h|^2|B_h|^2
\;\Rightarrow\; \text{diagonal }\lesssim H_D\,D\asymp D^2 X^{-1/4}.
$$
At $D\asymp X^{1/2}$ this is $X^{3/4}$, exceeding the squared target $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$.

**(b) Weighted $|\alpha_h|$-Cauchy**:
$$
|\mathcal M_2|^2\le 16\Bigl(\sum_h|\alpha_h|\Bigr)\sum_h|\alpha_h|\,|C_h|^2|B_h|^2.
$$
With $\sum_h|\alpha_h|\asymp\log H_D$ the diagonal mass becomes $D\log^2 H_D$, acceptable under $X^\epsilon$. However, $|C_h|^2$ is $4$ for odd $h$ and $0$ for even $h$; the sign of $\chi_4(h)$ is lost, leaving only odd-frequency support. This is a **bounded-scope diagnostic**.

Explicit kernels:

$$
K_{2;d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}=w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2)\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}|\alpha_{h,H_D}||C_h|^2
e\Bigl(\frac{hX}{4}\Bigl(\frac1{d_1}-\frac1{d_2}\Bigr)\Bigr).
$$

For verification one should report diagonal mass, off-diagonal signed mass, off-diagonal absolute mass, operator norm, and signed/unsigned ratios.

### 6. Fourth-moment bins and near-collision bands

The two-sided $S_2=\mathcal M_2/4$ satisfies

$$
|S_2|^4=\sum_{\mathbf h,\mathbf d}\beta_{h_1}\overline{\beta_{h_2}}\beta_{h_3}\overline{\beta_{h_4}}
\prod_{j=1}^4 w_D(d_j)
e\Bigl(\frac X4\Bigl(\frac{h_1}{d_1}-\frac{h_2}{d_2}+\frac{h_3}{d_3}-\frac{h_4}{d_4}\Bigr)\Bigr),
$$
with $\beta_h=\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h$. The cleared-denominator resonance integer is

$$
N=h_1d_2d_3d_4-h_2d_1d_3d_4+h_3d_1d_2d_4-h_4d_1d_2d_3.
$$

**Exact $N=0$ families** (as proposed by A2) include:

* exact diagonal;
* pair-swapped;
* semi-diagonal;
* denominator-paired ($h_1=ac,\;h_2=bc$ etc.);
* mixed patterns and sign-symmetric variants;
* truncation-edge and unclassified residue.

For each family one can compute signed and absolute coefficient-weighted mass. The taxonomy is **incomplete**; not all families are proved to be below the required threshold.

**Near-collision bands**. For $0<|N|\sim T$ one bins the values and reports signed vs. absolute mass. A naive absolute-mass heuristic suggests a total mass as large as $X^{5/4}$ (or $X^{9/4}$) at the endpoint; this heuristic must be tested numerically with actual $\beta_h$ weights, gcd structure, and dyadic cutoffs.

**Denominator-paired singular checks**. Cases where denominators in the parametrization vanish or lie near truncation boundaries (e.g. $h_1=ac$, $h_3=-bc$, or small $|h_1-ac|$) require separate bounding.

### 7. CRI endpoint tests

Under the two-sided convention,

$$
S_2=2i(\Sigma_1-\Sigma_3),\qquad
\Sigma_r=\sum_{\substack{1\le|h|\le H_D\\h\equiv r\;(4)}}\alpha_{h,H_D}\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

The ratio

$$
R_{\rm CRI}=\frac{|\Sigma_1-\Sigma_3|^2}{|\Sigma_1|^2+|\Sigma_3|^2}
$$

is a falsification statistic: values near $1$ indicate little cancellation; values significantly below $1$ may justify deeper study. Because the denominator is the sum of two squares, $0\le R_{\rm CRI}\le2$.

One should compute $R_{\rm CRI}$ at the endpoint $D\asymp X^{1/2}$ and at intermediate scales. If the ratio is consistently near $1$, CRI is not a promising route.

### 9. High-precision Fejer safety

Evaluating $K_H(t)$ via $\frac1{H+1}(\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t})^2$ with ordinary floating-point arithmetic near integer $t$ is numerically unstable. The implementation must:

* Detect exact integers $t$ (or rationals with small denominator) and set $K_H(t)=H+1$.
* For near-integer $t$ use high-precision arithmetic or the explicit sum formula $K_H(t)=\sum_{|k|\le H}(1-|k|/(H+1))e(kt)$ evaluated via a stable FFT.
* For the shifted legs, detect integer or small-denominator rational values of $(X/d+\rho)/4$.

Without these precautions, the residual tables can show spurious blow-ups or miss exact spikes, invalidating the test.

### 10. B-process/Hardy-window test

The B-process (Poisson in $d$) applied to $\mathcal M_2$ leads, after stationary phase, to a phase $\sqrt{hXm}$ and a local divisor-window coefficient

$$
X^{1/4}q^{-3/4}\sum_{\substack{h\mid q\\h\asymp D\sqrt{q/X}}}h\beta_h\,
w_D\!\Bigl(\frac12\sqrt{\frac{h^2X}{q}}\Bigr),\qquad q=hm.
$$

One can test this structural form on small scales:

* Choose $X=10^6$, $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, compute the primal $\mathcal M_2$, and compare with the reconstructed sum over $q$ using the stationary-phase approximation. Agreement should be only approximate; the test is a **sanity check** that the algebra is consistent, not an asymptotic estimate.
* Compute signed and absolute masses of the local divisor-window coefficient to see whether any cancellation survives beyond the absolute-value majorant.

### 11. H13 endpoint test

The H13 transform (B-process-first) applied to the spatial-character M9a sum transfers $\chi_4(d)$ to a dual variable, producing a dual character $\chi_4(m)$ and a square-root phase $\sqrt{Xhm}$. At the endpoint $D\asymp X^{1/2}$ and maximal $h\asymp X^{1/4}$ the dual length is $m\asymp X^{1/4}$. One can implement a small test:

* Start with a primal sum over a handful of $d$ values.
* Apply an exact Poisson summation modulo 4 (using numeric integration for the stationary phase if needed).
* Form the quadratic form (post-Cauchy--Schwarz) that would be used in a spacing argument, and check whether the dual $\chi_4(m)$ appears as a diagonal unitary conjugation (in which case Q1-Spectral erases it) or in a genuinely signed off-diagonal statistic.

This is intended only as a **falsification test**, not as a proof route.

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps

1. **Real weight assumption for paired formulas**. The paired real forms are valid only for real dyadic weights $w_D$; complex weights must revert to the raw two-sided sums. This restriction must be stated in the proof draft.

2. **Short blocks $D<X^{1/4}$**. The product-count proof assumes $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$; for very small $D$ the natural Vaaler height might be $<1$. These blocks must be bounded separately using $|\psi_F|\le1/2$; the draft should include an explicit line.

3. **Positivity of $\ell=4m-\rho$** in the shifted legs. For large $X$ the choice $m$ nearest to $X/d$ may yield $\ell=0$ or negative values; the proof should assert that $\ell$ is positive in the active range after a small-$X$ separation.

4. **Nearest-integer tie rule**. When the argument lies exactly halfway between two integers a deterministic rule must be fixed and the estimates checked for that rule.

5. **CRI sufficiency**. The current CRI statistic is not linked to an actual bound for $\mathcal M_2$; it remains a diagnostic until a theorem-level implication is supplied.

6. **Fourth-moment taxonomy**. Several exact-resonance families are unclassified; the claimed target $|S_2|^4\ll X^{1+\epsilon}$ is **not** proved.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

* **Fejer resonances**: Test $X$ that are highly composite, containing many divisors in $[X^{1/4},X^{1/2}]$. The product-count bound must hold; if tables show larger-than-expected residuals, it could indicate an overlooked logarithmic factor.

* **CRI neutral behavior**: If $R_{\rm CRI}\approx1$ across many samples, it signals that the two residue classes do not interfere usefully, deprioritizing CRI as a cancellation mechanism.

* **Fourth-moment absolute mass**: If the observed absolute mass in the near-collision bands significantly exceeds the target, any absolute-majorant approach (including naive Bombieri--Iwaniec estimates) would be hopeless. This does **not** rule out a signed approach.

* **B-process/Hardy-window cancellation**: If the signed local divisor-window coefficient sums to nearly zero for many $q$, it might suggest hidden structure; conversely, if the signed sum resembles the absolute sum, that route is likely dead.

### Symbolic / manual checks

### Implementational notes

* Fejer kernel: switch to exact value $H+1$ when $t\in\mathbb Z$; otherwise use high-precision or FFT-based summation.
* Product counting: compute $n=md$ and $n=d(4m-\rho)$ with arbitrary-precision integers.
* CRI and fourth-moment tests: use the exact two-sided convention or clearly state conversion constants.

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

Instead of continuing the search for a pure analytic M9 estimate, one might consider a **computational certificate**: if high-precision numerical evaluation of $\mathcal M_1,\mathcal M_2$ over a wide range of $X$ and $D$ shows that the normalized sums $|\mathcal M_i|/X^{1/4}$ stay bounded by a small constant (independent of $\epsilon$), that would provide strong evidence that the conjectural exponent is sharp. Such a computational experiment--while not a proof--could guide further theoretical work by identifying the hardest parameter regimes (e.g., near-square $X$). The project could set up a massively parallel computation using known techniques for evaluating exponential sums (FFT, non-uniform DFT) to push the range to, say, $X=10^{12}$ for a few selected $D$, and report the observed growth rate. This is a purely empirical alternative, but it would complement the analytic diagnostics and give a concrete falsification target: if the sums ever exceed, say, $10\,X^{1/4}$, the conjectural bound would be in doubt.
