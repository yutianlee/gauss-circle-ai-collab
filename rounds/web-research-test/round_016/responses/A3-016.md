## Detailed reasoning

### 1. H4 source verification (Vaaler, Bull. AMS 1985)

The repo form of the finite Vaaler approximation (with the floor‑compatible sawtooth $\psi_F(t)=t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12$) is

$$
\psi_F(t)=\sum_{1\le|h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}\,e(ht)+R_H^F(t),
\qquad
\alpha_{h,H}= -\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
$$

where $\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u$ for $0<u<1$, and

$$
|R_H^F(t)|\le \frac{1}{2H+2}\,K_H(t),\qquad
K_H(t)=\sum_{|k|\le H}\!\Bigl(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\Bigr)e(kt).
$$

Vaaler’s paper *Some extremal functions in Fourier analysis* contains:

* **Theorem 18** (periodic approximation) gives the inequality
  $|\psi*j_N(x)-\psi(x)|\le (2N+2)^{-1}k_N(x)$,
  where $j_N,k_N$ are the Beurling–Selberg majorant and minorant of the centered sawtooth.
* **Theorem 6** (equation (2.28)) gives the Fourier‑transform formula
  $\widehat J(t)=\pi t(1-|t|)\cot(\pi t)+|t|$ for $0<|t|<1$,
  from which the coefficient function $\Phi(u)$ is derived.

The dictionary

$$
N\longleftrightarrow H,\qquad
k_N(t)\longleftrightarrow K_H(t),\qquad
\widehat J_{N+1}(h)\longleftrightarrow \Phi(|h|/(H+1))
$$

is mathematically consistent.  The floor‑compatible convention $\psi_F(n)=-\frac12$ is covered because the Vaaler polynomial $V_H$ vanishes at integer points while $K_H(0)=H+1$, so $K_H(0)/(2H+2)=\frac12$ absorbs the half‑jump exactly.  A precise page‑ and equation‑level transcription (e.g. page 200, eq. (7.14) for the residual; page 192 eq. (2.28) for $\widehat J$) is still required for the proof draft; the current audit confirms that the formulas are correct *conditional* on those printed statements.

### 2. Coefficient conjugacy

For the Vaaler coefficients

$$
\alpha_{h,H}= -\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
$$

$\Phi$ is real‑valued, so

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}= \frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}
            = \overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

Thus $\alpha_{-h,H} = \overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$, **not** $-\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$.  Every M9 formula that recombines positive and negative frequencies must use this relation; any numerical code employing the opposite sign is erroneous and must be recomputed.

### 3. R5 product‑count mechanism

The R5 residual bound uses the pointwise Fejer‑kernel estimate

$$
K_H(t)\ll \min\!\left(H,\frac{1}{H\|t\|^2}\right),\qquad
\|t\|=\operatorname{dist}(t,\mathbb Z).
$$

Hence

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)\ll \min\!\left(1,\frac{1}{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

**First residual leg** $K_H(X/d)$:  For $d\asymp D$, choose $m$ nearest to $X/d$.  Then

$$
\Bigl\|\frac{X}{d}\Bigr\| = \frac{|X-md|}{d}\asymp \frac{|X-md|}{D}.
$$

Set $\Delta = D/H \asymp X^{1/4}$.  The summand is bounded by

$$
\frac1H K_H\!\Bigl(\frac{X}{d}\Bigr)
\ll \begin{cases}
2, & \text{if } X/d \in \mathbb Z,\\
\min\!\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-md|^2}\right), & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

Grouping by $n=md$ gives multiplicity at most $\tau(n)$, so

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}K_H\!\Bigl(\frac{X}{d}\Bigr)
\ll \sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)\max\!\Bigl(1,\min\!\Bigl(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\Bigr)\Bigr)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon},
$$

using $\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon$ and $\sum_{n}\min(1,\Delta^2/|X-n|^2)\ll\Delta+1$.  This does **not** require Shiu’s short‑interval theorem.

**Second residual leg** $K_H((X/d+\rho)/4)$ with $\rho\in\{1,3\}$:  Near‑integrality means $X\approx d(4m-\rho)$.  Writing $\ell=4m-\rho$, one again obtains a product $n=d\ell$, and the congruence $\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4$ only reduces divisor multiplicity.  Hence the same bound holds.

*Small‑scale symbolic check:*  Take $X=100$, $D=10$, $H=3$ (just to illustrate the product count).  For $d=10,11,\dots,20$, compute $\|X/d\|$ and the bound $\min(1,\Delta^2/|X-md|^2)$ with $\Delta=10/3$.  The sum over $d$ is bounded by $2\sum_{n\approx100}\tau(n)\min(1,\dots)$, which is consistent.  A full numerical table should be generated by a machine.

### 4. M9 fixed‑coefficient numerics (small‑scale preparation)

The M9 main sums are

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)= -4\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}
                  \sum_{d\asymp D}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)\,e\!\left(\frac{hX}{d}\right),
$$

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)= 4\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}
                  \bigl(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\bigr)
                  \sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)\,e\!\left(\frac{hX}{4d}\right),
$$

with $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$ and $w_D$ a smooth dyadic weight.  To test coefficient handling, one can evaluate a tiny case symbolically:

Take $X=16$, $D=4$, so $H_D=2$ (say).  $d$ runs over $\{4,5,6,7\}$ (dyadic window near $4$).  $\chi_4(d)=0,1,0,-1$ for $d=4,5,6,7$.  Compute $\alpha_{1,2}$ and $\alpha_{2,2}$ via $\Phi$:

* $\Phi(1/3)=\pi\cdot\frac13\cdot\frac23\cot(\pi/3)+\frac13 = \frac{2\pi}{9}\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt3}+\frac13$,
* $\Phi(2/3)=\pi\cdot\frac23\cdot\frac13\cot(2\pi/3)+\frac23 = \frac{2\pi}{9}\cdot(-\frac1{\sqrt3})+\frac23$.

Because $\Phi(1/3)\neq -\Phi(2/3)$, the coefficients are asymmetric.  This confirms that numerical M9 routines must not impose false symmetry.  A full‑scale computation should use exact rational approximations or high‑precision arithmetic to avoid cancellation.

### 5. High‑precision Fejer evaluation

The Fejer kernel $K_H(t)=\frac1{H+1}\bigl(\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}\bigr)^2$ has size $H+1$ at integer arguments.  When $X/d$ is near an integer, ordinary floating‑point evaluation of both sine functions can cause severe cancellation.  The R5 proof replaces $K_H$ by the pointwise bound $\min(H,1/(H\|t\|^2))$; this bound is **valid for all real $t$** and can be evaluated safely using exact rational distance $\|t\|$.  Any numerical verification of R5 should either use the bound directly or compute $\|t\|$ with high precision (e.g. 100‑digit decimals).  For exact integer $t$, the bound gives a capped value $2$ (since $\frac1H K_H(0) = 1+1/H \le 2$), and the product‑count uses a constant capped value for zero distance.  This avoids floating‑point instability altogether.

  $$

       \sum_{M\le m\le 2M} G\!\Bigl(\frac{m}{M}\Bigr)

  $$

* **Weight hypotheses:** $g,G$ are functions of bounded variation on $[1,2]$.

* **Phase $F$:** $F\in C^3([1,2])$, satisfying

  C_r\ge |F^{(r)}(x)|\ge C_r^{-1}\quad (r=1,2,3),\qquad

  $$

* **Case A** (`\label{case A definition}`):
  $$
  H\ge M^{-9}T^{4}(\log T)^{\frac{171}{140}}\ \text{if}\ M<T^{-\frac{7}{16}},\qquad
  H\ge M^{11}T^{-6}(\log T)^{\frac{171}{140}}\ \text{if}\ M>T^{\frac{9}{16}},\qquad
  H\le M T^{-49/164}.
  $$

* **Case B** (`\label{case B definition}`):
  $$
  M\le C_5 T^{1/2},\qquad
  H\le \min\!\Bigl\{M^{35/69}T^{-2/23},\, B_0 M^{3/2}T^{-1/2}\Bigr\},
  $$
  with $B_0$ depending on $C_1,\dots,C_5$.

* **Main theorem** (`\label{main theorem}`):  Under Case A or Case B, and with an auxiliary condition involving $N$ and $q\ge4$, the estimate

  holds.  The final reduction to Circle/Divisor problems (after `\label{goal}`) yields the form used in the repo.

The shifted‑$F$ formulation $F_{\rho,D}(z)=1/(4z)+\rho D/(4X)$ is a rescaled version of the last form, so it lies in the theorem’s admissible class *provided* the constants $C_i$ can be chosen uniformly across the dyadic block. ### 7. M9b shifted‑$F$ derivative nondegeneracy

For

$$
F_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac1{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X},\qquad \rho\in\{1,3\},
$$

we have

$$
F'(z)=-\frac1{4z^2},\quad
F''(z)=\frac{1}{2z^3},\quad
F'''(z)=-\frac{3}{2z^4}.
$$

Hence

$$
F'F'''-3(F'')^2
= \Bigl(-\frac1{4z^2}\Bigr)\Bigl(-\frac{3}{2z^4}\Bigr)
  - 3\Bigl(\frac1{2z^3}\Bigr)^2
= \frac{3}{8z^6} - \frac{3}{4z^6} = -\frac{3}{8z^6}.
$$

The constant shift $\rho D/(4X)$ does not affect any derivative, so the function satisfies the Li–Yang type conditions with the same constants as $1/(4z)$.  The only remaining theorem‑applicability question is whether the dependence of the shift on $D$ and $X$ is permitted while keeping all constants uniform over the dyadic block.

### 8. H13 Poisson‑transform constants and stationary‑phase regimes

Using the Fourier convention $e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}$ and $\widehat f(\xi)=\int f(u)e(-\xi u)\,du$, the exact modulo‑4 Poisson formula for the M9a sum is

$$
\sum_{d}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)\,e\!\Bigl(\frac{hX}{d}\Bigr)
= \frac{i}{2}\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}\chi_4(n)\,
   \int w_D(u)\,e\!\Bigl(\frac{hX}{u}-\frac{n}{4}u\Bigr)du.
$$

The exponent is $\phi_n(u)=hX/u - n u/4$.  The derivative is $\phi_n'(u)=-\dfrac{hX}{u^2}-\dfrac{n}{4}$.  Setting $\phi_n'(u)=0$ yields

$$
u^2 = -\frac{4hX}{n},
$$

so a real stationary point exists only for $n<0$.  Write $n=-m$ ($m>0$).  Then

$$
u_0 = 2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}},\qquad
\phi_{-m}(u_0) = 2\sqrt{hX m}.
$$

The second derivative at $u_0$ is

$$
\phi_{-m}''(u_0) = \frac{2hX}{u_0^3} = \frac{m^{3/2}}{4\sqrt{hX}}.
$$

Thus the stationary‑phase amplitude is

$$
|I_{-m}|\asymp \frac{|w_D(u_0)|}{\sqrt{|\phi_{-m}''(u_0)|}}
       \asymp D^{3/2}(hX)^{-1/2},
$$

when $m\asymp hX/D^2$ (interior stationary range).  The dual length $m\asymp hX/D^2$ is indeed the active scale.  At the endpoint $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, $h\asymp X^{1/4}$, the dual length is $m\asymp X^{1/4}$, comparable to the original $h$‑range.

**Hessian degeneracy:**  If one treats $(h,m)$ simultaneously as continuous variables, the effective phase is $\Phi(h,m)=\sqrt{hX m}$.  Straightforward computation gives

$$
\frac{\partial^2\Phi}{\partial h^2}= -\frac14 X^{1/2}m^{1/2}h^{-3/2},\quad
\frac{\partial^2\Phi}{\partial m^2}= -\frac14 X^{1/2}h^{1/2}m^{-3/2},\quad
\frac{\partial^2\Phi}{\partial h\partial m}= \frac14 X^{1/2}h^{-1/2}m^{-1/2},
$$

and $\det\nabla^2\Phi = \frac{X}{16hm} - \frac{X}{16hm}=0$.  Hence generic two‑dimensional stationary phase or decoupling cannot be applied to the H13 dual phase.  This guardrail remains valid.

**Regimes requiring uniform control:**

* $m\asymp 1$ (stationary phase breaks down; direct bound suffices after $1/H_D$ normalization, as shown in B‑Boundary).
* $m$ outside the stationary window: rapid decay by integration by parts (non‑stationary).
* Stationary point $u_0$ near the boundary of the support of $w_D$; transition asymptotics needed.

A complete uniform stationary‑phase lemma (C2‑SPU / H13‑SPU) is still required.

### 9. Q1‑Spectral toy test

To illustrate the operator‑norm blindness, construct a tiny matrix for a few odd denominators.  Let $D=4$, take odd $d\in\{5,7\}$ (say).  Define

$$
K_{d_1,d_2} = \sum_{h=-1}^{1} |\alpha_{h,H}|^2 e\!\Bigl(hX\Bigl(\frac1{d_1}-\frac1{d_2}\Bigr)\Bigr),
$$

with $X=16$, $H=2$, and $\alpha_{h,H}$ computed as above.  Set $U=\operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(d))$, where $\chi_4(5)=1$, $\chi_4(7)=-1$.  One can numerically (or symbolically) verify that $U$ is unitary and that

$$
\|U^*KU\|_{\rm op} = \|K\|_{\rm op}.
$$

The same holds for any conjugate‑invariant norm.  Hence any argument that places $\chi_4(d)$ into a diagonal unitary conjugation and then bounds by operator norm cannot exploit the spatial character.  This does **not** rule out signed bilinear estimates that are not norm‑based.

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps

* **Fejer spike handling:** The pointwise bound $K_H(t)\ll\min(H,1/(H\|t\|^2))$ is an upper bound, not an identity.  At integer arguments, $K_H(0)=H+1$, so $\frac1H K_H(0) = 1+1/H \le 2$.  The R5 proof must cap the min‑expression at a constant for $\|t\|=0$ (as would naturally follow from $\min(1, \infty$? Actually the naive formula $\min(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2})$ would be infinite at $\|t\|=0$.  One must treat $\|t\|=0$ separately, bounding $\frac1H K_H(t)\le 2$ and counting exact integers via divisor function.  The proof should be made explicit.
* **Tie‑breaking for nearest integer:** When $X/d$ is exactly halfway between two integers, the bound $\|X/d\|=1/2$ still works, and the product‑count remains valid with the chosen nearest integer.  The number of such midpoints is at most $\tau(X)$ and is harmless.
* **Short block removal:** Blocks with $D<X^{1/4}$ are handled trivially before Vaaler; the transition around $D\approx X^{1/4}$ must be checked for smoothness in the dyadic partition.
* **H13 Poisson constant:** The factor $i/2$ depends on the Fourier transform convention; a sign change is possible.  The stationary‑phase scaling and degeneracy are convention‑independent.  Any numerical H13 implementation must use the precise convention.
* **Uniformity of shifted‑$F$:** The derivative bounds for $F_{\rho,D}$ are independent of $D,X$, but the theorem’s constants $C_i$ are typically required to be absolute.  If the shift $\rho D/(4X)$ is $O(1)$ (it is $\le 1/2$), then the bounds remain uniform.  A line‑by‑line check of the theorem’s proof might require slight modification of the constants, but should hold.
* **Cyclic trace blindness:** The H12 statement applies only to pure conjugacy‑invariant cyclic traces; non‑conjugacy signed statistics may still detect the character.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

* **Fejer spikes at exact divisors:** For $X$ a square, $d\mid X$ gives $X/d$ integer, so $K_H(X/d)=H+1$.  The R5 bound must treat these terms with a capped value; grouped by $d$, there are at most $\tau(X)$ such terms, contributing $O_\epsilon(X^\epsilon)$ additional.  The overall bound $X^{1/4+\epsilon}$ is not violated.
* **Second‑leg spike when $X/d+\rho$ is a multiple of 4:** Condition $X\approx d(4m-\rho)$ can be satisfied for many $d$ if $X$ has many small prime factors.  The divisor bound $\tau(n)\ll n^\epsilon$ already absorbs this uniformly; no counterexample is found.
* **H13 Hessian degeneracy obstruction:** Because the dual phase $\sqrt{Xhm}$ has zero Hessian, any argument that attempts a generic two‑dimensional stationary phase or decoupling after H13 is automatically invalid.  Discrete spacing methods (which work on rational points rather than continuous Taylor expansions) are not affected by this continuous degeneracy, but they require a different theorem (e.g. Bombieri–Iwaniec).
* **Character blindness via Q1‑Spectral:** If a proposed signed‑spacing estimate eventually bounds a quantity that is invariant under $U^*K U$, the character disappears.  A candidate “signed” argument must avoid passing through such an invariant.  The toy matrix test can be applied to any proposed kernel to check this.

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

**Alternative 1: Mellin–Perron with intact Vaaler coefficients.**  
Instead of expanding $\psi$ via Vaaler, one could insert the floor‑compatible sawtooth identity directly into a Perron integral for $\sum r_2(n)$.  The Vaaler coefficients would then appear as Dirichlet series coefficients, leading to a four‑fold contour that seems highly resistant to simplification.  No advantage is visible.

**Alternative 2: Avoiding Vaaler entirely via signed Fourier truncation.**  
Truncating the formal Fourier series $\psi(t)=-\frac{1}{\pi}\sum_{h\ne0}\frac{e(ht)}{h}$ to height $H_D$ would produce a high‑frequency signed tail.  This tail formally preserves $\chi_4$, but bounding it absolutely near discontinuities appears to recreate an H5r‑type obstacle.  This remains a comparison route, not a replacement.

**Alternative 3: B‑process‑first combined with two‑coset spacing.**  
The H13 transform preserves $\chi_4$ at the dual level.  If a discrete spacing argument could be formulated that does **not** reduce to a diagonal‑unitary conjugation (Q1‑Spectral), it might exploit the dual character.  At present, no such argument exists; the Hessian degeneracy still blocks continuous‑phase tools, but discrete first‑spacing might be attempted.  This is a genuine exploratory direction.

**Falsification test for signed‑spacing proposals:**  
Take a small kernel derived from M9a, apply the proposed signed estimate, and compare with the unsigned/absolute majorant.  If the signed version gives no improvement over the absolute bound, the proposal is unlikely to survive at larger scale.
