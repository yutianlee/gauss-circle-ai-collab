## Claims that look correct

**1. Coefficient Conjugacy [PROVED]**
A3's claim that $\alpha_{-h,H} = \overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$ is algebraically correct. We verify this by expanding the Vaaler coefficient function:
$$
\alpha_{h,H} = -\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}, \qquad \Phi(u) = \pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u) + u.
$$
For $h > 0$, $\alpha_{h,H} = \frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi(h/(H+1))$. Since $\Phi(u)$ is strictly real-valued for $u \in (0,1)$, $\alpha_{h,H}$ is purely imaginary. For $-h$, we have:
$$
\alpha_{-h,H} = -\frac{\Phi(|-h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i (-h)} = \frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{-2\pi i h} = \frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi(h/(H+1)) = -\alpha_{h,H}.
$$
Since $\alpha_{h,H}$ is purely imaginary, $-\alpha_{h,H} = \overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$. This confirms the conjugacy relation and invalidates any prior numerical implementations using $\alpha_{-h,H} = -\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$.

**2. M9b Shift Cancellation [PROVED]**
A3's claim that standard Cauchy--Schwarz over $h$ for $\mathcal M_2$ reduces the $\rho/4$ phase information to odd-$h$ support is correct under the displayed normalization. The raw sum contains the factor $C_h = e(h/4) - e(3h/4)$. The magnitude squared is:
$$
|C_h|^2 = (e(h/4) - e(3h/4))(e(-h/4) - e(-3h/4)) = 2 - 2\cos(\pi h/2).
$$
For $h \equiv 1 \bmod 2$, $\cos(\pi h/2) = 0$, yielding $|C_h|^2 = 2$. Wait, let us re-evaluate this carefully.
If $h=1$, $e(1/4) = i$, $e(3/4) = -i$. $i - (-i) = 2i$. $|2i|^2 = 4$.
If $h=2$, $e(2/4) = -1$, $e(6/4) = -1$. $-1 - (-1) = 0$. $|0|^2 = 0$.
If $h=3$, $e(3/4) = -i$, $e(9/4) = i$. $-i - i = -2i$. $|-2i|^2 = 4$.
If $h=4$, $e(4/4) = 1$, $e(12/4) = 1$. $1 - 1 = 0$. $|0|^2 = 0$.
The magnitude squared is exactly $4$ for odd $h$ and $0$ for even $h$. In this Cauchy--Schwarz route, the sign of $\chi_4(h)$ is absorbed into the absolute magnitude, leaving an odd-$h$ restricted, character-blind reciprocal kernel in the spatial variables $d_1, d_2$.

**3. R5 Dyadic Tail Summation [DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS]**
A1's claim that the dyadic tail of the product-count sum is bounded by $O(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$ is structurally correct. We verify the summation:
$$
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n) = \sum_{|X-n| \le \Delta} \tau(n)(1) + \sum_{j \ge 1} \sum_{2^{j-1}\Delta < |X-n| \le 2^j\Delta} \tau(n) \frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}.
$$
The central term contains $O(\Delta)$ integers. Bounding $\tau(n) \ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon$, the central sum is $O(\Delta X^\epsilon)$.
The $j$-th annulus contains $O(2^{j-1}\Delta)$ integers. The weight is bounded by $\Delta^2 / (2^{j-1}\Delta)^2 = 4 \cdot 2^{-2j}$. The sum over the $j$-th annulus is bounded by $(2^{j-1}\Delta) \cdot (4 \cdot 2^{-2j}) \cdot X^\epsilon = 2 \cdot 2^{-j} \Delta X^\epsilon$.
Summing over $j \ge 1$ yields a convergent geometric series $\sum 2^{-j} = 1$. The total sum is $O(\Delta X^\epsilon)$. Since $\Delta \asymp X^{1/4}$, the bound $O(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$ holds.

## Claims that need proof

**1. H13 Leading-Term Invariance vs. Error Terms**
A3 claims that the H13 post-transform kernel places the dual character $\chi_4(m)$ into a diagonal unitary conjugation, triggering `Q1-Spectral` blindness. This claim requires proof regarding the error terms of the stationary phase approximation. The stationary phase integral is:
$$
I(-m) = \int w_D(u) e(hX/u + mu) du.
$$
The leading term is $A(m,h) e(2\sqrt{hXm})$. However, the first-order error term in the asymptotic expansion is bounded by $O(1 / (\phi''(u_0) u_0^2))$. We compute this:
$$
\phi''(u_0) = \frac{2hX}{u_0^3} = \frac{2hX}{(hX/m)^{3/2}} = \frac{2m^{3/2}}{(hX)^{1/2}}.
$$
$$
\phi''(u_0) u_0^2 = \left( \frac{2m^{3/2}}{(hX)^{1/2}} \right) \left( \frac{hX}{m} \right) = 2m^{1/2} (hX)^{1/2}.
$$
At the endpoint $h \asymp X^{1/4}, m \asymp X^{1/4}$, this error is $O(1 / (X^{1/8} X^{5/8})) = O(X^{-3/4})$. While this error is asymptotically small, it is not strictly of the form $A(m,h) e(2\sqrt{hXm})$. A3 needs to prove that these non-leading terms do not harbor residual sign information that escapes the diagonal unitary conjugation.

**2. M9b Shifted-$F$ Uniformity**
A1 claims that the shifted phase $F_{\rho,D}(z) = \frac{1}{4z} + \frac{\rho D}{4X}$ is a valid theorem-comparison formulation. This requires proof that the intended reciprocal-sum theorem permits a parameter-dependent additive shift. Standard theorems (e.g., Bombieri--Iwaniec) require the phase function $F(z)$ to have derivatives bounded uniformly away from zero and infinity on the support interval. While $F_{\rho,D}^{(k)}(z) = (1/4z)^{(k)}$ is independent of the shift, the function itself $F_{\rho,D}(z)$ depends on $D$ and $X$. If the theorem's error terms depend on $\|F\|_\infty$, the shift $\frac{\rho D}{4X}$ (which is $O(X^{-1/2})$ at the endpoint) should be rigorously tracked through the spacing matrix bounds.
