## Detailed reasoning: Frozen Cauchy-Schwarz Normalization for M9a

The active analytic bottleneck is the fixed-coefficient reciprocal main sum $\mathcal M_1(D;X)$, defined as:
$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X) = -4 \sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} \alpha_{h, H_D} \sum_{d \asymp D} \chi_4(d) w_D(d) e(hX/d)
$$
where the finite Vaaler coefficients are $\alpha_{h, H_D} = -\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H_D+1))}{2\pi i h}$, and the local Vaaler height is $H_D \asymp D X^{-1/4}$ for dyadic blocks $X^{1/4} \le D \le X^{1/2}$. The function $\Phi(u)$ is defined as $\Phi(u) = \pi u (1-u) \cot(\pi u) + u$ for $0 < u < 1$. The weight function $w_D(d)$ is a smooth dyadic partition of unity supported on $d \asymp D$, satisfying $0 \le w_D(d) \le 1$.

To bound the absolute value $|\mathcal M_1(D;X)|$, we must apply the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality. We freeze exactly one Cauchy-Schwarz normalization: we apply the inequality to the outer $h$-sum, using the absolute value of the Vaaler coefficients, $|\alpha_{h, H_D}|$, as the weight function. This choice is mathematically natural because the sum of the absolute values of the coefficients satisfies $\sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} |\alpha_{h, H_D}| \ll \log H_D \ll \log X$, which minimizes the logarithmic loss in the first Cauchy-Schwarz factor.

Applying the $|\alpha_{h, H_D}|$-weighted Cauchy-Schwarz inequality yields:
$$
|\mathcal M_1(D;X)|^2 \le 16 \left( \sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} |\alpha_{h, H_D}| \right) \sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} |\alpha_{h, H_D}| \left| \sum_{d \asymp D} \chi_4(d) w_D(d) e(hX/d) \right|^2
$$

We define the finite index set of odd denominators in the dyadic block as $\mathcal D_{\rm odd} = \{d : D \le d < 2D, 2 \nmid d\}$. Since the Dirichlet character $\chi_4(d) = 0$ for even $d$, the inner sum is strictly restricted to the set $\mathcal D_{\rm odd}$.

Expanding the squared absolute value of the inner sum, we obtain:
$$
\left| \sum_{d \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd}} \chi_4(d) w_D(d) e(hX/d) \right|^2 = \sum_{d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd}} \chi_4(d_1) \chi_4(d_2) w_D(d_1) w_D(d_2) e\left( hX \left( \frac{1}{d_1} - \frac{1}{d_2} \right) \right)
$$
where we used the fact that $\chi_4(d)$ is real-valued, so its complex conjugate satisfies $\overline{\chi_4(d_2)} = \chi_4(d_2)$.

Interchanging the order of summation between the frequency variable $h$ and the spatial variables $(d_1, d_2)$, the second factor in the Cauchy-Schwarz bound becomes a signed quadratic form:
$$
\sum_{d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd}} \chi_4(d_1) \chi_4(d_2) K_{d_1, d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}
$$
where the exact frozen Cauchy-Schwarz kernel is defined as:
$$
K_{d_1, d_2}^{(|\alpha|)} = w_D(d_1) w_D(d_2) \sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} |\alpha_{h, H_D}| e\left( hX \left( \frac{1}{d_1} - \frac{1}{d_2} \right) \right)
$$

This exact kernel $K^{(|\alpha|)}$ is the canonical object for all subsequent Q1-Spectral, H12, and BSOS diagnostics. Alternative kernels (e.g., unweighted kernels or $|\alpha_h|^2$-weighted kernels) are explicitly rejected to maintain a single, falsifiable normalization.

## Detailed reasoning: Exact BSOS Target and Implication

[PROVED] We now derive the exact target bound for the finite signed off-diagonal statistic (BSOS) and prove its conditional implication to the M9a main-term estimate.

We separate the signed quadratic form into diagonal ($d_1 = d_2$) and off-diagonal ($d_1 \ne d_2$) contributions:
$$
\sum_{d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd}} \chi_4(d_1) \chi_4(d_2) K_{d_1, d_2}^{(|\alpha|)} = \mathcal S_{\rm diag} + \mathcal S_{\rm signed}
$$

The diagonal contribution is given by:
$$
\mathcal S_{\rm diag} = \sum_{d \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd}} \chi_4(d)^2 K_{d, d}^{(|\alpha|)}
$$
Since $\chi_4(d)^2 = 1$ for all $d \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd}$, and the exponential factor evaluates to $e(0) = 1$, the diagonal term simplifies to:
$$
\mathcal S_{\rm diag} = \sum_{d \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd}} w_D(d)^2 \sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} |\alpha_{h, H_D}|
$$
Using the bounds $|w_D(d)| \le 1$, the cardinality $|\mathcal D_{\rm odd}| \asymp D$, and the coefficient sum bound $\sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} |\alpha_{h, H_D}| \ll \log X$, we obtain the rigorous upper bound:
$$
\mathcal S_{\rm diag} \ll D \log X \ll X^{1/2} \log X
$$
This diagonal contribution is perfectly consistent with the required endpoint-strength estimate, as it contributes $X^{1/4} (\log X)^{1/2}$ to the final bound on $\mathcal M_1(D;X)$.

The off-diagonal contribution is the exact BSOS statistic:
$$
\mathcal S_{\rm signed} = \sum_{\substack{d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd} \\ d_1 \ne d_2}} \chi_4(d_1) \chi_4(d_2) K_{d_1, d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}
$$

We define the corresponding unsigned and absolute-majorant comparators for falsification testing:
$$
\mathcal S_{\rm unsigned} = \sum_{\substack{d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd} \\ d_1 \ne d_2}} K_{d_1, d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}
$$
$$
\mathcal S_{\rm abs} = \sum_{\substack{d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd} \\ d_1 \ne d_2}} \left| K_{d_1, d_2}^{(|\alpha|)} \right|
$$
We also define the operator-norm comparator:
$$
\mathcal S_{\rm op} = |\mathcal D_{\rm odd}| \|K_{\rm off}^{(|\alpha|)}\|_{\rm op}
$$
where $K_{\rm off}^{(|\alpha|)}$ is the off-diagonal part of the kernel matrix, obtained by setting the diagonal entries to zero.

**Conditional Implication Proof:**
Assume the target bound $|\mathcal S_{\rm signed}| \ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}$ holds.
Then the total signed quadratic form is bounded by the sum of the diagonal and off-diagonal contributions:
$$
\mathcal S_{\rm diag} + \mathcal S_{\rm signed} \ll X^{1/2} \log X + X^{1/2+\epsilon} \ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}
$$
Substituting this bound back into the frozen Cauchy-Schwarz inequality:
$$
|\mathcal M_1(D;X)|^2 \le 16 \left( \sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} |\alpha_{h, H_D}| \right) (\mathcal S_{\rm diag} + \mathcal S_{\rm signed})
$$
$$
|\mathcal M_1(D;X)|^2 \ll_\epsilon (\log X) X^{1/2+\epsilon} \ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}
$$
Taking the square root yields the final bound:
$$
|\mathcal M_1(D;X)| \ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}
$$
This rigorously proves that the BSOS target bound $|\mathcal S_{\rm signed}| \ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}$ is sufficient to imply the M9a endpoint estimate. BSOS is therefore a valid, executable falsification statistic.

## Detailed reasoning: Q1-Spectral for the Exact Kernel

[PROVED] We now formalize the Q1-Spectral diagnostic for the exact frozen kernel $K^{(|\alpha|)}$.

Define the finite-dimensional Hilbert space $\mathcal V_D = \ell^2(\mathcal D_{\rm odd})$ with the standard inner product $\langle x, y \rangle = \sum_{d \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd}} \overline{x_d} y_d$. The unsigned weight vector $w \in \mathcal V_D$ has components $w_d = w_D(d)$. The signed test vector $v \in \mathcal V_D$ has components $v_d = \chi_4(d) w_D(d)$.

Define the diagonal character operator $U$ on $\mathcal V_D$ by:
$$
U = \operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(d))_{d \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd}}
$$
Since $\chi_4(d) \in \{\pm 1\}$ for all $d \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd}$, the operator $U$ is a unitary involution:
$$
U^* U = U U^* = I
$$

The signed quadratic form can be written as the inner product:
$$
\langle v, K^{(|\alpha|)} v \rangle = \sum_{d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal D_{\rm odd}} \overline{v_{d_1}} K_{d_1, d_2}^{(|\alpha|)} v_{d_2}
$$
Since $v = U w$ and $U$ is self-adjoint ($U^* = U$), we have:
$$
\langle U w, K^{(|\alpha|)} U w \rangle = \langle w, U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U w \rangle
$$
This demonstrates that the spatial character $\chi_4(d)$ enters the quadratic form exclusively through the diagonal unitary conjugation $K^{(|\alpha|)} \mapsto U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U$.

If an analytic method attempts to bound this quadratic form by applying the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality or Rayleigh quotient bound to the conjugated matrix, it will require estimating the operator norm $\|U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U\|_{\rm op}$.
However, the operator norm is unitarily invariant:
$$
\|U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U\|_{\rm op} = \sup_{x \ne 0} \frac{\|U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U x\|}{\|x\|} = \sup_{y = Ux \ne 0} \frac{\|U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} y\|}{\|U^* y\|} = \sup_{y \ne 0} \frac{\|K^{(|\alpha|)} y\|}{\|y\|} = \|K^{(|\alpha|)}\|_{\rm op}
$$
where we used the fact that $U$ is an isometry ($\|Ux\| = \|x\|$).

**Conclusion:** Any method that bounds the signed quadratic form solely through the operator norm of the conjugated matrix is strictly character-blind. The bound obtained will be identical to the bound for the unsigned quadratic form $\langle w, K^{(|\alpha|)} w \rangle$.

**Blocked Methods:**
- Operator norm bounds ($\|U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U\|_{\rm op}$).
- Spectral radius bounds ($\rho(U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U)$).
- Frobenius norm bounds ($\|U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U\|_F = \|K^{(|\alpha|)}\|_F$).
- Schur/Gershgorin bounds applied after taking absolute values of the matrix entries.
- Absolute-value matrix majorization ($|U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U| \le |K^{(|\alpha|)}|$).

**Unblocked Methods:**
- Direct evaluation of the signed bilinear form $\langle w, U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U w \rangle$ without passing to a matrix norm.
- Residue interference estimates that exploit the specific sign pattern of $U$.
- Open-path moments (e.g., $\langle w, (U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U)^m w \rangle$).
- Cross-residue statistics (e.g., CRI).

## Detailed reasoning: H12 Trace Invariance

[PROVED] We formalize the H12 trace invariance diagnostic narrowly for pure cyclic traces.

A common technique in spacing problems is to bound the operator norm by computing the trace of a high power of the matrix:
$$
\|A\|_{\rm op} \le \left( \operatorname{Tr}((A^* A)^m) \right)^{1/(2m)}
$$
For the conjugated matrix $A = U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U$, the cyclic trace is:
$$
\operatorname{Tr}((U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U)^m)
$$
Using the cyclic property of the trace ($\operatorname{Tr}(AB) = \operatorname{Tr}(BA)$) and the unitary involution property ($U U^* = I$), we have:
$$
\operatorname{Tr}((U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U)^m) = \operatorname{Tr}(U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U \cdots U^* K^{(|\alpha|)} U)
$$
$$
= \operatorname{Tr}(U^* (K^{(|\alpha|)})^m U) = \operatorname{Tr}(U U^* (K^{(|\alpha|)})^m) = \operatorname{Tr}((K^{(|\alpha|)})^m)
$$

**Conclusion:** Pure conjugacy-invariant cyclic traces are strictly character-blind. Computing the trace of any power of the conjugated matrix yields exactly the same result as computing the trace of the unconjugated matrix. This diagnostic explicitly does *not* block open-path moments or non-conjugacy signed forms, where the start and end indices of the path are distinct and the $U$ operators do not cyclically cancel.

## Detailed reasoning: H13-Dual Conditional Test

$$
\sum_d \chi_4(d) w_D(d) e(hX/d) = \frac{i}{2} \sum_n \chi_4(n) \int_{\mathbb R} w_D(u) e(hX/u - nu/4) du
$$
Let the phase function be $\phi_n(u) = hX/u - nu/4$. The stationary point occurs when $\phi_n'(u) = -hX/u^2 - n/4 = 0$. Since $h, X, u > 0$, a stationary point exists only for $n < 0$.
We set $n = -m$ with $m > 0$. The stationary point is:
$$
u_0 = 2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}}
$$
The active dual length is determined by the support of $w_D(u)$, which is $u \asymp D$. Thus:
$$
D \asymp 2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}} \implies m \asymp \frac{hX}{D^2}
$$
The leading phase at the stationary point is:
$$
\phi_{-m}(u_0) = \frac{hX}{2\sqrt{hX/m}} + \frac{m}{4} 2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}} = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{hXm} + \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{hXm} = \sqrt{hXm}
$$
The second derivative is $\phi_{-m}''(u_0) = 2hX/u_0^3 = \frac{m^{3/2}}{4\sqrt{hX}} = \frac{m}{2u_0}$.
The stationary phase integral $I(-m)$ has amplitude $|I(-m)| \asymp u_0 / \sqrt{m u_0} \asymp \sqrt{u_0/m} \asymp D^{3/2} (hX)^{-1/2}$.

## Detailed reasoning: C3 Parity Diagnostics

[PROVED] We formalize the C3 parity collapse diagnostics for specific spacing transformations.

Define the parity function $\sigma(\xi) = \frac{1}{2} (-1)^{2\xi}$ for $\xi \in \frac{1}{2} \mathbb Z$.

**Translation Shift Collapse:**
Consider a translation shift $q = \xi_1 - \xi_2 \in \mathbb Z$. The product of the parity functions is:
$$
\sigma(\xi_1) \sigma(\xi_2) = \frac{1}{4} (-1)^{2\xi_1 + 2\xi_2} = \frac{1}{4} (-1)^{2(\xi_1 - \xi_2) + 4\xi_2} = \frac{1}{4} (-1)^{2q} (-1)^{4\xi_2}
$$
Since $2\xi_2 \in \mathbb Z$, $4\xi_2$ is an even integer, so $(-1)^{4\xi_2} = 1$. Thus:
$$
\sigma(\xi_1) \sigma(\xi_2) = \frac{1}{4} (-1)^{2q}
$$
The parity sign factors out completely and is independent of the internal location variable $\xi_2$. This blocks coefficient-level parity gains for direct translation A-process models.

**Odd Dilation Collapse:**
Consider an odd dilation $\xi_2 = a \xi_1$, where $a$ is an odd integer. The product is:
$$
\sigma(\xi_1) \sigma(a \xi_1) = \frac{1}{4} (-1)^{2\xi_1 + 2a\xi_1} = \frac{1}{4} (-1)^{2\xi_1(1+a)}
$$
Since $a$ is odd, $1+a = 2k$ for some integer $k$. Thus $2\xi_1(1+a) = 4k\xi_1$. Since $2\xi_1 \in \mathbb Z$, $4k\xi_1$ is an even integer, so $(-1)^{4k\xi_1} = 1$. Thus:
$$
\sigma(\xi_1) \sigma(a \xi_1) = \frac{1}{4}
$$
The parity oscillation is completely erased under odd dilations.

These are diagnostic models for parity collapse under specific spacing transformations. They do not rule out non-translation, rational, or spectral spacing methods.
