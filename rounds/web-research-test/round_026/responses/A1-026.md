## Detailed reasoning

### 1. Proof-draft insertion: H4-R25

Use the convention

$$
e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}.
$$

Vaaler's source anchors to record in `best_proof_draft.md` are:

- Vaaler Theorem 6, printed p. 192, equation (2.28), for the Fourier transform formula for $J$ and hence the coefficient function $\widehat J$.
- Vaaler Section 7, printed p. 207, equations (7.1)--(7.3), for the periodic trigonometric polynomials $i_N,j_N,k_N$.
- Vaaler Theorem 18, printed p. 210, equations (7.13)--(7.17), especially equation (7.14), for the residual inequality.

The local PDF text extraction confirms the relevant structure. Theorem 6 states the Fourier transform formula

$$
\widehat J(t)=\pi t(1-|t|)\cot(\pi t)+|t|,
\qquad 0<|t|<1,
$$

and $\widehat J(t)=0$ for $1\le |t|$. Section 7 defines the periodic polynomials $i_N,j_N,k_N$, with $k_N$ the periodic Fejer kernel. Theorem 18 gives the finite periodic sawtooth approximation and the residual majorant

$$
|\psi*j_N(x)-\psi(x)|
\le
(2N+2)^{-1}k_N(x).
$$

Translated into repo notation, with $H=N$, this gives the theorem dependency:

$$
\psi_F(t)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}
\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)
+
R_H^F(t),
$$

where

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
$$

and

$$
|R_H^F(t)|
\le
\frac1{2H+2}K_H(t).
$$

Here

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u,
\qquad 0<u<1,
$$

and

$$
K_H(t)
=
\sum_{|k|\le H}
\left(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\right)e(kt)
=
\frac1{H+1}
\left(
\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}
\right)^2.
$$

The endpoint conversion should be stated explicitly. Vaaler's periodic sawtooth is centered at discontinuities, while the arithmetic sawtooth is floor-compatible:

$$
\psi_F(n)=-\frac12.
$$

At integer arguments, the Vaaler trigonometric polynomial has value $0$, and

$$
K_H(0)=H+1,
\qquad
\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12.
$$

Thus the residual majorant covers exactly the half-jump discrepancy between the centered value and the floor-compatible value. This is an endpoint convention, not an analytic estimate.

### 2. Proof-draft insertion: R5-Full-R25

The residual statement needed after applying H4 blockwise is:

For every active dyadic block $D$ with

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2},
\qquad
H=H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4},
$$

and for smooth bounded dyadic weights $w_D$, one has

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon},
$$

and, for $\rho\in\{1,3\}$,

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proves that the fixed Fejer residual contribution is acceptable after summing over dyadic $D$ and over both sawtooth legs.

The proof uses the standard Fejer bound

$$
K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(H,\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\right),
$$

hence

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

Set

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

Define the exact-resonance-safe cap

$$
W_\Delta(u)
=
\begin{cases}
1,&u=0,\\
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{u^2}\right),&u\ne0.
\end{cases}
$$

For the first residual leg, choose an integer $m=m(d)$ nearest to $X/d$. If there is a tie, choose the smaller of the two nearest integers. The tie convention affects no estimate; it only makes the map single-valued. For $d\asymp D$,

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
=
\frac{|X-md|}{d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-md|}{D}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)
\ll
W_\Delta(X-md).
$$

Grouping by

$$
n=md
$$

gives at most $\tau(n)$ representations. In the active range, $m\asymp X/D$, so $n=md\asymp X$. Hence

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n).
$$

Using the pointwise divisor bound

$$
\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon
$$

and the elementary estimate

$$
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}W_\Delta(X-n)\ll \Delta+1,
$$

one gets

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon(\Delta+1)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

The exact resonance case $X=md$ is harmless: then $W_\Delta(0)=1$ and

$$
\frac1H K_H(0)=\frac{H+1}{H}\ll 1.
$$

For shifted residual legs, choose $m=m_\rho(d)$ nearest to

$$
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}.
$$

Near-integrality of $(X/d+\rho)/4$ is equivalent to

$$
X\approx d(4m-\rho).
$$

Put

$$
\ell=4m-\rho.
$$

Then

$$
\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4.
$$

For active large $X$, $\ell>0$ because $d\le X^{1/2}$ gives $X/d\ge X^{1/2}$, so $(X/d+\rho)/4$ is positive and large. For small $X$, all contributions are absorbed into the implicit constant. Group by

$$
n=d\ell.
$$

The congruence restriction on $\ell$ only reduces divisor multiplicity, so the same estimate gives

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Short blocks $D<X^{1/4}$ are not sent through Vaaler. Since $|\psi_F|\le 1/2$,

$$
\sum_{d<X^{1/4}}|\psi_F(\cdots)|\ll X^{1/4}
$$

for each sawtooth leg, up to a bounded number of dyadic overlaps. Logarithmic losses from dyadic decomposition are absorbed into $X^\epsilon$.

### 3. Conditional bridge theorem

The proof draft should state the bridge exactly as:

**Bridge-R25.** Assume H1--H3, H4-R25, R5-Full-R25, and M9-R25. Then

$$
P(X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Proof sketch:

H1--H3 give the floor-compatible identity

$$
P(X)=W(X)+O(1),
$$

where $W(X)$ is the sum of two balanced sawtooth legs. Split both legs dyadically. Blocks with $D<X^{1/4}$ are bounded trivially. On each active block $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$, apply H4 with $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$. H4 decomposes each block into a finite Vaaler main term plus a Fejer residual. R5-Full controls all residuals by $O_\epsilon(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$ after dyadic summation. M9 controls all main terms by $O_\epsilon(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$ after dyadic summation. Thus

$$
P(X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This is not a proof of the conjectural bound because M9 remains open.

For $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$ and $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$,

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)

-4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}

where

### 5. Paired implementation formulas for real weights

For real dyadic weights $w_D$, the following are proved implementation identities.

First,

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

Since

$$
A_{-h}(D;X)=\overline{A_h(D;X)}
$$

for real $w_D$, one gets

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
\frac4\pi
\sum_{h=1}^{H_D}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\operatorname{Im}A_h(D;X).
$$

For the second leg, for odd $h$,

$$
C_h=2i\chi_4(h),
$$

and for even $h$, $C_h=0$. Also

$$
B_{-h}(D;X)=\overline{B_h(D;X)}.
$$

Thus

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
-\frac8\pi
\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\chi_4(h)\operatorname{Re}B_h(D;X).
$$

These identities are for implementation and regression only. If complex dyadic weights are introduced, use the raw two-sided definitions.

### 6. Algebraic coefficient lemmas

For $0<u<1$,

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u.
$$

A direct calculation gives

$$
\Phi(1-u)
=
\pi(1-u)u\cot(\pi-\pi u)+(1-u)
=
-\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+1-u.
$$

Therefore

$$
\Phi(u)+\Phi(1-u)=1,
$$

so

$$
\Phi(1-u)=\Phi(u)
$$

is false except at $u=1/2$.

For the coefficients,

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}.
$$

Since $\Phi$ is real-valued on $(0,1)$,

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i(-h)}
=
\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}
=
\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

### 7. M2 Cauchy diagnostics and fourth moment

The bounded-scope $\mathcal M_2$ Cauchy diagnostic remains:

Weighted $|\alpha_h|$ Cauchy over $h$ gives

$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2
\le
16L
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\,|C_h|^2\,|B_h(D;X)|^2,
$$

where

$$
L=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\asymp \log H_D.
$$

After expanding in $d_1,d_2$, the kernel is

$$
K_{2;d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}
=
w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2)
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\,|C_h|^2
e\left(\frac{hX}{4}\left(\frac1{d_1}-\frac1{d_2}\right)\right).
$$

But

$$
|C_h|^2=
\begin{cases}
4,&2\nmid h,\\
0,&2\mid h.
\end{cases}
$$

Thus this normalization loses the $\chi_4(h)$ sign and keeps only odd-frequency support. Its diagonal is acceptable, but it is character-blind with respect to the frequency character. This is a diagnostic for that specific normalization, not a global obstruction.

For the fourth-moment object, define

$$
S_2(D;X)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\beta_h
B_h(D;X),
\qquad
\beta_h=\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h.
$$

Then

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)=4S_2(D;X).
$$

A sufficient target is

$$
|S_2(D;X)|^4\ll_\epsilon X^{1+\epsilon}.
$$

The exact expansion is

$$
|S_2|^4
=
\sum_{\mathbf h}
\sum_{\mathbf d}
\beta_{h_1}\overline{\beta_{h_2}}
\beta_{h_3}\overline{\beta_{h_4}}
\prod_{j=1}^4 w_D(d_j)
e\left(
\frac{X}{4}
\left(
\frac{h_1}{d_1}
-\frac{h_2}{d_2}
+\frac{h_3}{d_3}
-\frac{h_4}{d_4}
\right)
\right).
$$

Clearing denominators gives the resonance integer

$$
N
=
h_1d_2d_3d_4
-
h_2d_1d_3d_4
+
h_3d_1d_2d_4
-
h_4d_1d_2d_3.
$$

The exact diagonal and pair-swapped diagonal families have total weighted mass $\asymp D^2\le X$, compatible with the fourth-moment target. This does not control all exact resonance configurations $N=0$ and does not control near-collision bands.

### 8. CRI identity and diagnostic status

Under a fixed two-sided convention, define

$$
\Sigma_r(D;X)
=
\sum_{\substack{1\le |h|\le H_D\\h\equiv r\pmod4}}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\sum_{d\asymp D}
w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)),
\qquad r\in\{1,3\}.
$$

Then, because $C_h=2i\chi_4(h)$ on odd $h$,

$$
S_2(D;X)=2i(\Sigma_1-\Sigma_3).
$$

Therefore

$$
|S_2(D;X)|^2=4|\Sigma_1-\Sigma_3|^2.
$$

Define the falsification ratio

$$
R_{\rm CRI}
=
\frac{|\Sigma_1-\Sigma_3|^2}
{|\Sigma_1|^2+|\Sigma_3|^2}.
$$

Small values of $R_{\rm CRI}$ indicate useful cross-residue cancellation in finite tests. Values near $1$ are neutral. Values near $2$ are adverse. This ratio is not a theorem-level sufficient condition for M9.

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps

1. **H4 convention mismatch.** Vaaler's centered sawtooth and the floor-compatible arithmetic sawtooth differ at integers. The Fejer residual covers the half-jump, but this should be written explicitly wherever H4 is invoked.

2. **R5 nearest-integer single-valuedness.** The nearest-integer map needs a tie rule. The proof does not depend on which tie rule is chosen, but the proof draft should specify one.

3. **R5 shifted positivity.** The shifted proof uses $\ell=4m-\rho>0$. This holds in the active large-$X$ range. Small $X$ should be separated.

4. **R5 support range.** The grouping $n=md\asymp X$ assumes $d\asymp D$ and $m\asymp X/D$. This is true in the active range, but the proof draft should state constants from dyadic support.

5. **Dyadic overlap.** The proof assumes the dyadic partition has bounded overlap. Without this, logs could be misstated.

### R5 exact resonance

Potential obstruction: many $d$ could make $X/d$ or $(X/d+\rho)/4$ integral.

Resolution: exact resonances are controlled by divisor multiplicity. For $X/d\in\mathbb Z$, the product $X=md$ gives at most $\tau(X)$ such $d$, and each exact-resonance contribution is $O(1)$ after the $1/H$ normalization. For real noninteger $X$, group by nearby products $n=md$ and use $W_\Delta(X-n)$.

### Shifted residual exact resonance

Resolution: this is

$$

$$

The congruence

$$
4m-\rho\equiv-\rho\pmod4

restricts the complementary factor and cannot increase divisor multiplicity.
