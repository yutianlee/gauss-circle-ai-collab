$$
\psi_F(t)=t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12,
\qquad
\psi_F(n)=-\frac12.

$$
K_H(0)=H+1,
\qquad
\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12,

so the residual majorant covers the half-jump exactly. The Round 26 packet records this endpoint conversion as a stable part of the proof draft.

The notation map should be written explicitly in `best_proof_draft.md`:

The finite polynomial is

### 2. R5-Full-R26 as a final proof-draft lemma

Let $D$ be an active dyadic block with

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}.
$$

Choose an integer $H=H_D$ satisfying

$$
1\le H\asymp D X^{-1/4}.
$$

Define

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

The Fejer kernel satisfies the standard bound

$$
K_H(t)\ll \min\left(H,\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\right),
$$

hence

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

For exact resonances and near-resonances define

$$
W_\Delta(u)
=
\begin{cases}
1,&u=0,\\
\min(1,\Delta^2/u^2),&u\ne0.
\end{cases}
$$

This avoids singular displays at $u=0$ and is the correct cap for Fejer spikes.

#### First leg

For each $d\asymp D$, let $m=m(d)$ be a nearest integer to $X/d$. If there is a tie, choose the smaller nearest integer. The tie convention has no analytic effect; it only makes the map $d\mapsto m(d)$ deterministic.

Then

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
=
\frac{|X-md|}{d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-md|}{D}
$$

because $d\asymp D$. Therefore

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)
\ll
W_\Delta(X-md).
$$

Grouping by the integer product

$$
n=md
$$

gives at most $\tau(n)$ representations. Since $m=X/d+O(1)$ and $d\le X^{1/2}$ in the active range, for large $X$ one has $m>0$ and $n\asymp X$. Small $X$ can be absorbed into the implicit constant after increasing the base threshold $X\ge X_0$.

Thus

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n).
$$

Using

$$
\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon
$$

and

$$
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}W_\Delta(X-n)\ll \Delta+1,
$$

we get

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon(\Delta+1)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proves the first-leg residual bound for active dyadic blocks.

#### Shifted second legs

The shifted residuals contain

$$
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right),
\qquad \rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

For each $d\asymp D$, let $m$ be a nearest integer to $(X/d+\rho)/4$. Then

$$
\left\|
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}
\right\|
=
\frac{|X/d+\rho-4m|}{4}
=
\frac{|X-d(4m-\rho)|}{4d}.
$$

Let

$$
\ell=4m-\rho.
$$

Then

$$
\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4.
$$

For large active $X$, $\ell>0$ because $X/d\ge X^{1/2}$ and $m\asymp X/(4d)$. Small exceptions are included in the small-$X$ constant. The shifted residual is bounded by

$$
\frac1H
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll
W_\Delta(X-d\ell).
$$

Grouping by

$$
n=d\ell
$$

again gives divisor multiplicity at most $\tau(n)$; the congruence condition $\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4$ only reduces the number of representations. Therefore

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}
|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

#### Short blocks and dyadic summation

For $D<X^{1/4}$, do not invoke Vaaler. Use

$$
|\psi_F(t)|\le\frac12
$$

away from the fact that the floor-compatible value at integers is $-1/2$, so the absolute bound remains valid. The total contribution over short blocks is

$$
\sum_{D<X^{1/4}}O(D)\ll X^{1/4}
$$

with a logarithmic factor if dyadic blocks are counted separately. A bounded-overlap dyadic partition absorbs the remaining $O(\log X)$ loss into $X^\epsilon$.

Thus R5-Full-R26 is a proof-draft lemma conditional on H4.

### 6. One-page B-process/Hardy-collapse audit for $\mathcal M_2$

Start with

$$
S_2(D;X)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\beta_h
\sum_{d\in\mathbb Z}
w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)),
\qquad
\beta_h=\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h.
$$

Assume $w_D(u)=W(u/D)$ is smooth and supported in $[D,2D]$. For fixed $h$, define

$$
F_h(u)=w_D(u)e(hX/(4u)).
$$

With the Fourier convention

$$
\widehat F_h(n)=\int_{\mathbb R}F_h(u)e(-nu)\,du,
$$

Poisson gives the exact formal identity

$$
\sum_{d\in\mathbb Z}w_D(d)e(hX/(4d))
=
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}
I_{h,n},
$$

where

$$
I_{h,n}
=
\int_{\mathbb R}
w_D(u)e\left(\frac{hX}{4u}-nu\right)\,du.
$$

For $h>0$, the phase is

$$
\phi_{h,n}(u)=\frac{hX}{4u}-nu.
$$

Its derivative is

$$
\phi_{h,n}'(u)
=
-\frac{hX}{4u^2}-n.
$$

Thus a stationary point exists only for $n<0$. Write $n=-m$, $m>0$. Then

$$
\phi_{h,-m}(u)
=
\frac{hX}{4u}+mu,
$$

and the stationary point is

$$
u_0=\frac12\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}}.
$$

The support condition $u_0\asymp D$ gives the active dual length

$$
m\asymp \frac{hX}{D^2}.
$$

At the stationary point,

$$
\phi_{h,-m}(u_0)=\sqrt{hXm}.
$$

Also,

$$
\phi_{h,-m}''(u)
=
\frac{hX}{2u^3},
$$

so

$$
\phi_{h,-m}''(u_0)
=
\frac{4m^{3/2}}{(hX)^{1/2}}.
$$

The leading stationary-phase term is therefore

$$
I_{h,-m}
\approx
\frac{(hX)^{1/4}}{2m^{3/4}}
w_D\left(\frac12\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}}\right)
e\left(\sqrt{hXm}+\frac18\right),
$$

for interior stationary points. The corresponding scale is

$$
|I_{h,-m}|
\asymp
\frac{D^{3/2}}{(hX)^{1/2}}
$$

when $m\asymp hX/D^2$.

For $h<0$, the active sign is reversed and the conjugate formula is obtained after changing signs. This must be handled by the two-sided convention or by paired real formulas; mixing conventions risks a sign error.

Substitute this into $S_2$ for positive odd $h$ only, schematically:

$$
S_2(D;X)
\approx
\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}
\beta_h
\sum_{m\asymp hX/D^2}
\frac{(hX)^{1/4}}{2m^{3/4}}
w_D\left(\frac12\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}}\right)
e\left(\sqrt{hXm}+\frac18\right)
+
\text{conjugate/sign terms}.
$$

Now test the proposed Hardy-collapse substitution

$$
q=hm.
$$

The phase becomes

$$
e(\sqrt{Xq}).
$$

This resembles a Hardy/Voronoi square-root phase. But the coefficient for a given $q$ is not a standard divisor coefficient. It is approximately

$$
X^{1/4}q^{-3/4}
\sum_{\substack{h\mid q\\ h\le H_D\\ q/h\asymp hX/D^2}}
h\,\beta_h
\,
w_D\left(\frac12\sqrt{\frac{h^2X}{q}}\right).
$$

Since

$$
\beta_h=\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h
\asymp \frac{\chi_4(h)}{h}\Phi(h/(H_D+1))
$$

for odd $h$, the factor $h\beta_h$ is roughly a bounded Vaaler cutoff times $\chi_4(h)$, but only over divisors $h$ satisfying the support condition

$$
q/h\asymp hX/D^2.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
h\asymp D\sqrt{\frac qX}.
$$

Thus the $q$-coefficient is a local divisor-window coefficient, not $r_2(q)$, not $\tau(q)$, and not a complete Dirichlet convolution. At the endpoint $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, the condition becomes $m\asymp h$, so $q\asymp h^2$ and only divisors near $\sqrt q$ contribute. For smaller $D$, the dual length grows and the local divisor window changes.

Therefore the current conclusion is negative but useful:

$$
\text{B-process on }\mathcal M_2
\text{ gives a square-root phase but not a controlled Hardy-series reduction.}
$$

Missing before any promotion:

1. Uniform stationary phase with explicit error for $h$ from $1$ to $H_D$.
2. Nonstationary integration-by-parts bounds for $n$ outside $|n|\asymp |h|X/D^2$.
3. Boundary treatment when $u_0$ is near the support edge.
4. Treatment of $h$ very small and $m\asymp1$.
5. Two-sided sign convention for $h<0$ and $n>0$.
6. Exact transfer of $\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h$ into the $q=hm$ coefficient.
7. A theorem controlling the local divisor-window square-root sum.
8. A proof that truncation and smoothing errors stay below $X^{1/4+\epsilon}$.

This audit supports keeping HardyCollapse-Audit-R26 exploratory only.
