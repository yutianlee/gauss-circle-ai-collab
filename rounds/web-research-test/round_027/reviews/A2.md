## Most valuable input from others

### A1's B-Process/Hardy-Collapse Structural Audit
A1's most valuable contribution is the explicit derivation of the B-process/Hardy-collapse model for $\mathcal M_2$. A1 starts with the primal sum:
$$
S_2(D;X)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\beta_h
\sum_{d\in\mathbb Z}
w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)),
\qquad
\beta_h=\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h.
$$
Applying Poisson summation to the inner sum over $d$ yields the dual sum:
$$
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}
I_{h,n},
\qquad
I_{h,n}
=
\int_{\mathbb R}
w_D(u)e\left(\frac{hX}{4u}-nu\right)\,du.
$$
A1 correctly identifies that for $h>0$, the active stationary branch requires $n=-m<0$. The stationary point is $u_0 = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}}$, which enforces the dual length $m \asymp \frac{hX}{D^2}$. The leading stationary-phase amplitude is proportional to $\frac{(hX)^{1/4}}{m^{3/4}}$, and the phase is $\sqrt{hXm}$.

The critical insight from A1 is the analysis of the substitution $q=hm$. This substitution collapses the two-dimensional sum over $(h,m)$ into a one-dimensional sum over $q$ with phase $e(\sqrt{Xq})$. The length of the $q$ sum is $H_D \cdot M_{\rm dual} \asymp (D X^{-1/4}) (X^{-1/4} D^{-1} X) = X^{1/2}$, which is independent of the dyadic scale $D$. This matches the length of the classical Hardy-Voronoi series.

However, A1 correctly diagnoses that the coefficient of $e(\sqrt{Xq})$ is not a standard divisor coefficient. It is a local divisor-window coefficient:
$$
a_D(q)
\approx
X^{1/4}q^{-3/4}
\sum_{\substack{h\mid q\\ h\le H_D\\ q/h\asymp hX/D^2}}
h\,\beta_h
\,
w_D\left(\frac12\sqrt{\frac{h^2X}{q}}\right).
$$
The support condition $q/h \asymp hX/D^2$ restricts the sum over divisors $h$ to the narrow window $h \asymp D\sqrt{q/X}$. This proves that the B-process does not immediately reduce $\mathcal M_2$ to a known one-dimensional Hardy series. The local divisor-window coefficient $a_D(q)$ must be controlled, and its sparsity (many $q$ will have no divisors in this narrow window) is a central feature of the problem. This is a profound structural clarification that prevents the project from falsely assuming H13 is a trivial isomorphism to the classical circle problem.

## Possible errors or hidden assumptions

### A3's R5 Pseudocode Normalization Error
A3 provides a protocol for computing the R5 residual tables. The protocol implies computing the sum $\frac{1}{H_D} \sum_{d \asymp D} K_{H_D}(X/d)$ and then normalizing the result. However, A3's previous iterations of this pseudocode (and the implied logic in the current protocol) suggest dividing the total sum by $D X^{1/4}$.

This is a critical normalization error. The theoretical bound established by R5-Full is:
$$
\frac1{H_D} \sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_{H_D}(X/d) \ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$
This bound is *independent* of $D$. If the numerical script divides the sum by $D X^{1/4}$, it is testing the much weaker hypothesis that the sum is $\ll D X^{1/4}$. At the endpoint $D \asymp X^{1/2}$, dividing by $D X^{1/4}$ means dividing by $X^{3/4}$, which would artificially make the residual appear vanishingly small and mask any actual resonance spikes. The correct numerical protocol must compute the raw sum $\frac{1}{H_D} \sum_{d \asymp D} K_{H_D}(X/d)$ and divide *only* by $X^{1/4}$ to verify that the normalized value remains $O(1)$ across all dyadic scales.

### A1's Hardy-Collapse Boundary Assumption
A1's derivation of the Hardy-collapse model assumes that the Poisson summation integral $I_{h,n} = \int w_D(u)e(\frac{hX}{4u}-nu) du$ is perfectly approximated by its interior stationary point $u_0 = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}}$. This hides the assumption that the dyadic weight $w_D(u)$ is perfectly smooth and that the stationary point never falls in the transition region near the support boundaries $D$ and $2D$. In reality, when $m$ is near the edges of the dual range $m \asymp hX/D^2$, the stationary point hits the cutoff function, generating incomplete gamma functions or Fresnel integrals. These boundary terms do not cleanly collapse under the substitution $q=hm$ and must be bounded separately.

### The "Real Weights Only" Assumption in Paired Formulas
A1 and A3 correctly note that the paired real formulas for $\mathcal M_1$ and $\mathcal M_2$ require real dyadic weights $w_D(d)$. However, if future exploratory steps (such as the $\Delta$-method or spectral large sieve) require complex weights to separate variables or localize frequencies, the paired formulas will silently fail. The numerical regression of raw-vs-paired formulas must include a deliberate complex-weight failure test to ensure this assumption is actively monitored in the codebase.
