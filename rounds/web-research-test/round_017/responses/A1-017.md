## Detailed reasoning

### 1. H4-R17: source-normalized finite Vaaler approximation

Use the Fourier convention

$$
e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}.
$$

Vaaler defines the periodic trigonometric polynomials $i_N,j_N,k_N$ on printed page 207 by

$$
i_N(x)=\sum_{m=-\infty}^{\infty}I_{2N+2}(x+m),
$$

$$
j_N(x)=\sum_{m=-\infty}^{\infty}J_{N+1}(x+m),
$$

and

$$
k_N(x)=\sum_{m=-\infty}^{\infty}K_{N+1}(x+m),
$$

with finite Fourier expansions indexed by $-N\le n\le N$. Vaaler explicitly notes that $k_N$ is the periodic Fejer kernel.

Theorem 18 on printed page 210 states that

$$
\psi*j_N(x)
=
\sum_{\substack{n=-N\\n\ne0}}^N
(-2\pi i n)^{-1}\widehat J_{N+1}(n)e(nx)
$$

satisfies, among other properties,

$$
|\psi*j_N(x)-\psi(x)|\le (2N+2)^{-1}k_N(x).
$$

This is equation (7.14), with the proof passing through equation (7.17).

The coefficient function comes from Vaaler's function $J$. Around equations (2.32)--(2.33), the paper derives

$$
J(z)=\int_{-1}^1
\{\pi t(1-|t|)\cot(\pi t)+|t|\}e(tz)\,dt,
$$

and states that equation (2.28) follows. Thus, after the standard scaling in $j_N$,

$$
\widehat J_{N+1}(h)
=
\widehat J\left(\frac{h}{N+1}\right).
$$

Set

$$
H=N.
$$

Then the repo notation is

$$
K_H(t)=k_N(t)
=
\sum_{|k|\le H}
\left(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\right)e(kt)
=
\frac1{H+1}
\left(\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}\right)^2.
$$

Define

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u,
\qquad 0<u<1.
$$

Because $\widehat J$ is even in the relevant formula, the coefficient is

$$
\widehat J_{H+1}(h)
=
\Phi\left(\frac{|h|}{H+1}\right).
$$

Therefore the Vaaler polynomial in the repo convention is

$$
V_H(t)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht),
$$

where

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}.
$$

The sign is forced by the Fourier series

$$
\{t\}-\frac12
=
-\sum_{h\ne0}\frac{e(ht)}{2\pi i h}
$$

away from discontinuities. Since $\Phi(u)\to1$ as $u\to0^+$, the Vaaler coefficients are a smoothed truncation of these ordinary Fourier coefficients.

Thus the proof-draft H4 statement should be:

**H4-R17.** For every integer $H\ge1$ and every real $t$,

$$
\psi_F(t)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)
+
R_H^F(t),
$$

with

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
$$

and

$$
|R_H^F(t)|
\le
\frac1{2H+2}K_H(t).
$$

### 2. Centered-to-floor-compatible conversion at integers

Vaaler's periodic sawtooth is normalized at discontinuities. In repo notation, write

$$
\psi_C(t)=
\begin{cases}
t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12,&t\notin\mathbb Z,\\
0,&t\in\mathbb Z.
\end{cases}
$$

The arithmetic identity uses instead

$$
\psi_F(t)=t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12
$$

for all real $t$, so

$$
\psi_F(n)=-\frac12,
\qquad
\psi_C(n)=0.
$$

Off the integers the two functions agree. At an integer $n$, one has

$$
V_H(n)=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}=0,
$$

because the $h$ and $-h$ terms cancel. Also

$$
K_H(0)=H+1,
$$

so

$$
\frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(0)=\frac12.
$$

Therefore

$$
|\psi_F(n)-V_H(n)|=\frac12
=
\frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(0).
$$

The Fejer majorant exactly covers the half-jump. This is the key endpoint convention needed before applying H4 to the floor-compatible formula H3.

### 3. Coefficient conjugacy and paired formulas

Since $\Phi$ is real-valued on $(0,1)$,

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i(-h)}
=
\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}
=
\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

Thus the correct conjugacy relation is

$$
\boxed{\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.}
$$

The negative-conjugate relation is an algebra error.

Let

$$
A_h(D;X)
=
\sum_d \chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),
$$

and

$$
B_h(D;X)
=
\sum_d w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

Assume $w_D$ is real-valued. Then

$$
A_{-h}(D;X)=\overline{A_h(D;X)},
\qquad
B_{-h}(D;X)=\overline{B_h(D;X)}.
$$

For $h>0$,

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right).
$$

Therefore

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
\frac4\pi
\sum_{1\le h\le H_D}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\Im A_h(D;X).
$$

For the second leg, use

$$
e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=2i\chi_4(h).
$$

Only odd $h$ contribute. Pairing positive and negative frequencies gives

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
-\frac8\pi
\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\chi_4(h)\Re B_h(D;X).
$$

These paired formulas should be the default forms for numerical and theorem-comparison work. The raw complex formulas remain safer if a later dyadic decomposition uses complex weights.

### 4. R5-Full-R17: first residual leg

Let

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2},
\qquad
H\asymp D X^{-1/4},
\qquad
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

Let $w_D$ be a dyadic weight supported on $d\asymp D$ with $|w_D(d)|\le C$ and bounded overlap over $D$.

The Fejer kernel satisfies the pointwise bound

$$
K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(H,\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\right),
$$

where

$$
\|t\|=\min_{m\in\mathbb Z}|t-m|.
$$

Thus

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

Define the exact-resonance-safe weight

$$
W_\Delta(u)
=
\begin{cases}
1,&u=0,\\
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{u^2}\right),&u\ne0.
\end{cases}
$$

For each integer $d\asymp D$, choose $m=m(d)$ to be a nearest integer to $X/d$. If there is a tie, choose the smaller nearest integer. The estimate is unaffected by this convention. Then

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
=
\left|\frac Xd-m\right|
=
\frac{|X-md|}{d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-md|}{D}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)
\ll
W_\Delta(X-md).
$$

Group by

$$
n=md.
$$

For a fixed positive integer $n$, the number of representations $n=md$ with $d\asymp D$ is at most $\tau(n)$. Relevant $n$ lie in $X+O(D)$, but it is harmless to sum over all positive integers. Hence

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}
\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n).
$$

Using

$$
\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon
$$

and

$$
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}W_\Delta(X-n)\ll \Delta+1,
$$

we get

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_\epsilon
X^\epsilon(\Delta+1)
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This covers real and integer $X$. If exact resonance occurs, then $X=md$ is an integer and the term is counted by $W_\Delta(0)=1$ with multiplicity at most $\tau(X)$.

### 5. R5-Full-R17: shifted residual legs

Now fix

$$
\rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

We need to bound

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}
|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right).
$$

For each $d$, choose $m=m(d)$ nearest to

$$
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}.
$$

Then

$$
\left\|\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right\|
=
\frac{|X-d(4m-\rho)|}{4d}.
$$

Put

$$
\ell=4m-\rho.
$$

Then

$$
\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4.
$$

In the active range $D\le X^{1/2}$, one has $X/d\gg X^{1/2}$, so for all sufficiently large $X$ the nearest $m$ is positive and hence $\ell>0$. Finitely many small $X$ values may be absorbed into the implicit constant.

Because $d\asymp D$,

$$
\left\|\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right\|
\asymp
\frac{|X-d\ell|}{D}.
$$

Thus

$$
\frac1H
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll
W_\Delta(X-d\ell).
$$

Group by

$$
n=d\ell.
$$

The congruence condition $\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4$ only reduces the number of factorizations. Hence the multiplicity is still at most $\tau(n)$. Therefore

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}
|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_\epsilon
X^\epsilon
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}W_\Delta(X-n)
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proves both shifted legs.

### 6. Zero mode, signs, dyadic weights, and short blocks

The zero Fejer mode is included in $K_H$. Its contribution on a block is

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
\ll
\frac{D}{H}
=
\Delta
\asymp
X^{1/4}.
$$

Thus the zero mode is not a separate obstruction.

Both signs of the Fourier frequency are also harmless for the residual, because the residual is controlled by the positive even majorant $K_H$.

For dyadic weights, the residual estimate should use $|w_D|$, not cancellation. If the partition is nonnegative, this is automatic. If some smooth decomposition creates signed weights, bounded overlap plus $|w_D|$ gives only logarithmic losses, absorbed into $X^\epsilon$.

For $D<X^{1/4}$, do not apply Vaaler. Bound directly:

$$
\sum_{d<X^{1/4}}|\psi_F(\cdots)|
\ll X^{1/4}
$$

for each sawtooth leg, since $|\psi_F|\le1/2$. With a dyadic partition this is at worst $O(X^{1/4}\log X)$, still acceptable after the usual $\epsilon$-loss convention.

### 7. Bridge-R17: final conditional form

**Bridge-R17.** Assume:

1. H1--H3: the balanced hyperbola/floor-compatible sawtooth reduction

$$
P(X)
=
-4\sum_{d\le y}\chi_4(d)\psi_F(X/d)
+
4\sum_{d\le y}
\left[
\psi_F\left(\frac{X/d+1}{4}\right)
-
\psi_F\left(\frac{X/d+3}{4}\right)
\right]
+
O(1).
$$

2. H4-R17: the finite Vaaler approximation with Fejer residual majorant.

3. R5-Full-R17: all Vaaler residuals from the two sawtooth legs are

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

4. M9: for every dyadic $D$ in $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$,

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X),\mathcal M_2(D;X)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Then

$$
P(X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Proof. Split the $d$-sums in H3 into short blocks $D<X^{1/4}$ and active dyadic blocks $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$. Short blocks are trivially acceptable. On each active block, apply H4 with $H=H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$. The Vaaler main terms are precisely $\mathcal M_1(D;X)$ and $\mathcal M_2(D;X)$, up to harmless dyadic weights and constants. The residuals are bounded by R5-Full-R17. Summing over $O(\log X)$ dyadic blocks and absorbing the logarithm into $X^\epsilon$ gives the result.

This theorem remains conditional because M9 is unproved.

### 9. M9b shifted-$F$ theorem-extension problem

The second main term should not be treated as a rough bounded-variation $\chi_4(h)$ weight. Instead use the phase-shift form

$$
e(hX/(4d))(e(h/4)-e(3h/4))
=
e\left(h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac14\right)\right)
-
e\left(h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac34\right)\right).
$$

Write

$$
d=Dz,
\qquad
T=X,
\qquad
M=D.
$$

Then

$$
h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac{\rho}{4}\right)
=
\frac{hX}{D}
\left(
\frac1{4z}
+
\frac{\rho D}{4X}
\right),
\qquad
\rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

Define

$$
F_{\rho,D}(z)
=
\frac1{4z}
+
\frac{\rho D}{4X}.
$$

Its derivatives are

$$
F_{\rho,D}'(z)=-\frac1{4z^2},
$$

$$
F_{\rho,D}''(z)=\frac1{2z^3},
$$

and

$$
F_{\rho,D}'''(z)=-\frac3{2z^4}.
$$

Hence

$$
F_{\rho,D}'F_{\rho,D}'''-3(F_{\rho,D}'')^2
=
\frac3{8z^6}-\frac3{4z^6}
=
-\frac3{8z^6}\ne0.
$$

The derivative nondegeneracy is unchanged by the additive constant $\rho D/(4X)$.

The theorem-extension problem is:

**M9b-shifted-$F$ target.** Prove or cite an endpoint reciprocal-sum theorem for

$$
S_\rho(D;X)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\sum_d
w_D(d)
e\left(
\frac{hX}{D}F_{\rho,D}(d/D)
\right),
$$

where

$$
F_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac1{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X},
\qquad
\rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

The theorem used must explicitly allow:

1. a $D,X$-dependent additive shift in $F$;
2. derivative constants uniform for $z$ in the support of the dyadic weight;
3. smooth dyadic $d$-weights;
4. dyadic $h$-weights coming from the actual Vaaler coefficient $\alpha_{h,H_D}$;
5. endpoint height $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$;
6. the paired positive/negative frequency formulation;
7. the required target size $X^{1/4+\epsilon}$ after summing dyadic $h$-blocks.

The fractional-frequency representation

$$
h=4q+r,\qquad r\in\{1,3\},
$$

which gives phases

$$
e((q+r/4)X/d),
$$

is algebraically valid but worse for black-box theorem comparison unless the theorem explicitly accepts fixed fractional frequency shifts.

### 10. H13 falsification checklist

H13 remains an exploratory sign-preserving transform for M9a, not a proof.

Start with

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
\sum_d\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d).
$$

After splitting modulo $4$ and applying Poisson summation, the expected transform is of the form

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
c
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}
\chi_4(n)
\int_\mathbb R
w_D(u)e(hX/u-nu/4)\,du,
$$

with convention-dependent constant $c$; prior normalization suggests $c=i/2$, but this must be checked under the fixed Fourier convention.

The phase is

$$
\phi_n(u)=\frac{hX}{u}-\frac{nu}{4}.
$$

Stationary points require

$$
\phi_n'(u)=-\frac{hX}{u^2}-\frac n4=0,
$$

so the active sign is $n<0$. Put $n=-m$ with $m>0$. Then

$$
u_0=2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}},
$$

and the active dual length is

$$
m\asymp \frac{hX}{D^2}.
$$

At maximal Vaaler height

$$
h\asymp H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4},
$$

and with $D=X^\delta$, this gives

$$
m\asymp X^{3/4-\delta}.
$$

The dual length is roughly endpoint-balanced only near

$$
D\asymp X^{1/2},
$$

where

$$
m\asymp h\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

The leading stationary phase is proportional to

$$
\sqrt{Xhm}.
$$

Its Hessian determinant vanishes:

$$
\det \nabla^2\sqrt{Xhm}=0.
$$

Therefore generic full-rank two-dimensional stationary phase or full-rank decoupling is not available.

The falsification checklist is:

1. Fix the exact Poisson constant and active sign.
2. Write the actual post-H13 kernel, including stationary amplitude weights and support cutoffs.
3. Apply the first intended Cauchy--Schwarz, spacing, or matrix step.
4. If the dual character appears only as a diagonal unitary conjugation

$$
U=\operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(m)),
$$

and the proof then takes an operator norm, Frobenius norm, Schur/Gershgorin bound, absolute-value matrix, or cyclic trace, Q1-Spectral erases the character. That version of H13 should be deprioritized.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

### 1. Exact Fejer resonances

The main possible failure mode in R5 is a large cluster of $d$ such that $X/d$ or $(X/d+\rho)/4$ is integral or nearly integral. The product-count proof handles this: exact resonances are counted by $W_\Delta(0)=1$, and the number of product representations is bounded by $\tau(n)$. Thus even square or divisor-rich $X$ does not create a dense obstruction in R5.

### 2. Short-block false start

If one applies Vaaler to $D<X^{1/4}$, the natural height $H_D\asymp DX^{-1/4}$ may be less than $1$. This is avoided by removing short blocks first and using $|\psi_F|\le1/2$.

### 3. Li--Yang endpoint obstruction

The endpoint height is

$$
\beta_V=\delta-\frac14.
$$

Case A is always lower by

$$
\frac{2}{41},
$$

and the final $\theta^*$ range is always lower by

$$
\theta^*-\frac14\approx0.064483.
$$

So the raw endpoint block is not covered by current printed Li--Yang technology. This is not a no-go theorem, but it blocks black-box import.

### 4. Operator-norm character blindness

If an M9 proof first turns $\chi_4(d)$ into

$$
U=\operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(d))
$$

and then bounds

$$
U^*KU
$$

only by operator norm, Frobenius norm, Schur/Gershgorin, absolute-value matrices, or cyclic traces, the character is invisible. This is a diagnostic obstruction to a large class of naive spacing arguments.

### 5. H13 degeneracy

The H13 dual phase is proportional to

$$
\sqrt{Xhm},
$$

whose continuous Hessian determinant is zero. This blocks generic full-rank stationary phase or full-rank decoupling arguments on the dual phase. H13 must be justified by a discrete signed-spacing argument, not by formal Hessian heuristics.
