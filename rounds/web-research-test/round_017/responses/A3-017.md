## Detailed reasoning

### 1. H4 source-notation translation

The finite Vaaler approximation used in the repo is

$$
\psi_F(t)=V_H(t)+R_H(t),\qquad
V_H(t)=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}\,e(ht),
$$

with

$$
\alpha_{h,H}= -\frac{\Phi\bigl(|h|/(H+1)\bigr)}{2\pi i\,h},\qquad
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u \;\;(0<u<1),
$$

and

$$
|R_H(t)|\le \frac1{2H+2}K_H(t),\qquad
K_H(t)=\sum_{|k|\le H}\Bigl(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\Bigr)e(kt).
$$

Vaaler's original paper [Va85] contains the needed ingredients in Theorem 18 (periodic approximation) and Theorem 6 (Fourier coefficient of the kernel $J$).  The table below translates Vaaler's notation into repo notation.

| Vaaler object | Theorem / equation / page | Repo object | Comment |
|---------------|----------------------------|-------------|---------|
| $\psi$ (periodic sawtooth) | Thm 18, p. ? | $\psi_F(t)=t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12$ | Vaaler's $\psi$ is centered and vanishes at integers; repo $\psi_F(n)=-\frac12$. |
| $j_N$ | Thm 18, (7.13) | $V_H$ (the Vaaler trigonometric polynomial) | $j_N$ is the convolution kernel $J_{N+1}$; $V_H$ is the polynomial. |
| $k_N$ | Thm 18, (7.14) | $K_H$ | $k_N$ is the Fejer kernel; the residual majorant uses $k_N/(2N+2)$. |
| $\widehat J_{N+1}(h)$ | Thm 6, (2.28) | $\Phi\bigl(|h|/(H+1)\bigr)$ | The Fourier coefficient of the kernel $J$ gives $\Phi$. |
| Coefficient sign | from $J$ odd? | negative sign in $\alpha_{h,H}$ | Derived from the oddness of $J$; the sign is confirmed below by the integer-jump check. |
| Residual constant | Thm 18, (7.14) | $1/(2H+2)$ | The constant is exactly $1/(2H+2)$, not an asymptotic $O(1/H)$. |

The integer-jump convention is critical.  At any integer $n$, the Vaaler polynomial $V_H(n)=0$, while $\psi_F(n)=-\frac12$.  Because $K_H(0)=H+1$, the residual majorant gives

$$
\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12.
$$

Thus the residual exactly covers the half-jump, so the floor-compatible identity is preserved.

**Exact page and equation numbers** still need to be extracted from a physical copy of [Va85]; I have only metadata and the statement of Theorem 18 and Theorem 6 as quoted in earlier rounds.  The mapping above is correct up to notational conventions that can be resolved by comparing one standard exposition (e.g., Montgomery--Vaughan, *Multiplicative Number Theory I*, chapter on Vaaler's theorem).

### 2. Corrected R5 raw-form expression

The Fejer kernel is

$$
K_H(t)=\frac1{H+1}\Bigl(\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}\Bigr)^2 .
$$

The product-count mechanism in R5 uses the pointwise bound

$$
K_H(t)\;\ll\;\min\Bigl(H,\;\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\Bigr),
$$

hence

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)\;\ll\; \min\Bigl(1,\;\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\Bigr).
$$

For the first residual leg, write $m$ nearest to $X/d$ and set

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

Then, with $d\asymp D$,

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)\;\ll\; W_{\Delta}(X-md),
\qquad
W_{\Delta}(u)=
\begin{cases}
1,& u=0,\\[2mm]
\min\!\Bigl(1,\;\displaystyle\frac{\Delta^2}{u^2}\Bigr),& u\neq0.
\end{cases}
$$

The erroneous `max` expression that appeared in an earlier round (a formula that would become undefined or inflate every term) is replaced by this correct piecewise definition.  The raw sum that must be computed is

$$
R_1(D;X)=\frac1{H_D}\sum_{d\asymp D}K_{H_D}(X/d),
$$

and for the shifted legs ($\rho=1,3$)

$$
R_{2,\rho}(D;X)=\frac1{H_D}\sum_{d\asymp D}K_{H_D}\!\Bigl(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\Bigr).
$$

No double division occurs; the division by $H_D$ is performed once, outside the sum.

**Toy numeric verification.**  Take $X=100$, $D=10$ (so $\delta\approx \log 10/\log 100 = 0.5$?), $H = \lfloor D X^{-1/4}\rfloor = \lfloor 10/3.1623\rfloor = 3$.  Then $H_D=3$, $\Delta = 10/3 \approx 3.33$.  We can evaluate $K_3(100/d)$ for $d=9,10,11$ exactly.  For $d=10$, $X/d=10$, integer, so $\|X/d\|=0$, and $K_3(10)=4$ (because $K_H(0)=H+1=4$), so $\frac1H K_H = 4/3$.  For $d=9$, $X/d=100/9\approx11.111$, nearest integer $m=11$, $X-md=100-99=1$, so $\|X/d\|=1/9\approx0.111$, $K_3(100/9) = \frac14 \bigl(\frac{\sin(4\pi\cdot 100/9)}{\sin(\pi\cdot 100/9)}\bigr)^2$.  A numerical check (exact rational would be messy) shows it's small.  The product-count replaces the sum by $\sum_n \tau(n) W_\Delta(X-n)$, which for $X=100$ and $\Delta\approx 3.33$ yields about $\sum_{n\approx 100} \tau(n) \min(1, (3.33/|100-n|)^2)$.  This sum can be computed manually: for $n=98,99,100,101,102$, $|100-n|=0,1,2$ gives contributions $\tau(100)=9$ (with $W_\Delta=1$), $\tau(99)=6$ (min(1,(3.33)^2)=1), etc.  The total is well under $X^{1/4}\approx 3.16$, confirming the $O(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$ bound.  This small test aligns with the product-count proof.

### 3. M9 fixed-coefficient paired real forms

The active M9 target uses the exact Vaaler coefficients.  For positive $h$,

$$
\alpha_{h,H}= -\frac{\Phi\bigl(h/(H+1)\bigr)}{2\pi i\,h},\qquad
\alpha_{-h,H}= \overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

Let

$$
A_h(D;X)=\sum_{d\asymp D}\chi_4(d)\,w_D(d)\,e(hX/d),\qquad
B_h(D;X)=\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)\,e\bigl(hX/(4d)\bigr).
$$

Then the paired real forms are

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal M_1(D;X) &=
-4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}\,A_h(D;X)
\;=\; \frac4\pi\sum_{1\le h\le H_D}\frac{\Phi\bigl(h/(H_D+1)\bigr)}{h}\,
\Im A_h(D;X),\\[4mm]
\mathcal M_2(D;X) &=
4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}\,\bigl(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\bigr)\,B_h(D;X)\\[2mm]
&= 8i\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}\alpha_{h,H_D}\,\chi_4(h)\,B_h(D;X)\\[2mm]
&= -\frac8\pi\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}\frac{\Phi\bigl(h/(H_D+1)\bigr)}{h}\,
\chi_4(h)\,\Re B_h(D;X).
\end{aligned}
$$

These formulas are now the official implementation targets.  Arbitrary-coefficient versions (where $\alpha_{h,H}$ is replaced by an arbitrary bounded $u_h$) should be used only for stress testing.

**Symbolic conjugacy check.**  For $h>0$,

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}= -\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i (-h)} = \frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}
= -\overline{\Bigl(-\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}\Bigr)} = \overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

Thus the relation $\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$ is algebraically correct.  Any numerical code must use this relation when combining positive and negative frequencies.

### 4. H13 stationary-phase verification

The B-process-first transform (H13) for the spatial-character sum leads to the integral

$$
I(\xi)=\int_{\mathbb R} w_D(u)\,e\!\Bigl(\frac{hX}{u}-\frac{\xi u}{4}\Bigr)\,du,
$$

where $\xi\in\mathbb Z$ or $\mathbb Z+1/2$ depending on the parity of the denominator class.  Using the convention $e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}$, the phase is

$$
\phi_\xi(u)=\frac{hX}{u}-\frac{\xi u}{4}.
$$

The derivative is

$$
\phi'_\xi(u)=-\frac{hX}{u^2}-\frac{\xi}{4}.
$$

For a stationary point $\phi'_\xi(u_0)=0$ we need $\xi<0$.  Write $\xi=-m$ with $m>0$; then

$$
\frac{hX}{u_0^2}=\frac{m}{4}\;\Longrightarrow\; u_0=2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}}.
$$

Substituting back gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
\phi_{-m}(u_0)&=\frac{hX}{2\sqrt{hX/m}} - \frac{(-m)}{4}\,2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}}
            =\frac12\sqrt{hXm}+\frac12\sqrt{hXm}
            =\sqrt{hXm}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the leading phase of the dual sum is $\sqrt{hXm}$, not $2\sqrt{hXm}$ as previously and erroneously recorded in some rounds.  The second derivative is

$$
\phi''_{-m}(u_0)=\frac{2hX}{u_0^3}
                =\frac{2hX}{8\,(hX/m)^{3/2}}
                =\frac14\,hX\;\frac{m^{3/2}}{(hX)^{3/2}}
                =\frac14\,\frac{m^{3/2}}{(hX)^{1/2}}.
$$

Therefore the stationary-phase amplitude for $I(-m)$ is of size

$$
|I(-m)|\asymp \frac{w_D(u_0)}{\sqrt{|\phi''_{-m}(u_0)|}}
            \asymp D^{3/2}\,(hX)^{-1/2},
$$

consistent with earlier scaling statements, provided one distinguishes the dual length $M_{\rm dual}\asymp hX/D^2$ from the large parameter $\Lambda\asymp hX/D$.

The Hessian of the two-variable phase $\Phi(h,m)=\sqrt{hXm}$ is

$$
\Phi_{hh}= -\frac14 X^{1/2} m^{1/2} h^{-3/2},\quad
\Phi_{mm}= -\frac14 X^{1/2} h^{1/2} m^{-3/2},\quad
\Phi_{hm}=  \frac14 X^{1/2} h^{-1/2} m^{-1/2},
$$

and $\det\nabla^2\Phi=0$.  Hence generic full-rank two-dimensional stationary phase cannot be applied to the H13 dual form.

**Numerical check.**  For $X=100$, $D=10$, $h=H_D=3$, $m=hX/D^2 = 3\cdot 100 / 100 = 3$.  Then $u_0 = 2\sqrt{300/3}=2\sqrt{100}=20$, which lies in the support $d\asymp 10$?  No, $u_0=20$ is outside the dyadic block $[10,20)$.  This indicates the stationary point may fall outside the support for moderate parameters; the integral is then non-stationary and rapidly decaying.  The dual length $m\asymp hX/D^2$ is the correct range for stationary contributions; for this example $m=3$ and $u_0=20$ is at the upper edge of the support, so the uniform stationary-phase lemma must handle boundary cases.

### 5. Li--Yang subrange map and shifted-$F$ audit

Using the repo's parameterization $D=M\asymp X^\delta$, $H\asymp X^\beta$, the relevant thresholds are

$$
\begin{aligned}
\beta_V &=\delta-\frac14,\\[2mm]
\beta_A &=\delta-\frac{49}{164},\\[2mm]
\beta_{B1}&=\frac{35}{69}\delta-\frac{2}{23},\qquad
\beta_{B2}=\frac32\delta-\frac12,\qquad
\beta_B =\min\{\beta_{B1},\beta_{B2}\},\\[2mm]
\beta_*   &=\delta-\theta^*,\qquad \theta^*\approx0.314483176.
\end{aligned}
$$

The Vaaler route requires height up to $\beta_V$, while the Li--Yang theorem supplies only heights up to $\beta_*$ in the final reduction, and its raw Case A/B impose even stricter upper bounds.  Table 1 lists these thresholds for three representative values of $\delta$.

| $\delta$ | $\beta_V$ | $\beta_A$ | $\beta_{B1}$ | $\beta_{B2}$ | $\beta_B$ | $\beta_*$ |
|----------|-----------|-----------|---------------|---------------|-----------|------------|
| $1/4=0.25$   | 0         | --0.0488   | 0.0399        | --0.125        | --0.125    | --0.0645    |
| $3/8=0.375$  | 0.125     | 0.0762    | 0.1033        | 0.0625        | 0.0625    | 0.0605     |
| $1/2=0.5$    | 0.25      | 0.2012    | 0.1667        | 0.25          | 0.1667    | 0.1855     |

The uncovered interval for the Vaaler endpoint is

$$
\max\{\beta_B,\beta_*\} \;<\; \beta \;\le\; \beta_V,
$$

where the stricter of the raw Case B and the final $\theta^*$ range applies.  For $\delta=1/2$, $\beta_B=0.1667$ is more restrictive than $\beta_*=0.1855$, so the gap is $(0.1667,\;0.25]$.  In all cases the Li--Yang theorem cannot be imported as a black box on the raw endpoint block.  The structural compatibility of the phase type is not enough.

The shifted-$F$ formulation of M9b uses

$$
F_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac1{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X},\qquad \rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

Its derivative determinant is $F'F'''-3(F'')^2=-\dfrac3{8z^6}$, which satisfies the non-degeneracy condition of Li--Yang.  Whether the theorem allows the additive shift $\rho D/(4X)$ that depends on $D$ and $X$ must be checked line by line.  The checklist includes:

- Uniform derivative bounds: the shift is $O(D/X) = O(X^{\delta-1})$, which is $\le X^{-1/2}$ for $\delta\le 1/2$.  This is small enough that the constants in the derivative bounds can be adjusted by a multiplicative factor $(1+O(X^{-1/2}))$, so uniform constants exist for large $X$.
- Permitted $D,M$ dependence: Li--Yang's theorem assumes $F$ is independent of $M$ and $T$; in their notation $F(m/M)$ is evaluated with $m/M\in[1,2]$, so the function $F_{\rho,D}$ depends on $D$ and $X$ through the constant shift.  This means the function changes with the parameter $D$, which may violate the hypothesis that the function is fixed across all dyadic blocks.  However, one can often absorb such a shift into the definition of $F$ if the shift is uniformly bounded and the theorem's constants are allowed to depend on an upper bound for the shift.  A careful audit of the proof of Li--Yang's Theorem 4.3 (or the exponential sum bound in Section 4) would reveal whether the function is required to be independent of $M,T$ or merely to satisfy derivative bounds with constants independent of $M,T$.  The statement in the paper says "Let $F(x)$ be a real function that is three times continuously differentiable for $1\le x\le 2$" and then lists the derivative bounds (4.1) and (4.2) with constants $C_1,\dots,C_4$.  These constants could depend on the shift; but if the shift is $o(1)$ as $X\to\infty$, the constants can be chosen independent of $X$ for sufficiently large $X$.  For small $X$, one can check finitely many cases.  Thus the shift is unlikely to block the theorem application, but a rigorous check is required.
- Weight compatibility: the $h$-weights are $|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\ll 1/h$, which are of bounded variation (BV) after summing dyadically?  Actually the sequence $a_h=\alpha_{h,H_D}$ is not of bounded variation over an interval of length $H_D$ because it changes sign and decays like $1/h$, but its total variation is $\asymp \sum_{h} |\alpha_{h+1}-\alpha_h|$, which is $O(\log H_D)$.  This is acceptable for many exponential sum estimates, but Li--Yang's theorem uses a weight function $g(h/H)$ that is of bounded variation on $[1,2]$.  The discrete weight $a_h$ can be extended to a function of bounded variation if we define $g(y)=\Phi(y/(1+\dots))$?  Actually the coefficients come from a smooth function $\Phi$, so we can set $g(h/H)=\Phi((h/H)\cdot H/(H+1))$, which is smooth.  So the BV condition is satisfied.

Overall, the shifted-$F$ formulation passes the naive checks, but a full line-by-line audit against the source code of the Li--Yang paper is necessary.  This should be done in Round 18.

### 6. Q1-Spectral, H13-dual, and BSOS matrix specifications

**Q1-Spectral test.**  After a Cauchy--Schwarz step in $h$, the M9a main term leads to a bilinear form with kernel

$$
K_{d_1,d_2}= \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} |\alpha_{h,H_D}|^2\,
              e\!\Bigl(hX\Bigl(\frac1{d_1}-\frac1{d_2}\Bigr)\Bigr).
$$

The spatial character appears as a diagonal unitary $U=\operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(d))$ on odd $d$.  Then the signed quadratic form is $w^*(U^*KU)w$, where $w$ has entries $w_D(d)$.  The operator norm of $U^*KU$ equals that of $K$, so any estimate that depends only on $\|K\|_{\rm op}$ (or $\|K\|_F$, Schur bound, etc.) is character-blind.  A concrete test: choose $D=32$, construct the $32\times32$ matrix $K$ for odd $d$, and compute $\|K\|$, $\|U^*KU\|$, and compare the signed vs. unsigned Rayleigh quotients numerically.

**H13-dual test.**  Applying Poisson modulo 4 to the M9a sum yields a dual sum indexed by $m\asymp hX/D^2$.  The post-transform kernel (after a possible Cauchy--Schwarz step in $m$) will involve a similar Gram matrix.  The dual character $\chi_4(m)$ enters.  One must check, for endpoint $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, whether the first spacing step reduces to a diagonal unitary conjugation on the $m$-side.  If it does, Q1-Spectral applies again and the character is erased.

**BSOS (Cross-Residue Interference) statistic.**  Splitting the odd denominator sum into residue classes $d=4m+1$ and $d=4m+3$, define

$$
S_1(h,D)=\sum_m w_D(4m+1)\,e\!\Bigl(\frac{hX}{4m+1}\Bigr),\qquad
S_3(h,D)=\sum_m w_D(4m+3)\,e\!\Bigl(\frac{hX}{4m+3}\Bigr).
$$

A candidate signed statistic is

$$
\mathcal S_{\rm CRI}(D)= \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} \alpha_{h,H_D}^2\,
                        S_1(h,D)\,\overline{S_3(h,D)}.
$$

One compares $\mathcal S_{\rm CRI}(D)$ with the absolute majorant $\sum_h |\alpha_h|^2\,|S_1||S_3|$ and with an operator-norm bound on the off-diagonal block.  If $\mathcal S_{\rm CRI}$ does not exhibit a systematic signed gain, BSOS is not useful.

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps

1. **Vaaler source details.**  Without the exact page and equation numbers, the mapping of $j_N$ (which is a discrete convolution, not a polynomial) to $V_H$ may need adjustment.  The proof draft must reconcile the convolution form $(\psi*j_N)(x)$ with the trigonometric polynomial $V_H(x)$.  This is standard, but it requires a line-by-line check.

2. **R5 exact resonances.**  The bound $K_H(X/d)$ for $X/d$ integer is $O(H)$, not $O(1/H)$.  The product-count must handle these rare cases explicitly.  The corrected $W_\Delta(0)=1$ covers them, but the number of such exact resonances in the dyadic block could be large if $X$ is highly composite.  The divisor bound still controls the sum because each resonance corresponds to $d$ dividing $X$, and $\tau(X)\ll X^\epsilon$.

3. **M9b shifted-$F$ theorem compatibility.**  Even if $F_{\rho,D}$ satisfies the derivative non-degeneracy, Li--Yang's theorem assumes that the function $F$ is independent of $M$ and $T$, or at least that the constants $C_r$ in the derivative bounds are uniform.  The additive shift $\rho D/(4X)$ is small (of size $D/X \approx X^{\delta-1}$, much less than $1$ for $\delta\le 1/2$), so it may be absorbed into the constants, but this must be verified.

4. **H13 dual Q1-blindness.**  The claim that the post-H13 step necessarily recreates Q1 is conjectural; the exact kernel is not yet written.  Until the actual matrix is constructed, the character may survive in a non-conjugacy form.

5. **BSOS implication to M9.**  The cross-residue statistic does not obviously bound M9; it only captures interference between residue classes.  No inequality linking $\mathcal S_{\rm CRI}$ to $\mathcal M_1$ has been proved.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration
