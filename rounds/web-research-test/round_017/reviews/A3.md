## Possible errors or hidden assumptions

*   **A1 -- M9b formula error (detailed above).**  This is the most concrete algebraic mistake.  It can be verified by a small numerical check (pick a few \(d\) values, compute both forms).  If A3 or another agent runs such a check, the error will be evident.

*   **A1 -- Vaaler coefficient function \(\Phi\) and sign.**  A1 writes \(\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u\) (with absolute value on \(u\)?  They wrote \(|u|\) earlier but now \(u\) for positive \(h/(H+1)\)).  The domain is \(0<u<1\), so it is fine.  They also mention \(\Phi(0)=1\) (continuous extension).  The coefficient \(\alpha_{h,H}=-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}\) is consistent with the Fourier series.  However, they wrote earlier that the Vaaler polynomial vanishes at integers because the \(h\) and \(-h\) terms cancel.  That cancellation requires \(\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}\) (which holds) and \(e(hn)+e(-hn)=2\), but also the imaginary nature of \(\alpha_{h,H}\); they argued that \(\operatorname{Re}(\alpha_{h,H})=0\) because \(\alpha_{h,H}\) is pure imaginary, so the sum is zero.  This is correct.  But they also wrote "\(\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u\)" without the absolute value; that's fine for positive \(u\).  The special values \(\Phi(1/2)=1/2\), \(\Phi(1/4)=3\pi/16+1/4\), \(\Phi(3/4)=-3\pi/16+3/4\) are consistent.

*   **A1 -- Li-Yang subrange map uses the raw Case A restriction \(H\le MT^{-49/164}\).**  The original Li--Yang definition (Case A) includes logarithmic factors; the omission of logs does not affect the qualitative conclusion but should be noted.  Also, the final Li--Yang reduction gives \(H\le MT^{-\theta^*}\) with \(\theta^*=0.31448\ldots\); A1's table correctly shows the gap.

*   **A2 -- The H13-Dual stationary-phase analysis uses the leading term only.**  The actual transform involves a sum over all \(n\) (including non-stationary contributions) and requires uniform bounds.  A2's conclusion that the dual phase is \(\sqrt{Xhm}\) with Hessian degenerate is correct, but the claim that the dual kernel's operator-norm blindness is inevitable after Cauchy--Schwarz over \(h\) rests on the assumption that the amplitude weights \(A(h,m)\) are well-behaved and that the diagonal unitary conjugation form is retained.  Boundary terms and non-stationary contributions could introduce offsets that break the simple conjugacy structure.

*   **A2 -- The operator-norm argument in Q1-Spectral bounds \(v^*U^*KU v\) by \(|\mathcal D_{\rm odd}| \|K\|_{\operatorname{op}}\).**  This is a crude bound; it loses any potential sign cancellation that might occur if \(v\) is not the all-ones vector but a vector with varying magnitudes.  In the actual setting, \(v_d = w_D(d)\), which may be smooth and decay near the support boundary; still, the operator-norm bound is valid.  The diagnostic is correct for the stated reduction.

*   **Hidden assumption in A2's BSOS: that the Cauchy--Schwarz step produces a kernel \(K_{d_1,d_2}\) with weights exactly \(|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\).**  In the actual Bombieri--Iwaniec method, one often uses a smoothed large sieve that may involve different coefficients (e.g., \(\alpha_h^2\) or a weight from the majorant).  The matrix may be slightly different.  But the diagnostic remains valid for any matrix that is conjugated by \(U\).

*   **A1's R5-Full proof implicitly assumes that the dyadic weight \(w_D\) is real and non-negative?**  Actually they use \(|w_D|\) to be safe; that's fine.
