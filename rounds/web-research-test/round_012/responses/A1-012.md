## Detailed reasoning

### 1. H4: finite Vaaler theorem in the normalization needed here

Let

$$
e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}.
$$

Define the Fejer kernel

$$
K_H(t)
=
\sum_{|k|\le H}
\left(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\right)e(kt)
=
\frac{1}{H+1}
\left(
\frac{\sin \pi(H+1)t}{\sin \pi t}
\right)^2.
$$

The Montgomery--Vaughan exposition defines

$$
\varphi(t)
=
\begin{cases}
1, & t=0,\\
\pi(1-|t|)t\cot(\pi t)+|t|, & 0<|t|\le 1,\\
0, & |t|>1,
\end{cases}
$$

and derives the coefficient formula used in the Vaaler approximation. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7} In the notation of this packet, write $\Phi(u)=\varphi(u)$ for $0\le u<1$.

The form needed here is:

**H4. Vaaler finite approximation with Fejer residual.** For $H\ge 1$, define

$$
V_H(t)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht),
\qquad
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}.
$$

Then the floor-compatible sawtooth

$$
\psi(t)=t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12
$$

satisfies

$$
|\psi(t)-V_H(t)|
\le
\frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(t)
$$

for all real $t$.

The sign in $\alpha_{h,H}$ deserves a separate check. Montgomery--Vaughan’s displayed inequality approximates $1/2-\{x\}$ by a trigonometric polynomial whose nonzero coefficients are

$$
\frac{\varphi(k/(K+1))}{2\pi i k}.
$$

Their residual is bounded by the Fejer kernel term $\Delta_{K+1}(x)/(2(K+1))$. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8} Since away from integers

$$
\psi(t)=\{t\}-\frac12
=
-\left(\frac12-\{t\}\right),
$$

the coefficient for $\psi$ has the negative sign

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}.
$$

At integer $t=n$, the trigonometric polynomial satisfies $V_H(n)=0$ because the terms with $h$ and $-h$ cancel. The floor-compatible sawtooth has $\psi(n)=-1/2$. Meanwhile

$$
K_H(0)=H+1,
\qquad
\frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(0)=\frac12.
$$

Thus H4 covers the jump discrepancy exactly at integers:

$$
|\psi(n)-V_H(n)|=\frac12.
$$

This proves that the Vaaler theorem is compatible with the floor convention used in H3.

### 2. Fejer pointwise bound

From the standard formula for $K_H$,

$$
K_H(t)=\frac1{H+1}
\left(\frac{\sin \pi(H+1)t}{\sin \pi t}\right)^2,
$$

one has

$$
K_H(t)\ll \min\left(H,\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\right),
$$

where $\|t\|$ is the distance to the nearest integer. Hence

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

The precise constant is irrelevant for exponent purposes. The important point is that this bound is pointwise and positive. Therefore the Vaaler residual can be bounded directly without expanding into arbitrary $k$-coefficient sums.

### 3. R5 first-leg product-count bound

Let $X\ge2$, let

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2},
$$

and choose

$$
H\asymp D X^{-1/4}.
$$

Let $w_D$ be any dyadic weight supported on $d\asymp D$ with $|w_D(d)|\le 1$. The first-leg residual is bounded by

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}
|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d).
$$

For each $d$, choose an integer $m=m(d)$ nearest to $X/d$. Ties are harmless; choose either nearest integer. Since $d\asymp D$,

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
=
\left|\frac Xd-m\right|
=
\frac{|X-md|}{d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-md|}{D}.
$$

Set

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}.
$$

With $H\asymp D X^{-1/4}$, one has

$$
\Delta\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-md|^2}\right).
$$

Now group terms by the product

$$
n=md.
$$

For $d\asymp D$ and $m$ nearest to $X/d$, the relevant $n$ lie in a fixed interval $n\asymp X$ after enlarging constants. For a fixed positive integer $n$, the number of representations $n=md$ with $d\asymp D$ is at most $\tau(n)$. Thus

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}
|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}
\tau(n)
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right).
$$

Using the pointwise divisor bound

$$
\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon
$$

gives

$$
\sum_{n\asymp X}
\tau(n)
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right)
\ll_\epsilon
X^\epsilon
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right).
$$

The last sum is

$$
\ll \Delta+1.
$$

Indeed, the terms with $|n-X|\le \Delta$ contribute $O(\Delta+1)$, and the tail contributes

$$
\sum_{j>\Delta}\frac{\Delta^2}{j^2}
\ll \Delta.
$$

Since $\Delta\asymp X^{1/4}$,

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}
|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This argument covers both integer and noninteger $X$. If $X$ is an integer and $n=X$ occurs, the summand is interpreted as $1$, and the contribution is at most $\tau(X)\ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon$, which is negligible relative to $X^{1/4+\epsilon}$.

### 4. R5 shifted second-leg product-count bound

The second leg contains residual kernels of the form

$$
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right),
\qquad
\rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

For each $d$, choose $m$ nearest to

$$
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}.
$$

Then

$$
\left\|\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right\|
=
\frac{|X/d+\rho-4m|}{4}
=
\frac{|X-d(4m-\rho)|}{4d}.
$$

Write

$$
\ell=4m-\rho.
$$

Then

$$
\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod 4.
$$

For the present ranges $X\ge2$ and $d\le X^{1/2}$, the nearest $m$ has $\ell\asymp X/d>0$, except for finitely many small-$X$ edge cases, which are absorbed by the constant in the asymptotic notation. Hence the same Fejer bound gives

$$
\frac1H
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-d\ell|^2}\right).
$$

Group by

$$
n=d\ell.
$$

The congruence restriction $\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4$ can only reduce the number of representations, so the number of admissible pairs $(d,\ell)$ with $n=d\ell$ is at most $\tau(n)$. Therefore

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}
|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}
\tau(n)
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right)
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proves the shifted second-leg residual bound for both $\rho=1$ and $\rho=3$.

### 5. R5-Full: total residual bound

The Vaaler residual contribution in each long dyadic block is bounded by the sum of three Fejer-product-count expressions:

1. the first leg $K_H(X/d)$, multiplied by $\chi_4(d)$ before absolute values;
2. the second leg shift $K_H((X/d+1)/4)$;
3. the second leg shift $K_H((X/d+3)/4)$.

By H4,

$$
|R_H(t)|\le \frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(t)\ll \frac1H K_H(t).
$$

The zero Fejer mode is already included in $K_H$. If it is separated, it contributes

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}1
\ll \frac{D}{H}
\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

The nonzero positive and negative modes are also included in the single positive kernel $K_H$, so no separate sign-of-frequency argument is needed. If one expands $K_H$, both $k>0$ and $k<0$ are present symmetrically.

For the short blocks $D<X^{1/4}$, do not apply Vaaler. Since $|\psi(t)|\le1/2$,

$$
\sum_{d\le X^{1/4}}|\psi(X/d)|
\ll X^{1/4},
$$

and similarly for the shifted second leg. With a bounded-overlap dyadic partition, the total short-block contribution is $O(X^{1/4})$.

There are $O(\log X)$ long dyadic blocks. Thus R5 on every dyadic block implies

$$
\text{total Vaaler residual}
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This is R5-Full.

### 6. Bridge theorem

**Bridge theorem.** Assume:

- H1--H3: the balanced floor-compatible sawtooth formula;
- H4: finite Vaaler with Fejer residual in the normalization above;
- R5-Full: all Vaaler residuals are $O_\epsilon(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$;
- M9: for every dyadic $D$ with $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$,

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X),\mathcal M_2(D;X)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Then

$$
P(X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Proof. Start from H3. Split the two sums into short dyadic blocks $D<X^{1/4}$ and long dyadic blocks $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$. The short blocks are $O(X^{1/4})$ by $|\psi|\le1/2$. On each long block, apply H4 with $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$. The Vaaler main polynomials give exactly $\mathcal M_1(D;X)$ and $\mathcal M_2(D;X)$, with the actual coefficients $\alpha_{h,H_D}$. The residuals are $O_\epsilon(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$ in total by R5-Full. Summing the M9 estimates over $O(\log X)$ dyadic blocks and absorbing the logarithm into $X^\epsilon$ gives

$$
P(X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This theorem is a reduction, not a solution, because M9 is not proved.

### 7. Li--Yang compatibility map for M9a

Li--Yang’s exponential-sum object is

$$
S
=
\sum_{H\le h\le 2H}g(h/H)
\sum_{M\le m\le 2M}G(m/M)
e\left(\frac{hT}{M}F(m/M)\right),
$$

with $g,G$ of bounded variation and $F\in C^3([1,2])$. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9} The derivative hypotheses include nonvanishing and bounded first, second, and third derivatives and

$$
|F'F'''-3(F'')^2|\ge C_4^{-1}.
$$

:contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

For M9a, split the denominator variable into residue classes

$$
d=4m+r,
\qquad
r\in\{1,3\}.
$$

Let $M\asymp D/4$ and $T=X$. The phase is

$$
e\left(\frac{hX}{4m+r}\right).
$$

This can be written in Li--Yang’s phase form as

$$
e\left(\frac{hT}{M}F_r(m/M)\right),
$$

where

$$
F_r(z)=\frac{M}{4Mz+r}
=
\frac{1}{4z+r/M}.
$$

On compact subintervals $z\asymp1$, this $F_r$ has the required reciprocal-shape derivative structure. In particular,

$$
F_r'F_r'''-3(F_r'')^2
$$

is a nonzero negative multiple of $(4z+r/M)^{-6}$, so the structural nondegeneracy condition is satisfied uniformly for large $M$.

However, structural compatibility is not theorem-level applicability. The M9a block carries the fixed Vaaler coefficient $\alpha_{h,H_D}$. On dyadic frequency subblocks $h\asymp H_0$, one may absorb the smooth factor $H_0\alpha_{h,H_D}$ into a bounded-variation $g(h/H_0)$, leaving a prefactor $1/H_0$. Thus a Li--Yang-type estimate for $S$ would have to imply

$$
S(H_0,D;X)\ll_\epsilon H_0X^{1/4+\epsilon}
$$

to give the M9a endpoint. Li--Yang’s final reduction asks instead for

$$
S/H\lesssim_\epsilon T^{\theta^*+\epsilon},
\qquad
\theta^*=0.3144831759741\ldots,
$$

over a range that includes $1\le H\le MT^{-\theta^*}$ in the circle/divisor reduction. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11} Since $\theta^*>1/4$, this is not the endpoint estimate needed for M9a.

For the raw endpoint block

Li--Yang Case A includes the upper restriction

At $M=X^{1/2}$ this is

which is below $X^{1/4}$. Case B includes

At $M=X^{1/2}$ this gives

### 8. Li--Yang compatibility map for M9b

M9b is not automatically covered by the same dictionary as M9a. The character is in the frequency variable:

$$
\chi_4(h)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

Splitting

$$
h=4q+r,
\qquad
r\in\{1,3\},
$$

gives

$$
\chi_4(4q+r)e((4q+r)X/(4d))
=
\chi_4(r)e((q+r/4)X/d).
$$

Thus M9b requires estimates for phases of the form

$$
e((q+\beta)X/d),
\qquad
\beta\in\left\{\frac14,\frac34\right\}.
$$

This is a fixed fractional frequency shift. Li--Yang’s displayed sum has an integer frequency variable $h$ multiplying $(T/M)F(m/M)$. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13} One cannot simply absorb the fractional shift $\beta$ into the $q$-weight, because

$$
e((q+\beta)X/d)=e(qX/d)e(\beta X/d),
$$

and the extra factor $e(\beta X/d)$ depends on $d$. Equivalently, the phase has a shifted frequency rather than a purely shifted amplitude.

There are two possible ways to handle this, both currently gaps:

1. Prove that the relevant Bombieri--Iwaniec/Li--Yang spacing theorem is stable under fixed fractional frequency shifts $q\mapsto q+\beta$.

2. Rewrite the phase by absorbing $e(\beta X/d)$ into a modified denominator weight and prove that the resulting weight remains inside the permitted smooth/BV class uniformly in the parameters.

The second option is not immediate, because derivatives of $e(\beta X/d)$ in $d$ can be nontrivial on the dyadic scale. Therefore H5b-Shift should remain an explicit theorem-extension problem.

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps

1. **Vaaler convention gap.** The approximation theorem is naturally stated for a centered sawtooth or for $1/2-\{x\}$. The proof draft must explicitly convert it to the floor-compatible $\psi(n)=-1/2$ convention. The integer check above shows the residual majorant covers the discrepancy, but the written proof must not omit this step.

2. **Primary-source normalization gap.** The Vaaler paper metadata is verified, and the formula is checked through Montgomery--Vaughan. For repository rigor, A3 should still compare the coefficient $\Phi$ and the constant $(2H+2)^{-1}$ against the original source or a reliable modern theorem statement.

3. **Dyadic partition gap.** R5-Full assumes a bounded-overlap dyadic partition with $|w_D|\le1$ after taking absolute values. If the repo later uses nonstandard partitions with large derivatives or unbounded weights, R5-Full must be restated.

4. **Rounding of $H_D$.** One must define, for example,

$$
H_D=\max\left(1,\left\lfloor cDX^{-1/4}\right\rfloor\right)
$$

on long blocks and avoid Vaaler on short blocks. All estimates only require $H_D\asymp DX^{-1/4}$.

5. **Small $X$ edge cases.** The proof is asymptotic for $X\ge2$ or $X$ sufficiently large. Finitely many small $X$ values should be absorbed into the implied constant.

6. **Nearest-integer tie cases.** In R5, if $X/d$ or $(X/d+\rho)/4$ is exactly halfway between two integers, either nearest integer works. At most a constant multiplicity is introduced.

7. **Second-leg positivity of $\ell$.** In the shifted proof, $\ell=4m-\rho$ is positive in the main range. If $X$ is very small or if $d$ is outside the intended range, finite exceptions must be handled separately.

8. **M9 coefficient handling.** The coefficient $\alpha_{h,H}$ is not arbitrary. Replacing it with arbitrary $u_h$ gives a stronger stress-test problem, not the actual theorem needed.

9. **Li--Yang weight compatibility.** Even in M9a, one must dyadically decompose the $h$-sum and check that $H_0\alpha_{h,H}$ fits the bounded-variation weight class. This is likely manageable but should be written.

10. **M9b fractional shift.** The frequency-character leg is not automatically reducible to Li--Yang’s integer-frequency theorem. The fixed shift $\beta\in\{1/4,3/4\}$ is a real compatibility gap.

11. **No endpoint theorem.** The Li--Yang record exponent $\theta^*>1/4$ means their final estimate is too weak even where the phase shape matches. This is a theorem-application limitation, not a broad no-go theorem.

12. **Residual cleared does not solve the problem.** R5-Full only controls the Vaaler residual. The main Vaaler sums are still as hard as the conjectural endpoint.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

### Fejer spike obstruction

The main possible obstruction to R5 is the case where many values of $X/d$ are near integers, causing $K_H(X/d)$ to spike. R5 handles this by converting spikes into near-products

$$
md\approx X.
$$

The number of exact or near product coincidences is bounded by the divisor function after grouping by $n=md$. Even if $X$ is an integer with many divisors, the exact spike contribution is at most

$$
\tau(X)\ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon,
$$

well below $X^{1/4+\epsilon}$.

## Verification

The following checks are now proof-draft-ready.

1. **Integer convention check.**

At $t\in\mathbb Z$,

$$
V_H(t)=0,
\qquad
\psi(t)=-\frac12,

K_H(t)=H+1.
$$

Hence

$$
|\psi(t)-V_H(t)|
=
\frac12
=
\frac{K_H(t)}{2H+2}.
$$

2. **Coefficient sign check.**

Montgomery--Vaughan approximates $1/2-\{t\}$ with coefficients

$$
\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}.
$$

Since $\psi(t)=\{t\}-1/2$ off the integers, the coefficient for $\psi$ is

$$
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}.
$$

3. **Second-leg character transfer check.**

For every integer $h$,

$$
e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=2i\chi_4(h).
$$

Indeed, for $h\equiv1\pmod4$ the factor is $2i$, for $h\equiv3\pmod4$ it is $-2i$, and for even $h$ it is $0$.

4. **R5 first-leg divisor grouping.**

The map

d\mapsto n=m(d)d

has multiplicity at most $\tau(n)$ because each representation is a factorization of $n$.

5. **R5 shifted-leg divisor grouping.**

has multiplicity at most $\tau(n)$, with an additional congruence condition on the second factor.

6. **Short-block check.**

Summing dyadically gives $O(X^{1/4})$.

Both are below $X^{1/4}$.

The fractional shift is not a scalar amplitude; it changes the $d$-dependent phase.

### H13 feasibility note: B-process-first for M9a

The proposed object is the spatial-character block

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
\sum_d \chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d).
$$

Split modulo $4$ and apply Poisson summation. Under the convention $e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}$, one expects a formula of the shape

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
\frac{i}{2}
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}\chi_4(n)
\int_{\mathbb R}
w_D(u)e(hX/u-nu/4)\,du,
$$

up to a possible harmless sign depending on the Fourier transform convention. The Gauss factor is

$$
\sum_{r\bmod4}\chi_4(r)e(nr/4)
=
2i\chi_4(n),
$$

again subject to the chosen Poisson sign convention.

For $h>0$, the phase

$$
\phi_n(u)=hX/u-nu/4
$$

has derivative

$$
\phi_n'(u)=-hX/u^2-n/4.
$$

Thus stationary points occur for $n=-m<0$, with

$$
u_0=2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}}.
$$

The dual length is

$$
m\asymp \frac{hX}{D^2}.
$$

At the maximal Vaaler height

$$
h\asymp H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4},
$$

this becomes

$$
m\asymp \frac{X^{3/4}}{D}.
$$

If $D=X^\delta$, with $1/4\le\delta\le1/2$, then

$$
m\asymp X^{3/4-\delta}.
$$

Thus:

- at $D=X^{1/2}$, the largest dual length is $m\asymp X^{1/4}$, comparable to the largest $h$;
- at $D=X^{3/8}$, the largest dual length is $m\asymp X^{3/8}$, larger than $h\asymp X^{1/8}$;
- at $D=X^{1/4}$, the largest dual length is $m\asymp X^{1/2}$ while $h\asymp1$.

So the transform is not uniformly symmetric. It may be useful near the endpoint denominator range $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, but it can lengthen the problem for smaller $D$.

After stationary phase, the dual phase has the form

$$
\Phi(h,m)\asymp \sqrt{hXm},
$$

with constants depending on the modulo-$4$ normalization. Its continuous Hessian is degenerate:

$$
\det\nabla^2\sqrt{hXm}=0.
$$

Therefore H13 cannot be justified by generic full-rank two-dimensional stationary phase or full-rank decoupling. It would need a discrete spacing theorem adapted to the degenerate square-root phase and to the dual $\chi_4(m)$ factor.

What would falsify H13 as a useful route:

1. If the exact Poisson formula shows the $\chi_4$ factor becomes only a diagonal unitary that is erased by the first necessary norm estimate.
2. If the dual length is longer than the primal length in all ranges where M9 is difficult.
3. If the transformed phase reduces to known Hardy--Voronoi sums with no better endpoint technology.
4. If a Cauchy--Schwarz step in the dual variable immediately triggers the same H7 parity collapse without preserving any signed spacing statistic.

What would support H13:

1. A uniform stationary-phase lemma with controlled boundary terms.
2. A dyadic range where both $h$ and $m$ are near $X^{1/4}$.
3. A signed spacing statistic that keeps $\chi_4(m)$ through the first nontrivial matrix estimate.
4. A theorem showing that the fixed Vaaler coefficients $\alpha_{h,H_D}$ interact beneficially with the dual amplitude.
