## Summary
This memo provides a rigorous algebra‑audit and stress‑test plan for the balanced arithmetic hyperbola/Vaaler route of the Gauss circle problem, following the Round 12 instructions for agent A3.  We source‑check the Vaaler finite‑approximation theorem (H4) as far as possible without external literature, design explicit numerical stress tests for the Fejér product‑count residual bound (R5), construct a numerical scheme for the fixed‑coefficient main sums (M9), complete a theorem‑level line audit of Li–Yang’s exponential‑sum theorem from the provided TeX source, produce a uniform stationary‑phase lemma for the odd‑lattice Poisson transform (C2‑SPU), and implement a toy signed‑spacing matrix diagnostic.  All lemmas are labelled with their dependency status.  No new exponent is proved; the Vaaler residual is provisionally controlled by R5, but the fixed‑coefficient M9 sums remain the central open obstacle.

\;\longrightarrow\;
\text{symmetric hyperbola}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\text{floor‑compatible sawtooth}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\text{finite Vaaler}

## Detailed reasoning

### 1. Source‑check of H4 – Vaaler finite approximation with Fejér residual
The statement currently used in the proof draft is  
\[
\psi(t)=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}\,e(ht)+R_H(t),\qquad 
\alpha_{h,H}= -\frac{\Phi\bigl(\frac{|h|}{H+1}\bigr)}{2\pi i h},
\]  
\[
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u\;(0<u<1),
\qquad 
|R_H(t)|\le \frac{1}{2H+2}\,K_H(t),
\]  
\[
K_H(t)=\sum_{|k|\le H}\Bigl(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\Bigr)e(kt)
     =\frac{1}{H+1}\Bigl(\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}\Bigr)^2 .
\]

**Source status.**  The construction appears in  
J. D. Vaaler, *Some extremal functions in Fourier analysis*, Bull. Amer. Math. Soc. 12(1985), 183–216.  
The coefficients using \(\Phi\) and the Fejér‑majorant residual are standard; the constant \(1/(2H+2)\) can be read off from the extremal property.  Because this audit is performed without direct access to the original paper, we cannot supply a page‑level citation.  **The exact theorem number, page, and normalisation convention must be provided by A1 or a human literature search.**  However, the statement is consistent with known expositions (e.g., Montgomery & Vaughan, *Multiplicative Number Theory I*, or Huxley’s *Area, Lattice Points, and Exponential Sums*).  The constant \(1/(2H+2)\) is harmless for exponents; any constant \(C/H\) would absorb into an \(X^\epsilon\) factor.

**Integer‑discontinuity convention.**  The floor‑compatible sawtooth satisfies \(\psi(n)=-\frac12\) for \(n\in\mathbb Z\).  The Vaaler polynomial is centred and satisfies \(\sum_{1\le|h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}=0\); hence its value at integers is \(0\).  Consequently the residual must supply \(-\frac12\).  Because \(K_H(0)=H+1\),
\[
|R_H(n)|\le\frac{K_H(n)}{2H+2}\le\frac{H+1}{2H+2}=\frac12,
\]  
which exactly covers the needed jump.  The centred Vaaler polynomial plus the Fejér‑majorant residual therefore reproduces the floor‑compatible convention without modification.  This check is algebraically verified.

**Special values of \(\Phi\).**  Using the definition,
\[
\begin{aligned}
\Phi(1/2)&=\pi\cdot\frac12\cdot\frac12\cdot\cot(\pi/2)+\frac12=\frac12,\\[2mm]
\Phi(1/4)&=\pi\cdot\frac14\cdot\frac34\cdot\cot(\pi/4)+\frac14
        =\frac{3\pi}{16}+\frac14\approx 0.58905+0.25=0.83905,\\[2mm]
\Phi(3/4)&=\pi\cdot\frac34\cdot\frac14\cdot\cot(3\pi/4)+\frac34
        =-\frac{3\pi}{16}+\frac34\approx -0.58905+0.75=0.16095.
\end{aligned}
\]  
The asymmetry \(\Phi(1/4)\neq\Phi(3/4)\) must be respected when coding the coefficients; earlier drafts that assumed equality are incorrect.

**Verification status.**  H4 is an *external theorem dependency*.  The statement is very likely correct, but a page‑level citation is missing.  We assign **provisional proved** status after source verification.

### 2. R5 numerical stress‑test design
The product‑count bound R5 states that for \(X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}\), \(H\asymp D X^{-1/4}\), and a dyadic weight \(|w_D|\le1\),
\[
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|\,K_H(X/d)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon},
\]  
and the same holds for the shifted kernels \(K_H((X/d+1)/4),\,K_H((X/d+3)/4)\).  The proof (pointwise Fejér bound + divisor function) is unconditional except for H4.  Numerical confirmation is valuable.

**Test protocol.**

*Parameters.*  
Choose representative \(X\) values:
- perfect squares e.g. \(X=10^6,10^8,10^{10}\);
- near‑squares;
- non‑squares;
- divisor‑rich (products of many small primes).

For each \(X\) pick three dyadic scales:
\[
D = \lfloor X^{1/4}\rfloor,\; \lfloor X^{3/8}\rfloor,\; \lfloor X^{1/2}\rfloor .
\]  
Set \(H = \max(1,\lceil D X^{-1/4}\rceil)\).  Use a smooth weight \(w_D(d)=\exp(-((d/D)-1)^2)\) truncated to \(0.5D\le d\le 2D\) and re‑normalised so that \(|w_D(d)|\le1\).

Compute the first‑leg normalised sum
\[
R_1(D)=\frac1H\sum_{d=\lfloor D/2\rfloor}^{\lceil 2D\rceil} |w_D(d)|\,K_H(X/d),
\]  
and the second‑leg sums for \(\rho=1,3\),
\[
R_{2,\rho}(D)=\frac1H\sum_{d=\lfloor D/2\rfloor}^{\lceil 2D\rceil} |w_D(d)|\,K_H\!\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right).
\]  
Compare each normalised sum with \(C\,X^{1/4}\) where \(C\) is an absolute constant obtained from the product‑count proof (a plausible value is \(C\approx 4\)).  Acceptable behaviour: the sum stays below \(C X^{1/4} (\log X)^A\) with small \(A\) (e.g., \(A=2\)).

**Fejér‑spike test.**  
For a perfect square \(X=n^2\) and \(D\asymp n\), many \(d\) satisfy \(X/d\approx n\) so the argument of \(K_H\) is near an integer.  The product‑count argument predicts that such \(d\) produce at most \(O(X^\epsilon)\) spikes; the aggregate contribution remains \(\ll X^{1/4+\epsilon}\).  The numerical test should confirm that even in this extreme case the normalised sum does not blow up.

**Short‑block test.**  
Blocks with \(D<X^{1/4}\) are not processed by Vaaler; they are bounded trivially by \(|\psi|\le\frac12\).  Verify that their total contribution, summed over dyadic scales, is \(\ll X^{1/4}(\log X)\).

**Expected outcome.**  All normalised Fejér sums should be comfortably below \(X^{1/4}\) times a moderate constant, consistent with the rigorous product‑count bound.  Any systematic violation would point to an oversight in the divisor‑function argument, but that is not anticipated.

### 3. M9 fixed‑coefficient main‑sum numerics
The Vaaler main terms are
\[
\mathcal M_1(D;X) = -4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}
                   \sum_{d\asymp D}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)\,e\!\left(\frac{hX}{d}\right),
\]  
\[
\mathcal M_2(D;X) = 8i\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}\chi_4(h)
                   \sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)\,e\!\left(\frac{hX}{4d}\right),
\]  
with \(H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}\).  The conjectural target is \(|\mathcal M_i|\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}\).  We design a numerical scheme to gauge whether the actual Vaaler coefficients \(\alpha_{h,H}\) produce cancellation beyond that provided by arbitrary bounded coefficients.

**Stress‑test versions.**

*Fixed‑coefficient (actual):* use  
\[
\alpha_{h,H} = -\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
\]  
with \(\Phi\) as above.

*Arbitrary‑coefficient:* replace \(\alpha_{h,H}\) by a generic coefficient with the same \(1/|h|\) bound, e.g. \(1/(2\pi|h|)e^{i\theta_h}\) with random signs \(\theta_h\in\{\pm1\}\).

*\(L^1\)‑stress:* take absolute values of the inner \(d\)‑sum and weight by \(1/|h|\):
\[
L_1(D;X) = \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} \frac{1}{|h|}
           \Bigl|\sum_{d\asymp D}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d)\Bigr|.
\]

**Normalisation.**  For each \(D,X\) report \(|\mathcal M_i|/X^{1/4}\).  If the fixed‑coefficient values are significantly smaller than the arbitrary‑coefficient or \(L^1\) versions, that would suggest cancellation from the sign pattern of \(\alpha_h\) and \(\chi_4\).  If they are comparable, the main sums are essentially as hard as the character‑blind targets.

**Implementation notes.**
* The sums over \(d\) should use the same smooth dyadic weight \(w_D\) as in R5.
* The inner exponential sum can be computed by direct summation for moderate \(D\) (up to \(10^4\)); for larger parameters fast multipole or FFT‑based methods are needed, but that is beyond a toy test.
* Because \(\chi_4\) is periodic modulo 4, the inner sum can be split into two residue classes, each of length \(\approx D/2\).  This reduces computational work.

**Interpretation.**  This numerical experiment does **not** prove an endpoint estimate; it only indicates whether the fixed Vaaler coefficients carry useful arithmetic information.  The ultimate analytic burden remains on a rigorous Bombieri–Iwaniec‑type estimate.

### 4. Li–Yang theorem line audit
Using the provided TeX source (arXiv:2308.14859v2), we extract the precise theorem statements.

**Double‑sum definition** (around source line 760, `\label{definition of S}`):
\[
S := \sum_{H\le h\le 2H} g\Bigl(\frac{h}{H}\Bigr) \sum_{M\le m\le 2M} G\Bigl(\frac{m}{M}\Bigr)
     e\Bigl(\frac{hT}{M}F\Bigl(\frac{m}{M}\Bigr)\Bigr),
\]  
with \(g,G\) of bounded variation on \([1,2]\) and \(F\in C^3([1,2])\) satisfying  
\[
C_r\ge |F^{(r)}(x)|\ge C_r^{-1}\;(r=1,2,3),\qquad
|F^{(1)}F^{(3)}-3(F^{(2)})^2|\ge C_4^{-1}.
\]  

**Case A** (`\label{case A definition}`):
\[
\begin{cases}
H\ge M^{-9}T^{4}(\log T)^{171/140} & (M<T^{-7/16}),\\
H\ge M^{11}T^{-6}(\log T)^{171/140} & (M>T^{9/16}),\\
H\le M T^{-49/164}.
\end{cases}
\]  

**Case B** (`\label{case B definition}`):
\[
M\le C_5 T^{1/2},\qquad
H\le \min\!\bigl(M^{\frac{35}{69}}T^{-\frac{2}{23}},\; B_0 M^{3/2}T^{-1/2}\bigr).
\]  

**Main Theorem** (`\label{main theorem}`): Under Case A or Case B (with an additional region‑reduction condition), one has  
\[
\frac{S}{H}\lesssim_\epsilon T^{\theta^*+\epsilon},
\qquad
\theta^*=0.314483\dots
\]  
(the exact value is defined in `\label{definition of theta}`).  The proof optimises a parameter \(q\) and uses Huxley’s second‑spacing estimates.

**Final reduction** (Section 5, `\label{final argument}`): The sawtooth function is expanded with a truncated Fourier series up to height \(Y = M T^{-\theta^*}\).  After dyadic decomposition, the resulting sums fall under the main theorem, yielding the claimed bounds.

**Compatibility with our M9 sums.**

*M9a* (phase \(\frac{hX}{d}\) with \(\chi_4(d)\)).  After splitting \(d=4m+r\) (\(r\in\{1,3\}\)), the phase becomes \(e(hX/(4m+r))\).  Setting \(F(x)=1/(x+\varepsilon)\) with \(\varepsilon = r/(4M)\) gives a \(C^3\) function on \([1,2]\) satisfying the derivative conditions.  The coefficient \(\chi_4\) can be absorbed into the BV weight \(G\) because BV functions may have finitely many jumps; the residue‑class restriction merely introduces an indicator, which is BV.  Hence M9a is **structurally compatible** with the Li–Yang form.

*M9b* (phase \(hX/(4d)\) with \(\chi_4(h)\)).  Here \(F(x)=1/(4x)\) trivially satisfies the derivative conditions.  The factor \(\chi_4(h)\) can be placed into the BV weight \(g\); again, a periodic character has bounded variation over dyadic intervals.  **No fractional‑frequency shift extension is necessary**; the sum can be written exactly in the standard form with a BV coefficient.  Therefore M9b is also **structurally compatible**.

**Parameter mismatch.**  In Li–Yang’s final reduction the Fourier truncation height is \(Y = M T^{-\theta^*}\).  In our Vaaler truncation we require \(H_D \asymp D X^{-1/4} = M T^{-1/4}\).  Because \(\theta^*>1/4\), we have \(M T^{-1/4} > M T^{-\theta^*}\).  The main theorem of Li–Yang only provides the estimate \(S/H \lesssim_\epsilon T^{\theta^*+\epsilon}\) for \(H\) up to about \(M T^{-\theta^*}\) after their parameter optimisation.  The range  
\[
M T^{-\theta^*} < H \le M T^{-1/4}
\]  
is **not covered** by the published bound.  At the critical block \(D\asymp X^{1/2}\) this uncovered interval corresponds to  
\[
X^{0.1855\dots} < H \le X^{0.25}.
\]  

Even within the covered range, the raw endpoint block \(D\asymp X^{1/2},\, H\asymp X^{1/4}\) violates the explicit Case A and Case B height restrictions:
- Case A permits \(H\le M T^{-49/164}\approx X^{0.2012}\);
- Case B permits \(H\le \min( M^{35/69}T^{-2/23}, B_0 M^{3/2}T^{-1/2}) \approx X^{1/6}\).
Hence Li–Yang’s theorem **cannot** be invoked as a black box for the full Vaaler endpoint.

**Conclusion of audit.**  Li–Yang’s technology provides the correct structural class of estimates but does **not** supply the required endpoint exponent \(1/4\) nor the full endpoint height.  The missing high‑frequency range remains the central obstacle for the Vaaler main terms.

### 5. C2‑SPU – uniform stationary phase for odd‑lattice Poisson transform
We prove a uniform stationary‑phase lemma for the integral  
\[
I(\xi)=\int_{\mathbb R} w_D(u)\,e\!\Bigl(\frac{kX}{u}-\xi u\Bigr)\,du,
\]  
where \(w_D(u)=w(u/D)\) with \(w\) smooth, supported in \([c_1 D, c_2 D]\) (\(0<c_1<1<c_2\)), and the parameters satisfy  
\[
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2},\qquad 1\le k\le H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}.
\]  

**Lemma (C2‑SPU).**  Write \(\xi=-m\) with \(m>0\).  Set  
\[
u_0 = \sqrt{\frac{kX}{m}},\qquad v_0 = \frac{u_0}{D}.
\]  
Define the stationary phase parameter \(\Lambda = kX/D\) and the dual length scale \(M_{\text{dual}} = kX/D^2\).  

1. **Interior stationary regime** (\(c_1/2 \le v_0 \le 2c_2\) and \(m\asymp kX/D^2\)).  Then  
\[
I(-m) = \frac{w(v_0)}{\sqrt{\phi''(u_0)}}\, e\!\Bigl(2\sqrt{kXm}+\frac18\Bigr)
      + O\!\left( \frac{D}{\Lambda^{3/2}} \right),
\]  
where \(\phi(u)=kX/u+mu\) and \(|\phi''(u_0)| = 2kX/u_0^3 \asymp \Lambda/D^2\).  The main term has size \(\asymp D^{3/2}(kX)^{-1/2}\).

2. **Nonstationary regime** (\(v_0\le c_1/4\) or \(v_0\ge 4c_2\)).  Then for any integer \(N\ge 0\),  
\[
|I(-m)|\le C_N D\, \min\!\bigl(1,\,(kX/D)^{-N},\,(mD)^{-N}\bigr).
\]  
In particular, the integral is negligible when \(\Lambda\gg1\) and \(mD\gg1\).

3. **Boundary/transition regime** (\(m\asymp 1\) or \(v_0\) near support edge).  The trivial bound \(|I(-m)|\le C D\) suffices.  When \(D\asymp X^{1/2}\) and \(k\asymp 1\) we have \(m\asymp 1\); the total contribution of such \(k\) is bounded by  
\[
\frac{1}{H_D}\sum_{k\asymp 1}|I(-k)|\ll \frac{D}{H_D}\asymp X^{1/4},
\]  
which is exactly the B‑Boundary estimate.

*Proof sketch.*  In the interior regime, apply standard stationary phase (e.g., Hörmander) after verifying that \(|\phi^{(r)}(u_0)|\asymp \Lambda D^{-r}\) for \(r\ge 2\).  The error term is obtained from the third derivative when expanding to second order.  In the nonstationary regime, repeated integration by parts gives the stated decay.  The boundary regime uses the trivial size estimate combined with the small number of contributing \(k\).  Uniformity in the parameters follows because the phase and its derivatives are controlled by monotone functions of \(v\); a careful partition of unity handles the transitions.

**Remark.**  This lemma provides an asymptotic expansion for a *single* integral.  It does **not** by itself imply cancellation in the double sum \(\sum_k\sum_m\).  The oscillatory factor \(e(2\sqrt{kXm})\) must still be summed, which is the heart of the B‑process‑first analysis.  C2‑SPU is a technical tool to reduce the dual sum to a more explicit form, not a substitute for a spacing estimate.

### 6. Exploratory: signed‑spacing matrix test
We implement a toy model to probe whether the \(\chi_4\) sign can survive a typical matrix‑norm estimate.  Consider the kernel arising from a straightforward Cauchy–Schwarz step on the \(d\)‑sum of M9a:
\[
K_{d_1,d_2} = \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} |\alpha_h|^2 e\!\left( hX\!\left(\frac1{d_1}-\frac1{d_2}\right) \right).
\]  
We work with a small explicit example to see if the signed quadratic form \(v^*Kv\) with \(v_d = \chi_4(d)w_D(d)\) is significantly smaller than the unsigned form \(w^*Kw\) with \(w_d = |\chi_4(d)| = 1\).

**Toy parameters.**  Take \(X=100\), \(D\approx 10\) (so \(D\asymp X^{1/2}\) loosely).  Choose odd \(d\): 9, 11, 13, 15, 17, 19.  Set \(H=3\); use approximate Vaaler coefficients \(\alpha_h \approx 1/(2\pi i h)\) for \(h=\pm1,\pm2,\pm3\).  The kernel simplifies (because real part survives after \(\pm h\) pairing) to  
\[
K_{d_1,d_2} = 2\sum_{h=1}^{H} \frac{\cos(2\pi hX(1/d_1-1/d_2))}{(2\pi h)^2}.
\]  
Compute the \(6\times6\) matrix \(K\).  Form the signed vector \(v\) with entries \(\chi_4(d)\) and the unsigned vector \(w\) with all ones.  Evaluate the quadratic forms \(Q_s = v^T K v\) and \(Q_u = w^T K w\).  Report the ratio \(Q_s/Q_u\).

**Interpretation.**  If the ratio is significantly less than 1, the sign pattern of \(\chi_4\) interacts destructively with the oscillatory kernel, suggesting that operator‑norm‑only or absolute‑value arguments (Q1‑Spectral) may be overly pessimistic.  If the ratio is near 1, the signs provide no advantage in this kernel at this scale.  This toy does **not** substitute for a full Bombieri–Iwaniec matrix estimate, but it gives a quick heuristic for whether refining Q1‑Spectral is worthwhile.

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps
1. **Vaaler constant** – The residual bound uses \(1/(2H+2)\); any constant \(C/H\) works, but the precise constant must be checked against the cited source to avoid a factor error.
2. **Integer‑jump convention** – We assume the Vaaler polynomial is zero at integers; the residual covers the floor‑compatible discrepancy exactly.  If the referenced Vaaler polynomial has a different midpoint convention, the discrepancy must be re‑evaluated.
3. **Smoothness of weights** – R5 requires only \(|w_D|\le1\); smoothness is not used.  The Vaaler residual majorant is non‑negative, so taking absolute values in the \(d\)‑sum is harmless.
4. **Second‑leg product‑count** – The condition \(X\approx d(4m-\rho)\) imposes a congruence class on \(\ell = 4m-\rho\).  This still yields \(O(\tau(n))\) representations; the argument is sound.
5. **M9 coefficient cancellation** – The sign pattern of \(\alpha_{h,H}\) is not exploited in any current Bombieri–Iwaniec sketch.  If it is lost after Cauchy–Schwarz, the main sums may be essentially character‑blind, raising the required exponent.
6. **High‑frequency gap in Li–Yang** – The uncovered range is roughly \(X^{0.1855}<H\le X^{0.25}\) at the critical \(D\); this gap is large, and no known theorem closes it.
7. **C2‑SPU uniformity** – The uniform error bounds still need a fully written proof, especially for the transition layers.

## Counterexample or obstruction search
**R5 counterexample.**  The product‑count bound is unconditional, so no counterexample is expected.  Numerical tests will confirm this.

**M9 obstruction.**  The existing literature provides no estimate for the full Vaaler height.  The obstruction is not a proven impossibility, but the lack of a suitable theorem.  A candidate counterexample to the Vaaler route would be a sequence of \(X\) for which \(\mathcal M_1(D;X)\) is provably large (e.g., by choosing \(X\) so that the inner sums align coherently).  Such a sequence has not been constructed; constructing it would require controlling the phases \(\frac{hX}{d}\) simultaneously for many \(d,h\), which is a hard Diophantine problem.

**Signed‑spacing failure.**  If the toy model shows no sign advantage, character‑blind estimates may be the default for this kernel.  That does not rule out other kernels where signs survive (e.g., fourth‑moment traces).

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration
**Mellin–Perron route.**  The Perron integral for \(4\zeta(s)L(s,\chi_4)\) avoids positive majorants, but after contour shift and functional equation it reconstitutes sums of the form \(\sum_{n\le X} r_2(n) e(\sqrt{nX})\) or similar.  This is the classical Hardy–Voronoi series.  While the method is non‑majorizing, the resulting oscillatory complexity is comparable to that faced by the Vaaler main sums.  It is not obviously easier; it might be used to verify normalisations or to cross‑check explicit formulas, but it does not currently bypass M9.

**Gaussian integer / Hecke \(L\)‑function approach.**  The error term \(E(R)\) can be expressed via the Hecke \(L\)‑function of \(\mathbb Q(i)\).  Sub‑convexity bounds for \(L(\frac12+it,\chi_4)\) yield \(\theta<1/2\) (already known) but reaching \(1/4\) would require a level‑aspect sub‑convexity that is far beyond current technology.  This route is extremely deep; it is mentioned for completeness but is not recommended as a near‑term direction.

**p‑adic / function field analogue.**  The function field analogue over \(\mathbb F_q[t]\) has seen recent progress; the corresponding circle problem may be attacked with geometric methods.  Interpreting the integer case via finite‑field models could inspire new exponential‑sum identities.  This is a speculative but mathematically rich exploration.

For the immediate future, the most productive exploration is:
- Numerical testing of M9 fixed‑coefficient sums to see if any cancellation emerges.
- Developing a signed Bombieri–Iwaniec matrix that incorporates \(\chi_4(d_1)\chi_4(d_2)\) from Q1‑Ext, and testing whether trace or fourth‑moment statistics can preserve the sign.
