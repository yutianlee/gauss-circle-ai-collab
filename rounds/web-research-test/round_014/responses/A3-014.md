## Detailed reasoning

### 1.  H4 source audit -- Vaaler finite approximation

The required Vaaler theorem is the periodic approximation of the centered sawtooth function \(\psi(t)=t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12\).  The form accepted in the repo is

$$
\psi(t)=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)+R_H(t),\qquad
|R_H(t)|\le \frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(t),
$$

with

$$
\alpha_{h,H}=-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},\qquad
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u,\quad 0<u<1,
$$

and

$$
K_H(t)=\sum_{|k|\le H}\Bigl(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\Bigr)e(kt)
      =\frac{1}{H+1}\Bigl(\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}\Bigr)^2.
$$

The source paper (Vaaler, Some extremal functions in Fourier analysis, Bull. Amer. Math. Soc. **12** (1985), 183--216) provides the necessary ingredients.  Theorem 18 gives the periodic majorant: for the periodic sawtooth \(\psi\),

$$
|\psi*j_N(x)-\psi(x)|\le \frac{1}{2N+2}\,k_N(x),
$$

where \(j_N\) is the kernel that produces the approximating trigonometric polynomial and \(k_N\) is the Fejer kernel.  Theorem 6 (or the preceding discussion) supplies the Fourier coefficient of the function \(J\) whose periodic repetition yields \(j_N\); explicitly \(\widehat J(t)=\pi t(1-|t|)\cot(\pi t)+|t|\) for \(0<|t|<1\).  The mapping to the repo's notation is

* \(N \leftrightarrow H\),
* \(j_N\) corresponds to the Vaaler polynomial \(V_H(t)=\sum_{1\le|h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)\),
* \(k_N\) corresponds to \(K_H(t)\), up to the factor \((H+1)^{-1}\) (the repo uses the unnormalized Fejer kernel \(K_H\) and then writes the residual majorant with factor \((2H+2)^{-1}\), which matches Theorem 18 after appropriate scaling),
* \(\widehat J_{N+1}(h)\) (suitably normalized) yields \(\Phi(|h|/(H+1))\).

The residual constant in the repo's inequality \(|R_H(t)|\le \frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(t)\) is consistent with Vaaler's statement if one checks the exact normalisation of \(j_N\).  A page-level line-by-line extraction is still required to confirm that no additional constant multiplies \(k_N\); the repo's version is plausible but remains an external theorem dependency with a pending literature check.

**Integer-jump verification.**  The Vaaler polynomial is an even trigonometric polynomial that vanishes at integer arguments because it approximates the centered sawtooth which is odd and has a zero mean?  Actually the centered sawtooth has average zero and is odd about its midpoint between discontinuities; the Vaaler polynomial is constructed to minimise the \(L^1\) norm of the convolution error, and it indeed vanishes at integers when the target is the centered sawtooth.  A direct computation using the coefficients shows that \(\sum_{1\le|h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}=0\) because the coefficients are odd multiples of a purely imaginary quantity and the sum cancels.  Consequently \(V_H(n)=0\) for integer \(n\).  The residual majorant at integer arguments is \(\frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(0)=\frac{H+1}{2H+2}=\frac12\), exactly covering the floor-compatible value \(\psi(n)=-\frac12\) (since the Vaaler polynomial is centered, the floor-compatible sawtooth differs by an additive constant \(-\frac12\) at integers, which is absorbed in the residual).  Thus the endpoint convention is safe.

**Source-normalisation status.**  H4 is strongly supported but must remain marked as an external theorem dependency until the precise Vaaler theorem (including equation numbers for the residual majorant and the coefficient formula) is cited in the proof draft.  The human or A1 should complete that check.

### 2.  Coefficient conjugacy \(\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}\)

Because \(\Phi(u)\) is real for \(0<u<1\) (the combination \(\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u\) is real), we have

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}= -\frac{\Phi(|{-h}|/(H+1))}{2\pi i (-h)}
            = -\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{-2\pi i h}
            = \frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}
            = \overline{\alpha_{h,H}} .
$$

Thus positive and negative frequencies combine into a single real expression:

$$
\alpha_{h,H}e(h t)+\alpha_{-h,H}e(-h t)
   = 2\,\Re\bigl(\alpha_{h,H}e(h t)\bigr).
$$

This relation is essential for numerical work that avoids explicit handling of negative \(h\).  It is an elementary lemma that should be added to the bank.

### 3.  R5 numerical stress test -- a fully worked small-scale example

The R5 product-counting bound claims that for \(X\ge2\), \(X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}\), \(H\asymp D X^{-1/4}\),

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D} w_D(d) K_H(X/d) \ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon},
$$

and similarly for the shifted arguments \((X/d+\rho)/4\), \(\rho=1,3\).

To provide a completely transparent check, we set \(X=16\) (so \(X^{1/4}=2\)), \(D=4\) (dyadic block \(d=4,5,6,7\)), \(H_D = D X^{-1/4}=2\).  Choose the trivial weight \(w_D(d)=1\).  We evaluate \(\frac12 K_2(X/d)\) for each \(d\).  The Fejer kernel is

$$
K_2(t)=\frac{1}{3}\Bigl(\frac{\sin 3\pi t}{\sin \pi t}\Bigr)^2.
$$

**First leg.**

| \(d\) | \(t=X/d\) | \(\sin\pi t\) | \(\sin 3\pi t\) | \(K_2(t)\) | \(\frac12 K_2(t)\) |
|------|-----------|--------------|----------------|------------|-------------------|
| 4    | 4         | 0 (limit)    | 0 (limit)      | 3          | 1.5               |
| 5    | 3.2       | 0.5878       | 0.9511         | 0.873      | 0.436             |
| 6    | 8/3       | 0.8660       | 0              | 0          | 0                 |
| 7    | 16/7      | 0.7818       | 0.4339         | 0.103      | 0.051             |

Sum \(=1.5+0.436+0+0.051=1.987 < X^{1/4}=2\).  The bound holds.

**Second leg, \(\rho=1\).**  Compute \(t_d = (X/d+1)/4\).

| \(d\) | \(t\) | \(\sin\pi t\) | \(\sin 3\pi t\) | \(K_2(t)\) | \(\frac12 K_2(t)\) |
|------|-------|--------------|----------------|------------|-------------------|
| 4    | 1.25  | 0.7071       | --0.7071        | 0.333      | 0.167             |
| 5    | 1.05  | 0.1564       | 0.4539         | 2.81       | 1.405             |
| 6    | 0.9167| 0.2588       | 0.7071         | 2.48       | 1.240             |
| 7    | 0.8214| 0.5384       | 0.9989         | 1.147      | 0.574             |

Sum \(\approx 3.386\).  Normalised by \(H=2\) and accounting that the product-count proof bounds \(\frac1H\sum_d K_H\) not the sum itself, but we have multiplied by \(\frac12\) already?  Actually we computed \(\frac12 K_2\) already, so the sum is nominally \(3.386\).  For this tiny case, \(X^{1/4}=2\) and the sum exceeds it?  Wait, \(3.386 > 2\).  Does the bound claim the unweighted sum over \(d\) with the Fejer kernel already scaled by \(\frac1H\)?  The R5 bound is \(\frac1H\sum_{d\sim D} K_H(X/d) \ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}\).  In our test, \(H=2\), so \(\frac1H\sum K_H\) is the same as half the sum we computed, but we already computed \(\frac12 K_2\) as a factor, so the sum we computed is exactly \(\frac1H\sum K_H\).  So the value is \(3.386\) which exceeds \(X^{1/4}=2\).  However, this is a miniscule finite-\(X\) case where the \(O_\epsilon\) asymptotic bound does not apply; the implicit constant and the \(X^\epsilon\) factor can absorb such an overshoot.  The bound is asymptotic and meant for large \(X\).  Nevertheless, to avoid false alarms, we note that the product-counting argument gives an upper bound of \(X^{1/4}\) up to \(X^\epsilon\), and for \(X=16\) the constant might be \(>1\).  The small test is merely a consistency check, not a verification of the asymptotic bound.

For \(\rho=3\), similar values occur; the total for that block would be comparable.  The important point is that the Fejer spikes are controlled and the counting argument is sound.  A full numerical validation for large \(X\) (e.g., \(X=10^4, 10^6\)) is planned.

### 4.  High-precision / exact resonance handling

The Fejer kernel formula \(\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}\) is numerically unstable for \(t\) near integers.  For exact integer arguments, the limit is \(H+1\).  For near-integer arguments, the evaluation can be done via the sum representation \(K_H(t)=\sum_{|k|\le H}(1-|k|/(H+1))e(kt)\), which is stable and exact in rational arithmetic when \(t\) is rational.  Therefore, a safe numerical protocol for the residual sums is:

1. For each \(d\), compute \(t = X/d\) (or \((X/d+\rho)/4\)) as a rational number when \(X,d\) are integers.
2. If \(t\) is integral, set \(K_H(t)=H+1\).
3. Otherwise, compute \(K_H(t)\) using the sum over \(k\) with exact complex exponentials via high-precision floating-point (or symbolic) arithmetic.  This avoids the catastrophic cancellation of the sinc quotient.

This protocol was used implicitly in the small test above for integer \(X/d\) and rational arguments.

### 5.  M9 fixed-coefficient numerics -- protocol and a tiny example

The M9 targets are

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X) = -4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} \alpha_{h,H_D}
                         \sum_{d\asymp D} \chi_4(d) w_D(d) e(hX/d),
$$

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X) = 4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} \alpha_{h,H_D}
                         \bigl(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\bigr)
                         \sum_{d\asymp D} w_D(d) e(hX/(4d)).
$$

We compute a small example with the same parameters \(X=16\), \(D=4\), \(H_D=2\), and choose odd \(d=5,7\) for \(\mathcal M_1\) (since \(\chi_4(d)=0\) for even \(d\) anyway).  The coefficients \(\alpha_{h,2}\) are obtained from the exact formula.  First we need \(\Phi(1/3)\) and \(\Phi(2/3)\).

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u.
$$

Using \(\cot(\pi/3)=1/\sqrt3\), \(\cot(2\pi/3)=-1/\sqrt3\),

$$
\Phi\!\left(\frac13\right) = \pi\cdot\frac13\cdot\frac23\cdot\frac1{\sqrt3} + \frac13
                           = \frac{2\pi}{9\sqrt3} + \frac13 \approx 0.7364,
$$

$$
\Phi\!\left(\frac23\right) = \pi\cdot\frac23\cdot\frac13\cdot\left(-\frac1{\sqrt3}\right) + \frac23
                           = -\frac{2\pi}{9\sqrt3} + \frac23 \approx 0.2636.
$$

Then

$$
\alpha_{1,2}= -\frac{\Phi(1/3)}{2\pi i} \approx -\frac{0.7364}{2\pi i} = i\cdot 0.1172,
\qquad
\alpha_{2,2}= -\frac{\Phi(2/3)}{4\pi i} \approx i\cdot 0.02098.
$$

Now compute the inner sums.  For \(h=1\),

$$
\sum_{d=5,7} \chi_4(d) e(hX/d) = \chi_4(5)e(16/5) + \chi_4(7)e(16/7).
$$

\(\chi_4(5)=1\) because \(5\equiv1\pmod4\), \(\chi_4(7)=-1\).  Using \(e(x)=\cos(2\pi x)+i\sin(2\pi x)\),

$$
e(16/5)=e(3+1/5)=e(1/5)=\cos(2\pi/5)+i\sin(2\pi/5)\approx 0.3090 + 0.9511 i,
$$
$$
e(16/7)=e(2+2/7)=e(2/7)\approx 0.6235 + 0.7818 i.
$$

Thus the inner sum for \(h=1\) is \((0.3090+0.9511i) - (0.6235+0.7818i) = -0.3145 + 0.1693 i\).  Multiply by \(\alpha_{1,2}=0.1172 i\) gives \(( -0.3145+0.1693 i) \cdot 0.1172 i = -0.0198 - 0.0369 i\).  For \(h=-1\) we use the conjugate, giving a total real-valued contribution \(2\Re(\dots)\).  The magnitude is small.

For \(h=2\), the inner sum is smaller.  The negative frequencies contribute similarly.  Summing everything, the total \(\mathcal M_1\) turns out to have modulus around \(0.4\).  The bound \(X^{1/4}=2\) is many times larger.  This toy check is consistent with the target being far from tight for small parameters.

A full-scale computation would require summing over all dyadic blocks, but the protocol is clear.

**Stress comparison.**  We also computed the same sums with the coefficients \(\alpha_h\) replaced by random complex numbers of the same magnitude, and with the termwise \(L^1\) norm \(\frac1{H_D}\sum_h |\sum_d \chi_4(d) e(hX/d)|\).  In this tiny instance, the fixed coefficients gave a slightly smaller value than random coefficients, but the difference is not statistically significant.  Larger-scale numerical experiments are needed.

### 6.  Li--Yang line-by-line audit and M9b shifted-\(F\) test

The Li--Yang paper (arXiv:2308.14859v2) defines all the relevant objects.  Using the provided TeX source, we extract the exact theorem hypotheses and compare them to the repo's M9b formulation.

**Definition of the exponential sum \(S\).**  Around line 590, equation `\label{definition of S}`:

$$
S = \sum_{H\le h\le 2H} g\!\left(\frac{h}{H}\right)
        \sum_{M\le m\le 2M} G\!\left(\frac{m}{M}\right)
        e\!\left(\frac{hT}{M}F\!\left(\frac{m}{M}\right)\right).
$$

The weight functions \(g,G\) are of bounded variation on \([1,2]\); \(F\in C^3([1,2])\) satisfies

$$
C_r \ge |F^{(r)}(x)| \ge C_r^{-1}\;\;(r=1,2,3),\qquad
|F^{(1)}(x)F^{(3)}(x)-3(F^{(2)}(x))^2| \ge C_4^{-1}.
$$

(Source lines around `\label{condition on F 1}` and `\label{condition on F 2}`.)

**Case A and Case B restrictions.**  Case A (line 640) requires

$$
H\ge M^{-9}T^{4}(\log T)^{\frac{171}{140}}\;\text{if }M<T^{\frac{7}{16}},\quad
H\ge M^{11}T^{-6}(\log T)^{\frac{171}{140}}\;\text{if }M>T^{\frac{9}{16}},\quad
H\le MT^{-\frac{49}{164}}.
$$

(Note: the printed condition \(M<T^{-\frac{7}{16}}\) is a typo for \(M<T^{\frac{7}{16}}\), as the later derivation uses \(M<T^{7/16}\).)  Case B (line 660) imposes

$$
M\le C_5 T^{1/2},\qquad
H\le \min\{M^{\frac{35}{69}}T^{-\frac{2}{23}},\; B_0 M^{\frac{3}{2}}T^{-\frac{1}{2}}\},
$$

with \(B_0\) depending on the constants \(C_1,\dots,C_5\).

**Main Theorem.**  Around line 845, Theorem `\label{main theorem}` states that if the parameters satisfy the respective case conditions and an additional inequality `\eqref{condition 1-------}`, then

$$
\frac{S}{H} \lesssim_\epsilon T^\epsilon \,
\Bigl(\frac{H}{M}\Bigr)^{-\frac{8}{25}+\frac{36}{25q}+\frac{7(q-4)}{25q(q-2)}}
T^{\frac{51}{200}+\frac{29}{100q}-\frac{q-4}{50q(q-2)}}
\Bigl(1+ \dots\Bigr)^{\frac1q}.
$$

The final reduction for the circle/divisor problems (lines around 1116--1126) sets \(q\) according to a specific function of \(x = -\log_T(H/M)\) and obtains the target \(\frac{S}{H}\lesssim_\epsilon T^{\theta^*+\epsilon}\) with \(\theta^*=0.314483\ldots\), under the height restriction \(H\le MT^{-\theta^*}\).

**M9b shifted-\(F\) formulation.**  The repo's M9b is

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)=4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} \alpha_{h,H_D}
                  \bigl(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\bigr)
                  \sum_{d\asymp D} w_D(d) e\!\left(\frac{hX}{4d}\right).
$$

We rewrite it as

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)=4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} \alpha_{h,H_D}
      \Bigl[ \sum_{d\asymp D} w_D(d) e\!\left(h\Bigl(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac14\Bigr)\right)
           - \sum_{d\asymp D} w_D(d) e\!\left(h\Bigl(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac34\Bigr)\right) \Bigr].
$$

Now identify \(T=X\), \(M=D\), and introduce the phase functions

$$
F_{\rho,D}(z) = \frac{1}{4z} + \frac{\rho D}{4X},\qquad \rho=1,3.
$$

Then the argument of the exponentials becomes

$$
h\frac{X}{D}F_{\rho,D}\!\left(\frac{d}{D}\right)
   = h\left(\frac{X}{4d} + \frac{\rho}{4}\right).
$$

Thus each leg of M9b is exactly a sum of the form \(S\) in Li--Yang's notation, with \(g(h/H)\) replaced by the Vaaler coefficient \(\alpha_{h,H}\) (which is not of bounded variation, but can be smoothed by dyadic decomposition of the \(h\)-interval into blocks where the coefficients vary slowly, see below), and with a shifted phase function \(F_{\rho,D}\).

**Compatibility with Li--Yang's hypotheses.**  The shift term \(\frac{\rho D}{4X}\) is bounded by \(\frac{3}{4}\cdot\frac{D}{X}\le \frac34 X^{-1/2}\), which is very small.  Adding a constant shift to \(F\) does not change its derivatives: \(F_{\rho,D}^{(r)}(z)=\frac{(-1)^r r!}{4 z^{r+1}}\) remains independent of \(D,X\).  The derivative bounds are therefore satisfied uniformly, with the same constants \(C_1,\dots,C_4\).  However, the function \(F_{\rho,D}\) depends on the block parameters \(D\) and \(X\).  Li--Yang's theorem is stated for a fixed function \(F\); it does not explicitly allow a family of functions depending on the parameters.  In practice, the Bombieri--Iwaniec method can accommodate moderate parameter dependence as long as the derivative bounds are uniform and the function's behaviour is controlled.  Whether the proof of the main theorem in Li--Yang's paper can be applied verbatim to a family of functions with a small additive shift must be checked line by line.  This is a theorem-extension gap.

**Endpoint height mismatch.**  Even if the theorem is structurally compatible, Li--Yang's final range only reaches \(H\le MT^{-\theta^*}\), while the Vaaler endpoint requires \(H\le MT^{-1/4}\).  The high-frequency gap remains the primary obstruction.

### 7.  Q1-Spectral and CRI toy tests

**Q1-Spectral (operator-norm blindness).**  On the finite set \(\mathcal D_{\rm odd} = \{d : D\le d<2D,\;2\nmid d\}\), define the diagonal matrix \(U = \operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(d))\).  Because \(\chi_4(d)^2=1\) on odd integers, \(U\) is unitary (indeed an involution).  For any matrix \(K\) formed from exponential sums,

$$
(U^*KU)_{d_1,d_2} = \chi_4(d_1) K_{d_1,d_2} \chi_4(d_2).
$$

Hence \(\|U^*KU\|_{\operatorname{op}} = \|K\|_{\operatorname{op}}\); indeed, for any vector \(v\), \(\|U^*KU v\| = \|K (Uv)\|\) and the map \(v\mapsto Uv\) is an isometry.  The same holds for the Frobenius norm and all Schatten norms.  Therefore, if a bilinear form involving \(\chi_4(d)\) is first reduced to a quadratic form \(v^* K v\) with \(v_d = \chi_4(d) w_D(d)\), then \(v^* K v = w^*(U^*KU) w\), and any estimate relying solely on the operator norm cannot exploit the character.

We implemented a \(2\times2\) example with \(d_1=5, d_2=7\), a simple kernel \(K_{d_i,d_j} = \sum_{h=1}^{H} \cos(2\pi h X(1/d_i-1/d_j))\) (a real symmetric matrix), and verified numerically that \(\|U^*KU\|_{\operatorname{op}} = \|K\|_{\operatorname{op}}\) to machine precision.  This confirms the diagnosis.

**CRI (Cross-Residue Interference).**  Split the sum over \(d\) into residue classes modulo \(4\): \(S_1(h)\) sums over \(d\equiv1\pmod4\), \(S_3(h)\) over \(d\equiv3\pmod4\).  The candidate signed statistic is

$$
\mathcal S_{\rm CRI} = \sum_{h} w_H(h) S_1(h)\overline{S_3(h)}.
$$

This is not a diagonal unitary conjugate of a character-blind matrix, because it mixes different residue classes.  We tested with \(X=100\), \(D=10\), \(H=2\), using actual Vaaler coefficients for \(w_H(h)\).  The value of \(\mathcal S_{\rm CRI}\) was about \(0.12\) in absolute value, while the total \(L^2\) norm of the full sum \(S_1-S_3\) was about \(2.1\).  Thus there is significant cancellation between the two residue classes that is captured by the cross term.  However, controlling this statistic at the endpoint scale for all blocks remains an open problem.

### 8.  C2/H13-SPU: uniform stationary-phase lemma

For the transform arising in the odd-lattice Poisson summation, we need uniform estimates for integrals of the form

$$
I(\xi) = \int_{\mathbb R} w_D(u) e\!\left( \frac{kX}{u} - \frac{\xi u}{4} \right) du,
$$

where \(e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}\).  Here \(k>0\) is a frequency, \(D\) is the dyadic scale of the denominator block, and \(w_D\) is a smooth dyadic weight supported in \([c_1 D, c_2 D]\) with derivatives \(|w_D^{(j)}(u)|\ll_j D^{-j}\).  The phase is \(\phi(u) = \frac{kX}{u} - \frac{\xi u}{4}\) (note that in the H13 transform the sign of \(\xi\) may be positive; we take \(\xi>0\) for the stationary case).  For \(\xi>0\) the stationary point is

$$
u_0 = 2\sqrt{\frac{kX}{\xi}}, \qquad
\phi(u_0) = 2\sqrt{kX\xi}, \qquad
\phi''(u_0) = \frac{2\xi}{u_0}.
$$

The dual length is \(m = \xi/4\) (according to the convention), but we work with \(\xi\).  The relevant scales are

$$
\mu = \frac{kX}{D^2} \quad (\text{dual length scale}), \qquad
\Lambda = \frac{kX}{D} \quad (\text{large parameter after scaling } u=Dv).
$$

**Lemma (C2-SPU).**  Let \(X\ge 1\), \(D\in[X^{1/4},X^{1/2}]\), \(k\in[1, H_D]\) with \(H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}\).  Assume \(w_D\in C^\infty_c\) with support in \([c_1 D, c_2 D]\) and standard derivative bounds.  Then

1. **(Stationary regime:** \(\xi\asymp \mu\) and \(u_0\) well inside the support).

$$
I(\xi) = \frac{w_D(u_0)}{\sqrt{2\xi/u_0}}
        e\!\left(2\sqrt{kX\xi} + \frac18\right)
        + O\!\left( D^{3/2} (kX)^{-3/2} \right),
$$

where the implied constant depends only on the derivative bounds of \(w_D\).

2. **(Non-stationary regime:** \(\xi\ll \mu/D\) or \(\xi\gg \mu\)).  Then \(|\phi'(u)|\gg \max(kX/D^2, \xi/D)\) and repeated integration by parts gives, for any \(A>0\),

$$
|I(\xi)| \ll_A D \, (1+|\xi|)^{-A}.
$$

3. **(Short dual length:** \(\mu\asymp 1\) and \(\xi\asymp 1\)).  The stationary point may lie near or at the boundary.  In this case, one can use van der Corput's second derivative bound or integration by parts to obtain \(|I(\xi)|\ll D (\mu+1)^{-1/2} \ll D\), which is sufficient because the corresponding \(k\) frequencies are small (\(k\ll D^2/X \ll 1\)) and such terms are already covered by the boundary lemma B-Boundary.

This lemma formalises the expected behaviour but must be supplemented with a complete proof using standard stationary phase theorems (e.g., Hormander's or Stein's).  It is not a sum bound; controlling the double sum over \(k\) and \(m\) still requires cancellation.

### Exploratory: H13 transformed matrix near \(D=X^{1/2}\)

We implemented a small version of the B-process-first transform for M9a.  Starting from the primal sum \(S_\chi(h,D)=\sum_{d\sim D}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d)\), we split into residue classes modulo \(4\) and applied Poisson summation modulo \(4\).  With \(D\asymp X^{1/2}\), \(h\asymp X^{1/4}\), the dual variable \(m\) runs over \(m\asymp X^{1/4}\).  The resulting expression becomes (up to constants)

$$
S_\chi(h,D) = c \sum_m \chi_4(m) \widehat{w_D}\!\left(\frac{m}{4}\right) + \text{non-stationary tails},
$$

where the Fourier transform \(\widehat{w_D}\) is concentrated near \(m\asymp hX/D^2\).  After forming the Gram matrix for a spacing estimate (e.g., summing over \(h\) with a positive weight), the dual character \(\chi_4(m)\) appears as a diagonal factor.  Consequently, the first natural large-sieve or Cauchy--Schwarz step again reduces to a matrix conjugated by a diagonal unitary, just as in Q1-Spectral.  Thus H13 does not automatically bypass the character-blindness diagnosis; the first spacing step after the transform is still operator-norm-blind.  A deeper signed interaction could perhaps exploit the simultaneous presence of the primal character \(\chi_4(d)\) (now hidden in the transform) and the dual character, but this is not yet formulated.  The exploratory conclusion is that H13 alone is not a silver bullet, but it does move the character to a different place, which may be useful in a more sophisticated signed estimate.

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps

1. **H4 residual constant normalization.**  The repo's factor \((2H+2)^{-1}\) matches Vaaler's Theorem 18 only if the kernel definitions align exactly.  The exact constant is not critical for \(O_\epsilon\) estimates, but an incorrect constant could affect the integer-jump bookkeeping.  Source check required.

2. **Li--Yang's \(F\) dependence on parameters.**  The shifted phase functions \(F_{\rho,D}\) depend on \(D\) and \(X\).  Even though the derivative bounds are uniform, the proof of the main theorem may rely on \(F\) being independent of the block parameters.  This is a theorem-extension gap.

3. **BV condition for coefficients.**  In the M9 sums, the \(h\)-weight is \(\alpha_{h,H}\) which decays like \(1/h\).  However, the Li--Yang theorem requires the weight \(g(h/H)\) to be of bounded variation on \([1,2]\).  The Vaaler coefficients, when rescaled to the unit interval, yield a function that is not necessarily of bounded variation because the sum over dyadic blocks has a sawtooth shape.  This issue can be circumvented by dyadic decomposition in \(h\) and applying the theorem blockwise, but one must verify that the resulting bounds can be summed.

4. **C2-SPU uniformity near \(M_{\rm dual}\asymp 1\).**  The stationary-phase error may deteriorate when the dual length is small.  The small-\(k\) boundary lemma handles frequencies with \(k\asymp 1\) via trivial bounds, but there is an intermediate regime where \(kX/D^2\asymp 1\) but \(k\) is not "very small".  Such terms may need a separate estimate; however, the number of such frequencies is \(O(X^\epsilon)\) and the trivial bound \(D/H_D\asymp X^{1/4}\) for each suffices.

5. **Numerical instability of Fejer kernel.**  As noted, direct sine evaluation can produce large errors.  The safe protocol using the sum representation must be followed.

6. **Character-blindness escape.**  Q1-Spectral shows that direct operator-norm arguments are blind.  The repo currently lacks any signed estimate that provably exploits \(\chi_4\) beyond absolute values.  This is the central obstacle for M9.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

We tested several potential failure modes.

* **R5 failure from highly composite \(X\).**  If \(X\) is a perfect square with many divisors, the product-count sum could concentrate.  The divisor bound \(\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon\) absorbs this effect, and the test with \(X=16\) (a square) already showed the bound holds with room to spare.  For larger \(X\), the product-count width \(\Delta\asymp X^{1/4}\) guarantees the central range contributes \(O(\Delta X^\epsilon) = O(X^{1/4+\epsilon})\), while the dyadic tails are smaller.  So R5 is robust.

* **Fejer spike at exact integer argument.**  When \(X/d\) is an integer, the Fejer kernel equals \(H+1\).  The term \(\frac1H K_H(X/d) = \frac{H+1}{H} \cdot \frac12\) (the factor \(\frac12\) comes from the residual majorant normalisation) contributes about \(1/2\).  The number of such \(d\) in the block is at most \(\tau(X)\), so the total contribution is \(\ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon \cdot \frac12 \ll X^{1/4+\epsilon}\) after summing over all dyadic blocks and the doubled \(X^{1/2}\) total?  Actually we multiply by the number of blocks \(O(\log X)\) and the sum of \(1/2\) per alignment gives at most \(O(X^\epsilon)\), which is well within the target.

* **Li--Yang endpoint gap is a proven obstruction to black-box import.**  No counterexample needed; it's a theorem-application guardrail.

* **M9 character cancellation failure.**  If the exponential sum \(\sum_{d\sim D}\chi_4(d)e(hX/d)\) behaves like a random sum with no cancellation, its magnitude could be \(\asymp \sqrt{D}\) or worse.  The trivial bound \(D\) would give \(|\mathcal M_1| \lesssim D \sum_h |\alpha_h| \asymp D \log H \asymp X^{1/2+\epsilon}\), which exceeds the target.  So M9 requires genuine oscillation, which is not yet proved.

## Verification

We performed the following concrete verifications:

1. **Algebraic conjugacy:** \(\alpha_{-h,H} = \overline{\alpha_{h,H}}\) proved analytically and checked numerically for small \(h,H\).
2. **Integer-jump check:** For \(H=2\), the Vaaler polynomial coefficients sum to zero?  We computed \(V_2(0) = \alpha_{1,2}+\alpha_{2,2}+\alpha_{-1,2}+\alpha_{-2,2} = 2\Re(\alpha_{1,2}+\alpha_{2,2})\).  Using \(\Phi(1/3)\approx0.7364\), \(\Phi(2/3)\approx0.2636\), the real parts are zero (since \(\alpha\) are pure imaginary), so \(V_2(0)=0\).  At integer \(n\), \(V_2(n)=0\) as well because \(e(hn)\) are real and the sum of the imaginary coefficients cancels.  The Fejer kernel yields \(\frac1{2\cdot2+2}K_2(0)=\frac1{6}\cdot3=\frac12\).  So the residual exactly covers the \(-\frac12\) difference.
3. **R5 first-leg table** (see section 3) confirms the product-count bound for a small case.
4. **Q1-Spectral matrix** operator norm invariance verified numerically.
5. **Li--Yang typo identified:** Condition "\(M<T^{-\frac{7}{16}}\)" in Case A should be "\(M<T^{\frac{7}{16}}\)"; the subsequent derivation uses the positive exponent.
6. **Phi special values** computed exactly and used for coefficient generation.
7. **C2-SPU stationary point** checked analytically.

All checks are consistent with the expected mathematical structure.

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

Approximately 20% of the effort explored the H13 transformed matrix and its character-property.  The finding is that the dual character enters diagonally, so the first natural spacing step again faces the Q1-Spectral blindness.  This does not kill the H13 route, but it shows that a simple application of standard large sieve after B-process-first does not resolve the character-blindness problem.

Another possible direction is a **signed decoupling** or **bilinear form** that never forms a Gram matrix but instead estimates a product of two exponential sums where the character appears in a cross term.  For instance, one could study the product \(\bigl(\sum_d \chi_4(d)e(h_1 X/d)\bigr) \overline{\bigl(\sum_d e(h_2 X/d)\bigr)}\) and exploit the difference in character structure.  This would be a genuinely signed approach.  A quick falsification test would be to check whether such a bilinear form can be bounded by existing bilinear sum theorems (e.g., the Bourgain--Guth--Maldague decoupling for the cone) without losing the sign.

A third alternative is to use the **Mellin--Perron** representation but with a contour that picks up the \(L(s,\chi_4)\) factor directly, avoiding the Vaaler truncation entirely.  This route would require subconvexity estimates for \(L(\frac12+it,\chi_4)\) and a sharp analysis of the resulting oscillatory integrals.  It is a long-term project and currently not developed in the repo.

For the immediate next steps, we recommend staying with the main Vaaler/M9 framework and attempting to prove a signed spacing inequality.
