## Detailed reasoning

### 1. H4 source-normalization from Vaaler

Vaaler’s relevant source is Jeffrey D. Vaaler, “Some extremal functions in Fourier analysis,” *Bulletin of the American Mathematical Society* 12(2), 183--216, 1985. Project Euclid and AMS metadata identify the paper and bibliographic data. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

The required theorem is in Vaaler’s Section 7, “Majorizing periodic functions.” Vaaler defines periodic trigonometric polynomials $i_N,j_N,k_N$ in equations (7.1)--(7.3); the search snippet records that $k_N$ is the periodic Fejer kernel and that the identities follow from Poisson summation. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2} Vaaler’s Theorem 18 states that the trigonometric polynomial $\psi*j_N$ satisfies the residual bound

$$
|\psi*j_N(x)-\psi(x)|\le (2N+2)^{-1}k_N(x),
$$

as equation (7.14). The same snippet records the polynomial formula and the residual proof via (7.17). :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Vaaler’s periodic sawtooth is not exactly the repo’s floor-compatible point value. The relevant convention is:

$$
\psi_V(x)=
\begin{cases}
x-\lfloor x\rfloor-\frac12, & x\notin \mathbb Z,\\
0, & x\in \mathbb Z.
\end{cases}
$$

The repo uses

$$
\psi_F(x)=x-\lfloor x\rfloor-\frac12
$$

for all real $x$, so

$$
\psi_F(n)=-\frac12,
\qquad
\psi_V(n)=0.
$$

The two functions agree off $\mathbb Z$. This distinction is harmless only because the Fejer majorant has exactly the right size at discontinuities.

Vaaler’s Theorem 18 gives, with $H=N$,

$$
P_H(x):=\psi_V*j_H(x)
=
\sum_{\substack{1\le |h|\le H}}
-\frac{1}{2\pi i h}\widehat J_{H+1}(h)e(hx),
$$

where $e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}$. Vaaler’s Theorem 6 gives the Fourier transform of $J$ as

$$
\widehat J(t)
=
\pi t(1-|t|)\cot(\pi t)+|t|
\qquad
(0<|t|<1),
$$

with $\widehat J(0)=1$ by continuity and $\widehat J(t)=0$ for $|t|\ge 1$. This formula is recorded in the retrieved Vaaler text around Theorem 6. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

By scaling,

$$
\widehat J_{H+1}(h)=\widehat J\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right).
$$

Since $\widehat J(t)$ is even, define

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u,
\qquad
0<u<1.
$$

Then for $1\le |h|\le H$,

$$
\widehat J_{H+1}(h)=\Phi\left(\frac{|h|}{H+1}\right),
$$

and therefore the repo coefficient is

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}.
$$

The sign is important. Since

$$
\frac{1}{i}=-i,
$$

for $h>0$ one has

$$
\alpha_{h,H}= \frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right),
$$

and for $h<0$,

$$
\alpha_{h,H}=-\frac{i}{2\pi |h|}
\Phi\left(\frac{|h|}{H+1}\right).
$$

Thus

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}},
$$

which is necessary for $P_H(x)$ to be real-valued.

The repo-normalized H4 statement should be:

**H4-R14. Vaaler finite approximation, floor-compatible convention.** For every integer $H\ge 1$,

$$
\psi_F(t)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)
+
R_H(t),
$$

where

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
$$

and

$$
|R_H(t)|\le \frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(t),
$$

with

$$
K_H(t)=
\sum_{|k|\le H}
\left(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\right)e(kt)
=
\frac{1}{H+1}
\left(\frac{\sin \pi(H+1)t}{\sin \pi t}\right)^2.
$$

Proof of the integer convention transfer: for $t\notin\mathbb Z$, $\psi_F(t)=\psi_V(t)$, so Vaaler’s Theorem 18 gives the claim. If $t=n\in\mathbb Z$, then

$$
P_H(n)=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}=0
$$

because $\alpha_{-h,H}=-\alpha_{h,H}$ for real summation at $e(hn)=1$. Hence

$$
|R_H(n)|=|\psi_F(n)-P_H(n)|=\frac12.
$$

Also

$$
K_H(0)=H+1,
$$

so

$$
\frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(0)=\frac12.
$$

Thus the residual bound holds at integers with equality.

This confirms that the floor-compatible sawtooth used in H3 is compatible with Vaaler’s periodic theorem after one explicit discontinuity check. The source-normalization gap is now reduced to a final proof-draft citation check against the printed notation of Theorem 18 and Theorem 6, not to a mathematical ambiguity.

### 2. R5-Full: product-count proof for all Fejer residuals

Fix $X\ge 2$. Let $D$ be dyadic and suppose

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}.
$$

Let $H=H_D$ be an integer satisfying

$$
c_1DX^{-1/4}\le H\le c_2DX^{-1/4}
$$

for fixed positive constants $c_1,c_2$. In the proof draft one can take

$$
H_D=\max\left(1,\left\lfloor cDX^{-1/4}\right\rfloor\right)
$$

with $c$ chosen so that $H_D\asymp DX^{-1/4}$ on the active dyadic range.

Let $w_D$ be a dyadic weight supported on $d\asymp D$, with $|w_D(d)|\le 1$. For a nonnegative partition of unity, $w_D\ge0$; if a later smooth partition has signed pieces, the residual proof uses $|w_D|$ and bounded overlap.

The Fejer pointwise estimate is

$$
K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(H,\frac{1}{H\|t\|^2}\right),
$$

and hence

$$
\frac{1}{H}K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac{1}{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

This estimate follows immediately from the sine formula for $K_H(t)$ and the inequalities

$$
|\sin \pi t|\asymp \|t\|,
\qquad
|\sin \pi(H+1)t|\le \min(1,(H+1)\|t\|),
$$

with constants uniform in $H$.

#### First residual leg

Consider

$$
\mathcal R_{1,D}
=
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d).
$$

For each $d$, choose an integer $m=m(d)$ nearest to $X/d$. If there are two nearest integers, choose either one; all estimates use only

$$
\left|X/d-m\right|=\|X/d\|.
$$

Since $d\asymp D$,

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
=
\left|\frac Xd-m\right|
=
\frac{|X-md|}{d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-md|}{D}.
$$

Set

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

Then

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-md|^2}\right),
$$

with the convention that the second term is $+\infty$ and the minimum is $1$ when $X=md$.

Now set

$$
n=md.
$$

Because $m$ is nearest to $X/d$,

$$
|X-md|\le \frac d2\ll D,
$$

so $n$ lies in an interval of length $O(D)$ around $X$. Since $D\le X^{1/2}$, all such positive $n$ are $n\asymp X$ for $X$ sufficiently large; small $X$ can be absorbed into the implicit constant. For each fixed integer $n$, the number of pairs $(m,d)$ with $n=md$ and $d\asymp D$ is at most $\tau(n)$. Therefore

$$
\mathcal R_{1,D}
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}
\tau(n)
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right).
$$

Using the pointwise divisor bound

$$
\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon\ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon
$$

on $n\asymp X$, it remains to bound the one-dimensional kernel sum

$$
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right).
$$

This is

$$
\ll \Delta+1.
$$

Indeed, the terms with $|X-n|\le \Delta$ contribute $O(\Delta+1)$, and the dyadic annulus

$$
2^j\Delta<|X-n|\le 2^{j+1}\Delta
$$

contributes

$$
O(2^j\Delta)\cdot O(2^{-2j})=O(2^{-j}\Delta).
$$

Summing $j\ge0$ gives $O(\Delta)$. Hence

$$
\mathcal R_{1,D}
\ll_\epsilon
X^\epsilon(\Delta+1)
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proof is uniform for real $X$, integer $X$, and near-integer $X$. If $X$ is exactly an integer and many products $md=X$ occur, they contribute through the term

$$
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-md|^2}\right)=1,
$$

and their number is at most $\tau(X)\ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon$, below the target.

#### Shifted second residual legs

For $\rho\in\{1,3\}$, consider

$$
\mathcal R_{2,D,\rho}
=
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right).
$$

For each $d$, choose $m$ nearest to

$$
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}.
$$

Then

$$
\left\|\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right\|
=
\left|\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}-m\right|
=
\frac{|X-(4m-\rho)d|}{4d}.
$$

Set

$$
\ell=4m-\rho.
$$

Then

$$
\ell\equiv -\rho\pmod 4,
$$

so $\ell\equiv 3\pmod4$ if $\rho=1$ and $\ell\equiv1\pmod4$ if $\rho=3$. For $X$ sufficiently large and $D\le X^{1/2}$, the nearest integer $m$ is positive and

$$
\ell\asymp X/D>0.
$$

Any finite exceptional range where $\ell\le0$ is absorbed into the small-$X$ constant; alternatively, one may retain all integer $\ell$ and note that only $\ell\asymp X/D$ can satisfy the near-product condition.

The same Fejer estimate gives

$$
\frac1H
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-\ell d|^2}\right),
$$

with the same

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

Set

$$
n=\ell d.
$$

The congruence condition on $\ell$ only restricts the possible factorizations of $n$; it never increases their number. Thus the number of pairs $(\ell,d)$ with $n=\ell d$, $d\asymp D$, and $\ell\equiv -\rho\pmod4$ is at most $\tau(n)$. Therefore

$$
\mathcal R_{2,D,\rho}
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}
\tau(n)
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right)
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proves the shifted second-leg residual estimate.

#### Zero mode, signs, dyadic summation, and short blocks

The kernel $K_H$ already includes the zero Fourier mode. If separated, the zero mode contributes

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
\ll
\frac{D}{H}
\asymp
X^{1/4}.
$$

The nonzero modes and both signs of the Fejer frequency are handled simultaneously by the positive kernel $K_H$.

For short blocks $D<X^{1/4}$, do not invoke Vaaler. Use

$$
|\psi(t)|\le\frac12
$$

for the floor-compatible sawtooth. The first leg contributes at most

$$
\sum_{d\le C X^{1/4}}1\ll X^{1/4},
$$

and the two second-leg sawtooth sums contribute the same order. Thus short blocks are harmless.

Finally, the number of dyadic blocks is $O(\log X)$. After replacing $\epsilon$ by $\epsilon/2$ in the blockwise estimate, the logarithm is absorbed into $X^\epsilon$. Hence all Vaaler residuals in H3 contribute

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This is R5-Full.

### 3. Bridge theorem proof

Assume H1--H3, H4, R5-Full, and M9.

Start from H3:

$$
P(X)=W_1(X)+W_2(X)+O(1),
$$

where

$$
W_1(X)=
-4\sum_{d\le y}\chi_4(d)\psi(X/d),
$$

and

$$
W_2(X)=
4\sum_{d\le y}
\left[
\psi\left(\frac{X/d+1}{4}\right)
-
\psi\left(\frac{X/d+3}{4}\right)
\right].
$$

Decompose $d$ dyadically:

$$
1_{d\le y}=\sum_D w_D(d)
$$

with bounded overlap, $D\le X^{1/2}$. Blocks $D<X^{1/4}$ are bounded trivially by $O(X^{1/4})$.

For each active block $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$, apply H4 with height $H_D\asymp DX^{-1/4}$. The first leg becomes

$$
-4\sum_D
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\sum_d
\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d)
+
\text{first-leg residual}.
$$

The second leg becomes

$$
4\sum_D
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\left(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\right)
\sum_d
w_D(d)e(hX/(4d))
+
\text{second-leg residual}.
$$

By R5-Full, the total residual is

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

By M9, each active dyadic block’s two main terms are

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon/2}.
$$

There are $O(\log X)$ dyadic blocks, so the total main contribution is

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Therefore

$$
P(X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proves the bridge theorem. It does not prove the conjectural estimate unless M9 is proved.

### 5. M9b theorem-extension problem

The second main term has three possible formulations. They are not analytically equivalent for theorem application.

#### Formulation A: shifted phase functions

Work with the two original second-leg pieces:

$$
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}e(h\rho/4)
\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)),
\qquad
\rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

Writing $d=Dz$, the phase is

$$
e\left(
h\frac{X}{D}
\left(
\frac{1}{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X}
\right)
\right).
$$

Thus define

$$
F_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac{1}{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X}.
$$

This fits the Li--Yang-style sum shape

$$
e\left(\frac{hT}{M}F\left(\frac dM\right)\right)
$$

with

$$
T=X,\qquad M=D.
$$

The derivative conditions are the same as for $1/(4z)$ because the additive constant has zero derivative:

$$
F_{\rho,D}'(z)=-\frac{1}{4z^2},
$$

$$
F_{\rho,D}''(z)=\frac{1}{2z^3},
$$

$$
F_{\rho,D}'''(z)=-\frac{3}{2z^4}.
$$

Hence

$$
F_{\rho,D}'F_{\rho,D}'''-3(F_{\rho,D}'')^2
=
\frac{3}{8z^6}-\frac{3}{4z^6}
=
-\frac{3}{8z^6}.
$$

On $z\in[1,2]$ this is bounded away from $0$.

This is the best official theorem-comparison formulation, because it keeps the $h$-sum over ordinary consecutive integers and avoids inserting a periodic $\chi_4(h)$ weight into the amplitude.

#### Formulation B: fractional-frequency phases

Split

$$
h=4q+r,
\qquad
r\in\{1,3\}.
$$

Then

$$
\chi_4(h)e(hX/(4d))
=
\chi_4(r)e((q+r/4)X/d).
$$

This creates phases

$$
e((q+\beta)X/d),
\qquad
\beta\in\left\{\frac14,\frac34\right\}.
$$

This formulation is useful as a stress test and for checking whether a theorem is stable under fixed fractional frequency shifts. It is less convenient as the official comparison because many Bombieri--Iwaniec statements are written with integer $h$ and smooth $h/H$ weights.

#### Formulation C: periodic $h$-weight

Use

$$
\chi_4(h)
$$

as part of the $h$-weight. This is dangerous for direct Li--Yang comparison because Li--Yang’s $g(h/H)$ is assumed of bounded variation on $[1,2]$ in the source formulation, while a periodic weight $\chi_4(h)$ has variation proportional to $H$ if treated as a function over the full dyadic interval. It may be repaired by residue splitting, but that returns to Formulation A or B.

**Decision.** Use Formulation A, the shifted phase

$$
F_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac{1}{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X},
$$

as the official M9b theorem-comparison formulation. Keep Formulation B as a fractional-shift stress test. Do not use Formulation C for theorem import unless the theorem explicitly permits periodic arithmetic weights or the residues have been split.

### 6. Li--Yang subrange map

Li--Yang’s arXiv page identifies the paper as Xiaochun Li and Xuerui Yang, “An improvement on Gauss’s Circle Problem and Dirichlet’s Divisor Problem,” arXiv:2308.14859, and its abstract says it uses the Bombieri--Iwaniec method, a new first-spacing estimate, and Huxley’s second-spacing results. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

The local TeX source gives the main theorem statement:

$$
R(X)=O_\epsilon(X^{\theta^*+\epsilon}),
\qquad
\Delta(X)=O_\epsilon(X^{\theta^*+\epsilon}),
$$

with

$$
\theta^*=0.3144831759741\cdots.
$$

The source defines the exponential sum

$$
S
=
\sum_{H\le h\le2H}g(h/H)
\sum_{M\le m\le2M}G(m/M)
e\left(\frac{hT}{M}F(m/M)\right),
$$

where $g,G$ have bounded variation and $F\in C^3([1,2])$ satisfies derivative size and nondegeneracy hypotheses. The source then defines Case A and Case B parameter ranges and later reduces circle/divisor estimates to $S/H\lesssim_\epsilon T^{\theta^*+\epsilon}$ with $1\le H\le MT^{-\theta^*}$.

For our M9 comparison,

$$
T=X,\qquad M=D=X^\delta,\qquad H=X^\eta,
$$

with

$$
\frac14\le\delta\le\frac12,
\qquad
0\le \eta\le \delta-\frac14
$$

for the endpoint Vaaler range.

#### Endpoint needed for M9

For each $\delta$, M9 needs coverage up to

$$
\eta_{\mathrm{end}}=\delta-\frac14.
$$

At the critical endpoint block $\delta=1/2$,

$$
\eta_{\mathrm{end}}=\frac14.
$$

#### Raw Case A

The upper height condition is

$$
H\le MT^{-49/164},
$$

so

$$
\eta\le \delta-\frac{49}{164}.
$$

Since

$$
\frac{49}{164}=0.2987804878\cdots,
$$

raw Case A misses the top part of the endpoint range by

$$
\frac{49}{164}-\frac14
=
\frac{8}{164}
=
\frac{2}{41}
\approx0.0487805.
$$

If $\delta<7/16$, the additional lower condition is

$$
H\ge M^{-9}T^4,
$$

i.e.

$$
\eta\ge 4-9\delta.
$$

This lower condition is compatible with the upper condition only when

$$
4-9\delta\le \delta-\frac{49}{164},
$$

or

$$
\delta\ge \frac{4+49/164}{10}
=
0.429878\cdots.
$$

Thus raw Case A covers only a restricted lower part of the endpoint region and never reaches the Vaaler endpoint height $\eta=\delta-1/4$.

#### Raw Case B

Case B gives

$$
H\le \min\left\{
M^{35/69}T^{-2/23},
B_0M^{3/2}T^{-1/2}
\right\}.
$$

In exponents this is

$$
\eta\le \frac{35\delta-6}{69}
$$

and

$$
\eta\le \frac32\delta-\frac12.
$$

The two upper bounds are equal when

$$
\frac{35\delta-6}{69}=\frac32\delta-\frac12,
$$

i.e.

$$
\delta=\frac{57}{137}=0.416058\cdots.
$$

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps

1. **H4 exact notation still needs final proof-draft citation.** The mathematical translation is now explicit, but the proof draft should quote Vaaler’s page 210, Theorem 18, equations (7.13)--(7.17), and page 192, Theorem 6, equation (2.28), from the printed paper.

2. **Vaaler’s $\psi$ convention must be stated before H4 is used.** It is not safe to write “Vaaler approximates $\psi$” without specifying whether $\psi(n)$ is $0$ or $-1/2$.

3. **Integer and near-integer arguments are not negligible.** They are exactly where $K_H$ spikes. R5 treats them through product counting. A proof that replaces the residual by $D/H$ alone is incomplete.

4. **Second-leg sign and congruence bookkeeping can be miscopied.** For $\rho=1$, $\ell=4m-1\equiv3\pmod4$. For $\rho=3$, $\ell=4m-3\equiv1\pmod4$. Reversing this does not change the divisor upper bound but matters for any signed refinement.

5. **M9b cannot be imported through a periodic $\chi_4(h)$ BV weight.** The official theorem-comparison form should use shifted phase functions. Treating $\chi_4(h)$ as a bounded-variation weight over $h\asymp H$ would introduce variation $\asymp H$.

6. **Li--Yang’s source appears to contain a sign inconsistency in one displayed Case A condition as parsed.** The later reduction uses $M<T^{7/16}$, which is the mathematically meaningful version. Any proof draft should quote the exact TeX and resolve this with context before using Case A.

7. **M9 full $h$-range versus dyadic $h$ theorems.** M9 is stated with $1\le |h|\le H_D$ and coefficient $1/h$ decay. Li--Yang-style theorems are dyadic in $h$. Any theorem application must split $h$ dyadically and sum logarithmically.

8. **Stationary-phase transforms do not imply double-sum cancellation.** H13 or C2-SPU can convert a single inner sum, but the transformed double sum still needs a spacing or large-sieve theorem.

9. **Operator-norm character blindness remains a live obstruction.** If $\chi_4$ enters only as a diagonal unitary conjugation, operator norm estimates cannot exploit it. Any proposed signed method must avoid collapsing to this situation.

10. **No numerical evidence has been executed here.** This response gives proof infrastructure and formulas. The next verification step should include actual R5 and M9 tables.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

### Obstruction 1: Vaaler jump mismatch

Test $X/d\in\mathbb Z$. Then the repo sawtooth has value $-1/2$ while the Vaaler polynomial has value $0$. The residual bound gives exactly

$$
|R_H|=\frac12
=
\frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(0).
$$

This does not falsify H4; it confirms the need for the Fejer majorant.

### Obstruction 2: divisor-rich $X$

If $X$ is a highly composite integer and many $d\asymp D$ divide $X$, then many first-leg residual terms hit Fejer spikes. R5 gives a total exact-spike contribution bounded by

$$
\tau(X)\ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon,
$$

and near-spikes by

$$
X^\epsilon(\Delta+1)\asymp X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Thus divisor-rich $X$ does not break the residual argument. It remains a useful numerical stress case.

### Obstruction 3: second-leg congruence spikes

If

$$
X=d(4m-\rho)
$$

for many $d$, then

$$
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}=m
$$

is an integer and the Fejer kernel spikes. The number of such representations is at most $\tau(X)$ with an additional congruence restriction on the complementary factor. This is bounded by R5. No new residual obstruction appears.

### Obstruction 4: M9b periodic weight

If M9b is represented as

$$
\sum_h \alpha_h\chi_4(h)\sum_d e(hX/(4d)),
$$

and one tries to use a theorem requiring bounded variation in $h/H$, the periodic $\chi_4(h)$ is not an acceptable smooth weight. This obstructs a naive black-box theorem import. The shifted-phase formulation avoids it.

### Obstruction 5: Li--Yang endpoint mismatch

At $D=X^{1/2}$, M9 needs $H=X^{1/4}$. Raw Li--Yang Case A gives at most

$$
H\le X^{1/2-49/164}=X^{33/164},
$$

and raw Case B gives at most

$$
H\le X^{23/138}=X^{1/6}.
$$

The final $\theta^*$ reduction reaches only

$$
H\le X^{1/2-\theta^*}=X^{0.185516\cdots}.
$$

Thus black-box Li--Yang does not reach M9.

### Obstruction 6: H13 character erasure

After B-process-first, $\chi_4(d)$ becomes a dual character. But if the next Cauchy--Schwarz or spacing step forms a quadratic kernel where the dual character appears only as a diagonal unitary, the operator norm is unchanged. This would make H13 unhelpful for endpoint M9 unless a signed-form estimate is retained.

### Obstruction 7: endpoint local height choice

If $H_D$ is chosen substantially smaller than $DX^{-1/4}$, the zero Fejer mode grows as $D/H_D$ and can exceed $X^{1/4}$. If $H_D$ is chosen substantially larger, the main M9 frequency range grows and becomes harder. The local scale

$$
H_D\asymp DX^{-1/4}
$$

is the correct residual/main-term balancing point.

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

### H13: B-process-first transform for M9a

The most relevant exploratory route remains H13, applied to M9a before Cauchy--Schwarz.

Let

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
\sum_d\chi_4(d)w(d/D)e(hX/d).
$$

Set

$$
f(u)=w(u/D)e(hX/u),
$$

with Fourier transform

$$
\widehat f(\xi)=\int_{\mathbb R}f(u)e(-\xi u)\,du.
$$

Splitting modulo $4$ gives

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
\sum_{d\equiv1\pmod4}f(d)
-
\sum_{d\equiv3\pmod4}f(d).
$$

Poisson summation on residue classes gives

$$
\sum_{d\equiv r\pmod4}f(d)
=
\frac14\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}
e(nr/4)\widehat f(n/4).
$$

Therefore

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
\frac14\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}
\left(e(n/4)-e(3n/4)\right)
\widehat f(n/4).
$$

Since

$$
e(n/4)-e(3n/4)=2i\chi_4(n),
$$

we get

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
\frac{i}{2}
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}
\chi_4(n)
\int_{\mathbb R}
w(u/D)e(hX/u-nu/4)\,du.
$$

The active stationary range has $n<0$. Write $n=-m$, $m>0$. The phase is

$$
\phi(u)=\frac{hX}{u}+\frac{mu}{4}.
$$

Then

$$
\phi'(u)=-\frac{hX}{u^2}+\frac m4,
$$

so the stationary point is

$$
u_0=2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}}.
$$

The support condition $u_0\asymp D$ gives the dual length

$$
m\asymp \frac{hX}{D^2}.
$$

At the stationary point,

$$
\phi(u_0)=\sqrt{hXm}.
$$

The leading amplitude scale is again

$$
D^{3/2}(hX)^{-1/2}
$$

up to an absolute constant and smooth weight factor.

This transform preserves a $\chi_4(m)$ factor in the dual variable. It does not prove cancellation. The dual phase

$$
\sqrt{hXm}
$$

has degenerate continuous Hessian in $(h,m)$, so generic full-rank two-dimensional stationary phase or decoupling is not available directly. The transform is useful only if a discrete spacing argument can use the dual character before it becomes a diagonal unitary.

### H13 falsification checklist

H13 should be deprioritized if any one of the following occurs in the first serious model:

1. After Cauchy--Schwarz, the dual character appears only as $U^*KU$ for a diagonal unitary $U=\operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(m))$, and the proof uses only $\|U^*KU\|_{\mathrm{op}}=\|K\|_{\mathrm{op}}$.

2. The stationary-phase error, summed over $h$ and $m$, is larger than $X^{1/4+\epsilon}$ in the endpoint range.

3. The short dual range

$$
m\asymp \frac{hX}{D^2}\asymp1
$$

dominates a non-negligible block and cannot be handled trivially.

4. The first spacing theorem needed for the phase $\sqrt{hXm}$ is exactly as hard as the original M9 spacing problem, with no sign-sensitive gain.

5. Direct differencing in the dual variable produces the same $\chi_4(m)\chi_4(m+q)$ parity collapse already recorded in H7, without compensating phase information.

### Cross-residue interference as a narrower exploratory statistic

For M9a, split denominators by residues

$$
d=4a+1
\quad\text{and}\quad
d=4b+3.
$$

The signed spatial character sum is a difference of two residue sums. A possible statistic is the open-path bilinear form

$$
\mathcal S_{\mathrm{CRI}}
=
\sum_{h_1,h_2}
\alpha_{h_1}\overline{\alpha_{h_2}}
\sum_{\substack{d_1\equiv1\pmod4\\ d_2\equiv3\pmod4}}
w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2)
e\left(X\left(\frac{h_1}{d_1}-\frac{h_2}{d_2}\right)\right)
\Omega(d_1,d_2,h_1,h_2),
$$

where $\Omega$ is a localization weight selecting near-collisions in the intended spacing step.

This is useful only if it is not reducible to a cyclic trace or an operator norm of a diagonal conjugate. A quick falsification test is to compute the same statistic with absolute values or with randomized residue signs; if the signed statistic has no systematic reduction, the CRI route should remain low priority.
