In repo notation, define

$$
K_H(t)
=
\sum_{|k|\le H}
\left(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\right)e(kt)
=
\frac1{H+1}
\left(\frac{\sin \pi(H+1)t}{\sin \pi t}\right)^2.
$$

Define

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u,
\qquad 0<u<1.
$$

The finite Vaaler approximation used in the proof draft is:

$$
\psi_F(t)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)
+
R_H^F(t),
$$

where

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
\qquad 1\le |h|\le H,
$$

and

$$
|R_H^F(t)|\le \frac1{2H+2}K_H(t).

The endpoint conversion is required for this proof draft. Vaaler's periodic approximation uses the centered value $0$ at integer discontinuities, while the arithmetic sawtooth has $\psi_F(n)=-1/2$. At integers,

$$
V_H(n)=0,

\psi_F(n)=-\frac12,
\qquad
K_H(0)=H+1.
$$

Hence

$$
\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12,
$$

$$
|\psi_F(n)-V_H(n)|=\frac12.

### 2. Alpha-Conjugacy-R24

For $h>0$,

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}
=
\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right).
$$

Since $\Phi$ is real-valued on $(0,1)$,

$$
\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}
=
-\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right).
$$

For $-h$,

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i(-h)}
=
-\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right).
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.}
$$

This is a proved algebraic lemma. The relation $\alpha_{-h,H}=-\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$ is false and should remain a regression flag.

### 3. Phi-Asymmetry-R24

For $0<u<1$,

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u.
$$

Using

$$
\cot(\pi(1-u))=-\cot(\pi u),
$$

we get

$$
\Phi(1-u)
=
\pi (1-u)u\cot(\pi(1-u))+(1-u)
=
-\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+1-u.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{\Phi(u)+\Phi(1-u)=1.}
$$

Consequently $\Phi(1-u)=\Phi(u)$ is false in general. For example,

$$
\Phi(1/4)=\frac{3\pi}{16}+\frac14,
\qquad
\Phi(3/4)=-\frac{3\pi}{16}+\frac34.
$$

Endpoint limits are

$$
\Phi(0^+)=1,
\qquad
\Phi(1^-)=0.
$$

The Round 23 state records the same correction: $\Phi(u)+\Phi(1-u)=1$, so $\Phi(1-u)=\Phi(u)$ is false.

### 4. R5-Full-R24: Fejer residual product-count bound

#### Statement

Let $X\ge2$ be real. Let

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2},
$$

and let $H$ be an integer satisfying

$$
c_0D X^{-1/4}\le H\le C_0D X^{-1/4}
$$

for fixed $0<c_0\le C_0$. Let $w_D$ be supported on $d\asymp D$ and satisfy $|w_D(d)|\le C_w$.

Then, for every $\epsilon>0$,

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_{\epsilon,c_0,C_0,C_w} X^{1/4+\epsilon},
$$

and for $\rho\in\{1,3\}$,

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_{\epsilon,c_0,C_0,C_w} X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Thus, assuming H4-R24, all fixed Fejer residuals from the two balanced sawtooth legs are

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}
$$

after dyadic summation.

#### Fejer pointwise bound

The Fejer kernel satisfies

$$
K_H(t)\ll \min\left(H,\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\right),
$$

where

$$
\|t\|=\min_{m\in\mathbb Z}|t-m|.
$$

Hence

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

Set

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}.
$$

Since $H\asymp D X^{-1/4}$,

$$
\Delta\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

Define the exact-resonance-safe cap

$$
W_\Delta(u)=
\begin{cases}
1,&u=0,\\
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{u^2}\right),&u\ne0.
\end{cases}
$$

The value $W_\Delta(0)=1$ is not cosmetic: it is required when $X/d$ or $(X/d+\rho)/4$ is exactly integral.

#### First residual leg

For each $d\asymp D$, choose an integer $m=m(d)$ nearest to $X/d$. If there is a tie, fix a deterministic convention, for example the smaller nearest integer. The proof uses only

$$
\left|\frac Xd-m\right|=\left\|\frac Xd\right\|.
$$

Then

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
=
\frac{|X-md|}{d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-md|}{D}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)
\ll
W_\Delta(X-md).
$$

Grouping by

$$
n=md
$$

gives at most $\tau(n)$ representations, since $m,d$ are integers. Thus

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n).
$$

The divisor bound

$$
\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon
$$

and the elementary estimate

$$
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}W_\Delta(X-n)\ll \Delta+1
$$

give

$$
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n)
\ll_\epsilon
X^\epsilon(\Delta+1)
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This argument is uniform for real $X$. If $X$ is integer and $n=X$, the exact resonance contribution is handled by $W_\Delta(0)=1$.

#### Shifted second residual legs

For

$$
t=\frac{X/d+\rho}{4},
\qquad \rho\in\{1,3\},
$$

near-integrality means that for some $m\in\mathbb Z$,

$$
\left|\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}-m\right|
=
\frac{|X-d(4m-\rho)|}{4d}.
$$

Write

$$
\ell=4m-\rho.
$$

Then

$$
\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4
$$

and

$$
X\approx d\ell.
$$

The same Fejer bound gives

$$
\frac1H
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll
W_{C\Delta}(X-d\ell)
$$

for an absolute constant $C$.

In the large active range, $\ell$ is positive on the near-resonant support. Indeed $d\le C D\le C X^{1/2}$ and $X/d\gg X^{1/2}$, so near-integrality forces $4m-\rho\asymp X/d>0$ for large $X$. Bounded $X$ can be separated and absorbed into the implicit constant. Thus $n=d\ell$ is a positive integer product with the congruence restriction $\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4$, and the multiplicity is at most $\tau(n)$. The congruence restriction only reduces the count. Therefore the same divisor-sum argument yields

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This is exactly the residual mechanism recorded in the Round 23 state.

#### Short blocks and dyadic overlap

For $D<X^{1/4}$, do not apply Vaaler. Use

$$
|\psi_F(t)|\le \frac12
$$

away from needing Fourier expansion. Summing over all $d<X^{1/4}$ gives

$$
O(X^{1/4})
$$

up to bounded dyadic overlap and harmless logarithmic factors.

A smooth dyadic partition with bounded overlap contributes only $O(\log X)$ blocks; this is absorbed into $X^\epsilon$.

### 6. M9-R24: fixed-coefficient main sums

Freeze M9 with actual Vaaler coefficients:

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}.
$$

For $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$ and $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$, define

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
-4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\sum_{d\asymp D}
\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),
$$

and

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\left(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\right)
\sum_{d\asymp D}
w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

Since

$$
e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=2i\chi_4(h)
$$

for odd $h$ and vanishes for even $h$, one may also write

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
8i\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}\chi_4(h)
\sum_{d\asymp D}
w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

The target is:

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal M_1(D;X),\mathcal M_2(D;X)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
}
$$

This is the active open analytic problem.

### 8. M2-CS-Normalizations-R24: bounded-scope diagnostic

Write

$$
C_h=e(h/4)-e(3h/4).
$$

Then

$$
C_h=
\begin{cases}
2i\chi_4(h),&2\nmid h,\\
0,&2\mid h,
\end{cases}
$$

and

$$
|C_h|^2=
\begin{cases}
4,&2\nmid h,\\
0,&2\mid h.
\end{cases}
$$

Let

$$
B_h(D;X)=\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

Then

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)=4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}C_hB_h(D;X).
$$

#### Weighted $|\alpha_h|$ Cauchy

Let

$$
L=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\asymp \log H_D.
$$

Then

$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2
\le
16L
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\,|C_h|^2\,|B_h(D;X)|^2.
$$

Expanding the square over $d_1,d_2$ gives the kernel

$$
K_{2;d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}
=
w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2)
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\,|C_h|^2
e\left(
\frac{hX}{4}
\left(\frac1{d_1}-\frac1{d_2}\right)
\right).
$$

The diagonal contribution inside the second factor is

$$
\asymp D\log H_D.
$$

After multiplying by $L$, the diagonal is

$$
\asymp D(\log H_D)^2
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon},
$$

which is acceptable for the squared endpoint target. But the sign of $\chi_4(h)$ is lost through $|C_h|^2$, leaving only odd-$h$ support. This is a bounded-scope diagnostic for this Cauchy normalization, not a global obstruction to M9b.

#### Unweighted $h$ Cauchy

Alternatively,

$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2
\le
16H_D
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
|\alpha_{h,H_D}|^2|C_h|^2|B_h(D;X)|^2.
$$

The diagonal contribution in the second factor is

$$
\asymp D
$$

because $\sum_h |\alpha_h|^2|C_h|^2\asymp1$. After multiplying by $H_D$,

$$
D H_D\asymp D^2X^{-1/4}.
$$

At the endpoint $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, this is

$$
X^{3/4},
$$

which exceeds the squared target $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$. Thus this normalization is too crude at the endpoint.

The Round 23 state records the same bounded-scope conclusion: unweighted $h$-Cauchy is too large, while weighted $h$-Cauchy has acceptable diagonal but loses the $\chi_4(h)$ sign.

### 10. CRI-M2-R24

$$
\Sigma_r^R(D;X)
=
\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\h\equiv r\pmod4}}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\operatorname{Re}
\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)),

Then the paired formula gives

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
-\frac8\pi\left(\Sigma_1^R(D;X)-\Sigma_3^R(D;X)\right).
$$

Thus the relevant cancellation is not the cross term alone, but the whole expression

$$
|\Sigma_1^R-\Sigma_3^R|^2
=
|\Sigma_1^R|^2+|\Sigma_3^R|^2
-
2\operatorname{Re}(\Sigma_1^R\overline{\Sigma_3^R}).
$$

A falsification ratio is

$$
R_{\rm CRI}
=
\frac{|\Sigma_1^R-\Sigma_3^R|^2}
{|\Sigma_1^R|^2+|\Sigma_3^R|^2}.
$$

Values near $1$ indicate little useful cross-residue interference. Values substantially below $1$ justify further proof work. This is not yet a theorem-level sufficient condition unless paired with bounds on $|\Sigma_1^R|^2+|\Sigma_3^R|^2$ at the correct scale.

$$

$$
\sum_n \tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n)

$$
\mathcal M_1^{(\theta)}(D;X),

for

$$

$$
P(X)\ll_\epsilon X^{\theta+\epsilon}.
$$

Li--Yang's record-exponent technology should be placed in this variable-$\theta$ track, not in the endpoint track. Their final exponent is

$$
\theta^*=0.3144831759741\ldots,

and the arXiv abstract confirms the method uses Bombieri--Iwaniec, a new first-spacing estimate, and Huxley's second-spacing results. ### 12. Lindelof-comparison note

The human note asks whether pointwise M9 control maps to $L(1/2+it,\chi_4)$ with

$$
t\asymp \frac{hX}{D}.
$$

There is a local analogy but not a theorem-level map.

For fixed $h$ and $d\asymp D$, the reciprocal phase is

$$
f(d)=\frac{hX}{d}.
$$

Near $d=D+u$,

$$
\frac{hX}{D+u}
=
\frac{hX}{D}
-
\frac{hX}{D^2}u
+
\frac{hX}{D^3}u^2
+O\left(\frac{hX}{D^4}|u|^3\right).
$$

A Dirichlet polynomial for $L(1/2+it,\chi_4)$ has phase

$$
-t\log d.
$$

Near $D+u$,

$$
-t\log(D+u)
=
-t\log D
-
\frac{t}{D}u
+
\frac{t}{2D^2}u^2
+O\left(\frac{t}{D^3}|u|^3\right).
$$

Matching the linear coefficient gives

$$
t\asymp \frac{hX}{D}.
$$

But the quadratic coefficient differs by a factor of $2$, and the reciprocal phase is not globally logarithmic. More importantly, the approximate functional equation for fixed modulus $L(1/2+it,\chi_4)$ has natural length about

$$
t^{1/2}.
$$

At the endpoint block

$$
D\asymp X^{1/2},
\qquad
h\asymp X^{1/4},
$$

one has

$$
t\asymp \frac{hX}{D}\asymp X^{3/4},
\qquad
t^{1/2}\asymp X^{3/8},
$$

whereas the M9 denominator length is

$$
D\asymp X^{1/2}.
$$

Thus the M9 inner sum is not a standard approximate-functional-equation block for $L(1/2+it,\chi_4)$. A short subblock of length $L\ll D$ may be locally modeled by a logarithmic phase after Taylor matching, but global M9 control is not equivalent to Lindelof for $L(1/2+it,\chi_4)$.

Conclusion: the Lindelof comparison is a heuristic local conductor analogy, not a theorem. A valid theorem would need an explicit partition into short intervals, Taylor error control, coefficient transfer, smoothing, and a summation over $h$ with the actual Vaaler coefficients.

### 13. Kloosterman/Kuznetsov map

M9 has phases of the form

$$
e\left(\frac{hX}{d}\right)
$$

and

$$
e\left(\frac{hX}{4d}\right),
$$

with either a spatial character $\chi_4(d)$ in $\mathcal M_1$ or a frequency character in $\mathcal M_2$.

A Kuznetsov formula typically applies to sums of Kloosterman sums

$$
S_{\chi}(m,n;c)
=
\sum_{\substack{x\bmod c\\(x,c)=1}}
\chi(x)e\left(\frac{mx+n\overline{x}}{c}\right),
$$

over moduli $c$, for a congruence subgroup such as $\Gamma_0(q)$ and nebentypus $\chi$. A plausible level for $\chi_4$ would be level $4$ with nebentypus $\chi_4$, but the M9 phase is not already a Kloosterman sum: it is a reciprocal real phase with $d$ as the varying denominator and no complete residue sum over $x\bmod d$.

To turn M9 into a Kuznetsov-compatible object, one would need at least:

1. a completion or delta-symbol step introducing a complete residue sum modulo $d$ or a related modulus;
2. a choice of $\Gamma_0(4)$ or another level structure with nebentypus compatible with $\chi_4$;
3. a test function whose Bessel transform matches the $d\asymp D$, $h\le H_D$ dyadic support;
4. conductor bookkeeping for $m,n,c$ in the spectral large sieve;
5. a proof that the resulting spectral bound reaches endpoint height $H_D=D X^{-1/4}$;
6. a check that the original fixed Vaaler coefficients $\alpha_{h,H_D}$ survive the completion without becoming an arbitrary-coefficient problem.

At present, this is a literature map only. It should not displace the finite-statistic M9 work. The exact Kuznetsov formula, level, nebentypus, test transform, and conductor dependencies remain missing.

### 14. H13 endpoint falsification test

The spatial-character main sum in $\mathcal M_1$ has an exploratory B-process-first transform. Splitting modulo $4$ and applying Poisson summation gives, up to convention-dependent global sign,

$$
\sum_d \chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d)
=
\frac{i}{2}
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}
\chi_4(n)
\int_{\mathbb R}
w_D(u)e\left(\frac{hX}{u}-\frac{nu}{4}\right)\,du.
$$

The phase is

$$
\phi_n(u)=\frac{hX}{u}-\frac{nu}{4}.
$$

Stationary points occur for $n<0$. Write $n=-m$, $m>0$. Then

$$
u_0=2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}},
$$

and the active dual length is

$$
m\asymp \frac{hX}{D^2}.
$$

At maximal Vaaler height

$$
h\asymp H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4},
$$

with $D=X^\delta$, this gives

$$
m\asymp X^{3/4-\delta}.
$$

The transform is roughly balanced only near $D\asymp X^{1/2}$. The leading phase is of square-root type,

$$
\Phi_{\rm dual}(h,m)\asymp \sqrt{Xhm},
$$

and

$$
\det \nabla^2\sqrt{Xhm}=0.
$$

Therefore H13 is not a path to generic full-rank stationary phase or full-rank decoupling.

Round 24 should keep H13 as one endpoint falsification test only: near $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, include the stationary amplitude, build the signed dual statistic involving $\chi_4(m)$, compare it to the unsigned statistic and the diagonal-unitary operator norm, and record whether the dual character survives beyond norm extraction. A positive numerical signal would still be evidence for further investigation, not a proof.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

1. **Scalar residual false proof.** Replacing the Fejer residual by $O(D/H)$ without product-counting the Fejer spikes is invalid. R5-Full avoids this by using $K_H$ and $W_\Delta$.

2. **Integer spike obstruction.** If $X/d$ or $(X/d+\rho)/4$ is integral, $K_H$ reaches $H+1$. R5 handles this through $W_\Delta(0)=1$ and divisor counting.

3. **M2 weighted Cauchy sign loss.** Weighted $h$-Cauchy has acceptable diagonal but replaces $C_h$ by $|C_h|^2$, retaining only odd $h$. This blocks that normalization from exploiting the frequency character.

4. **M2 unweighted Cauchy diagonal blowup.** Unweighted $h$-Cauchy gives $D H_D=D^2X^{-1/4}$, which is $X^{3/4}$ at $D=X^{1/2}$, above the squared target.

5. **Spatial Cauchy for M1.** Cauchy over $d$ preserves $h$-side information longer but creates a diagonal of size $D^2$, which is $X$ at the top block. This is too large without substantial off-diagonal cancellation.

6. **Operator-norm character blindness.** If $\chi_4(d)$ enters only as diagonal unitary conjugation and the proof then uses an operator norm or absolute-value matrix, the character is erased. This is a bounded-scope diagnostic, not a global no-go theorem.

7. **H13 full-rank trap.** The dual phase $\sqrt{Xhm}$ has zero Hessian determinant, so generic full-rank stationary phase/decoupling is unavailable.

8. **Li--Yang endpoint mismatch.** Structural phase similarity is not theorem applicability. The height range audited so far falls short of $D X^{-1/4}$ at the endpoint.
