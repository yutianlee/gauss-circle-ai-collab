## Detailed reasoning

### M2 Cauchy-Schwarz Normalizations

We analyze the standard Cauchy--Schwarz routes over the frequency variable $h$ for the second main term:
$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X) = 4 \sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} \alpha_{h,H_D} C_h B_h(D;X),
$$
where $C_h = e(h/4) - e(3h/4)$ and $B_h(D;X) = \sum_{d \asymp D} w_D(d) e(hX/(4d))$. Note that $C_h = 2i\chi_4(h)$ for odd $h$, and $0$ for even $h$. The target is $|\mathcal M_2(D;X)| \ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}$, which requires a squared target of $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$.

**1. Unweighted Normalization:**
If we extract the scalar factor without weights, we have:
$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2 \le 16 \left( \sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} 1 \right) \left( \sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} |\alpha_{h,H_D}|^2 |C_h|^2 |B_h(D;X)|^2 \right).
$$
The scalar factor is $\asymp H_D$. Expanding $|B_h|^2$, the resulting kernel is:
$$
K_{2;d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|^2)} = 4 w_D(d_1) w_D(d_2) \sum_{\substack{1 \le |h| \le H_D \\ 2 \nmid h}} |\alpha_{h,H_D}|^2 e\left( \frac{hX}{4} \left( \frac{1}{d_1} - \frac{1}{d_2} \right) \right).
$$
The diagonal contribution occurs when $d_1 = d_2$. Since $\sum_h |\alpha_h|^2 \asymp 1$, the diagonal of the kernel is $\asymp 1$. Summing over $d \asymp D$ gives a total diagonal sum of $D$. Multiplying by the scalar factor $H_D$, the total diagonal contribution to $|\mathcal M_2|^2$ is:
$$
\mathcal S_{\rm diag}^{({\rm unweighted})} \asymp H_D D \asymp D^2 X^{-1/4}.
$$
At the endpoint $D \asymp X^{1/2}$, this diagonal size is $X^{3/4}$, which exceeds the squared target $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$. Thus, the unweighted normalization fails on the diagonal.

**2. Weighted Normalization:**
If we extract the scalar factor with weights $|\alpha_h|$, we have:
$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2 \le 16 \left( \sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} |\alpha_{h,H_D}| \right) \left( \sum_{1 \le |h| \le H_D} |\alpha_{h,H_D}| |C_h|^2 |B_h(D;X)|^2 \right).
$$
The scalar factor is $L = \sum |\alpha_h| \asymp \log H_D$. The resulting kernel is:
$$
K_{2;d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)} = 4 w_D(d_1) w_D(d_2) \sum_{\substack{1 \le |h| \le H_D \\ 2 \nmid h}} |\alpha_{h,H_D}| e\left( \frac{hX}{4} \left( \frac{1}{d_1} - \frac{1}{d_2} \right) \right).
$$
The diagonal contribution ($d_1 = d_2$) of the kernel is $\sum |\alpha_h| \asymp \log H_D$. Summing over $d \asymp D$ gives $D \log H_D$. Multiplying by the scalar factor $L \asymp \log H_D$, the total diagonal contribution to $|\mathcal M_2|^2$ is:
$$
\mathcal S_{\rm diag}^{({\rm weighted})} \asymp D \log^2 H_D.
$$
At the endpoint $D \asymp X^{1/2}$, this is $X^{1/2} \log^2 X \ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}$. Thus, the weighted normalization is viable for the diagonal. However, the kernel replaces $C_h$ with $|C_h|^2 = 4$, removing the sign of $\chi_4(h)$ in this Cauchy--Schwarz route and leaving only odd-frequency support. This is the `M2-CS-Character-Blindness-R22` diagnostic.

### M2 Fourth-Moment Classification

To preserve the sign of $\chi_4(h)$, a useful route should avoid forming $|C_h|^2$. We expand the exact fourth moment:
$$
\mathcal Q_4(D;X) = \sum_{h_1, h_2, h_3, h_4} \alpha_{h_1} \overline{\alpha_{h_2}} \alpha_{h_3} \overline{\alpha_{h_4}} C_{h_1} \overline{C_{h_2}} C_{h_3} \overline{C_{h_4}} \sum_{d_1, d_2, d_3, d_4} w_D(\vec{d}) e\left( \frac{X}{4} \left( \frac{h_1}{d_1} - \frac{h_2}{d_2} + \frac{h_3}{d_3} - \frac{h_4}{d_4} \right) \right).
$$
The character factor is $C_{h_1} \overline{C_{h_2}} C_{h_3} \overline{C_{h_4}} = 16 \chi_4(h_1 h_2 h_3 h_4)$ for odd frequencies. The target bound is $|\mathcal Q_4(D;X)| \ll_\epsilon X^{1+\epsilon}$, which implies $\mathcal M_2 \ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}$ by fourth-root extraction. We classify the exact diagonal configurations where the phase vanishes: $\frac{h_1}{d_1} - \frac{h_2}{d_2} + \frac{h_3}{d_3} - \frac{h_4}{d_4} = 0$.

**1. Pair-Swapped Diagonal:**
Configurations where $(h_1, d_1) = (h_2, d_2)$ and $(h_3, d_3) = (h_4, d_4)$ (or the symmetric swap). The contribution is bounded by:
$$
\sum_{h_1, d_1} |\alpha_{h_1}|^2 |C_{h_1}|^2 \sum_{h_3, d_3} |\alpha_{h_3}|^2 |C_{h_3}|^2 \asymp D^2 \ll X \le X^{1+\epsilon}.
$$

**2. Semi-Diagonal:**
Configurations where $d_1 = d_2 = d_3 = d_4 = d$ and $h_1 - h_2 + h_3 - h_4 = 0$. The contribution is bounded by:
$$
\sum_d w_D(d)^4 \sum_{h_1 - h_2 + h_3 - h_4 = 0} \alpha_{h_1} \overline{\alpha_{h_2}} \alpha_{h_3} \overline{\alpha_{h_4}} 16 \chi_4(h_1 h_2 h_3 h_4).
$$
The inner sum over $h$ is the $L^4$ norm of the Fourier polynomial $\sum_h \alpha_h C_h e(hx)$. Since $\alpha_h \asymp 1/h$, this $L^4$ norm is uniformly bounded by $O(1)$. Thus, the total contribution is $\ll D \ll X^{1/2} \le X^{1+\epsilon}$.

**3. Denominator-Paired Subfamilies:**
Configurations where $d_1 = d_2$ and $d_3 = d_4$, but $h_1 \neq h_2$. The phase condition reduces to $(h_1 - h_2) d_3 = -(h_3 - h_4) d_1$. Let $k_1 = h_1 - h_2$ and $k_2 = h_3 - h_4$. Then $k_1 d_3 = -k_2 d_1$. The number of solutions, weighted by the $1/h$ coefficients, is bounded by:
$$
\sum_{d_1, d_3} \sum_{c \neq 0} \sum_{h_1, h_3} \frac{1}{h_1 |h_1 - ac| h_3 |h_3 + bc|} \ll \sum_{d_1, d_3} \frac{\log^2 D}{a b},
$$
where $a = d_1/\gcd(d_1, d_3)$ and $b = d_3/\gcd(d_1, d_3)$. Summing over $d_1, d_3 \asymp D$ gives $\ll D \log^3 D \ll X^{1/2+\epsilon} \le X^{1+\epsilon}$.

Conclusion: the displayed exact diagonal families of the fourth moment are compatible with an $X^{1+\epsilon}$ target. This does not yet exhaust all exact-resonance or near-collision families. The remaining work is to classify every solution to the cleared denominator equation and then bound the near-collision configurations at the same scale.

### Q1-Spectral and H12 Bounded Scope

We formalize the bounded scope of the character-blindness diagnostics. Let $\mathcal V_D = \ell^2(\mathcal D_{\rm odd})$ and $U = \operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(d))$. Since $\chi_4(d) \in \{\pm 1\}$ on odd $d$, $U$ is a unitary involution ($U^* U = I$).

For any matrix $K$, the operator norm is unitarily invariant:
$$
\|U^* K U\|_{\rm op} = \sup_{\|v\|=1} \|U^* K U v\| = \sup_{\|w\|=1} \|K w\| = \|K\|_{\rm op}.
$$
Similarly, for pure cyclic traces:
$$
\operatorname{Tr}((U^* K U)^m) = \operatorname{Tr}(U^* K^m U) = \operatorname{Tr}(K^m U U^*) = \operatorname{Tr}(K^m).
$$
**Blocked Methods:** Operator norm, Frobenius norm, spectral radius, Schur/Gershgorin bounds, absolute-value matrices, and pure cyclic traces applied *after* diagonal unitary conjugation.
**Not Blocked:** Direct signed bilinear forms $\langle w, U^* K U w \rangle$, open-path moments (like the fourth moment $\mathcal Q_4$), cross-residue statistics (like CRI), and non-conjugacy traces $\operatorname{Tr}(U K_1 U K_2)$ where $K_1 \neq K_2$.

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

We propose the **Cross-Residue Interference (CRI)** statistic as the primary non-conjugacy exploratory target for $\mathcal M_2$. By splitting the frequency sum into residue classes modulo 4, we have $\mathcal M_2 = 8i(\Sigma_1 - \Sigma_3)$. The squared magnitude is:
$$
|\mathcal M_2|^2 = 64 \left( |\Sigma_1|^2 + |\Sigma_3|^2 - 2 \Re(\Sigma_1 \overline{\Sigma_3}) \right).
$$
Standard Cauchy--Schwarz bounds $|\Sigma_1|^2$ and $|\Sigma_3|^2$ individually, ignoring the cross-term. The CRI statistic $\mathcal S_{\rm CRI} = \Sigma_1 \overline{\Sigma_3}$ explicitly captures the interference between the residue classes. If the phases of $\Sigma_1$ and $\Sigma_3$ are highly correlated due to the smooth dependence of the phase $e(hX/(4d))$ on $h$, the cross-term $2 \Re(\Sigma_1 \overline{\Sigma_3})$ may cancel the diagonal terms $|\Sigma_1|^2 + |\Sigma_3|^2$, yielding the required $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$ bound without requiring each individual residue class to achieve that bound. This avoids the $|C_h|^2$ sign loss and the $U^* K U$ operator-norm blindness.

## Local audit and calibration addendum

This addendum records the repository-side audit applied after the first A2 draft. The purpose is to separate what is actually proved by the displayed algebra from what remains a proposed research target. The initial draft correctly identified two Cauchy--Schwarz normalizations and it usefully wrote down the signed fourth moment, but it overstated the fourth-moment diagonal classification. The corrected status is as follows. The unweighted $h$-Cauchy diagonal failure is a route-specific obstruction. The weighted $|\alpha_h|$ Cauchy diagonal estimate is a route-specific success that discards the $\chi_4(h)$ sign. The pair-swapped, semi-diagonal, and denominator-paired fourth-moment families are bounded examples of exact resonances, not a proof that every exact resonance has been classified.

For the second moment, the normalization issue is precise. Write
$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)=4\sum_h \alpha_h C_h B_h(D;X),
\qquad
B_h(D;X)=\sum_{d\asymp D} w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$
The unweighted Cauchy--Schwarz route is
$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2
\le 16\Big(\sum_{|h|\le H_D}1\Big)
\Big(\sum_{|h|\le H_D}|\alpha_h|^2 |C_h|^2 |B_h(D;X)|^2\Big).
$$
On the diagonal $d_1=d_2$, the inner expanded kernel contributes $\asymp D$ because $\sum_h |\alpha_h|^2\asymp 1$ on the Vaaler range. The prefactor is $\asymp H_D$, hence the diagonal diagnostic is $D H_D$. With the endpoint height $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$, this becomes $D^2 X^{-1/4}$ and reaches $X^{3/4}$ when $D\asymp X^{1/2}$. This is larger than the squared target $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$. The claim is not that the original signed $\mathcal M_2$ has a lower bound of this size; it is that this particular upper-bound route has already spent too much mass on its own diagonal.

The weighted Cauchy--Schwarz route instead uses
$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2
\le 16\Big(\sum_{|h|\le H_D}|\alpha_h|\Big)
\Big(\sum_{|h|\le H_D}|\alpha_h||C_h|^2 |B_h(D;X)|^2\Big).
$$
Its diagonal is $D(\sum|\alpha_h|)^2\asymp D\log^2 H_D$, which is acceptable at $D\asymp X^{1/2}$. The price is structural: $C_h\overline{C_h}=|C_h|^2$ turns the factor $2i\chi_4(h)$ into a nonnegative odd-frequency selector. Thus the weighted route is a valid diagnostic and perhaps a useful auxiliary estimate, but it no longer measures the residue-class cancellation that made $\mathcal M_2$ interesting. This is why the obstruction is best phrased as character blindness of that normalization, not as a general theorem forbidding all Cauchy--Schwarz use.

For the fourth moment, the object to study is
$$
\mathcal Q_4(D;X)=
\sum_{\vec h,\vec d}
\alpha_{h_1}\overline{\alpha_{h_2}}\alpha_{h_3}\overline{\alpha_{h_4}}
C_{h_1}\overline{C_{h_2}}C_{h_3}\overline{C_{h_4}}
w_D(\vec d)
e\left({X\over4}
\left({h_1\over d_1}-{h_2\over d_2}+{h_3\over d_3}-{h_4\over d_4}\right)\right).
$$
Exact resonances satisfy
$$
{h_1\over d_1}-{h_2\over d_2}+{h_3\over d_3}-{h_4\over d_4}=0.
$$
After clearing denominators, the integer equation is
$$
N=h_1d_2d_3d_4-h_2d_1d_3d_4+h_3d_1d_2d_4-h_4d_1d_2d_3=0.
$$
A complete diagonal theorem needs to count all solutions of this equation with the dyadic restrictions, the actual Vaaler coefficient weights, and the odd-frequency character factors. The displayed pair-swapped family is only the most obvious subvariety. The semi-diagonal $d_1=d_2=d_3=d_4$ reduces to the additive equation $h_1-h_2+h_3-h_4=0$ and is controlled by the $L^4$ norm of the coefficient polynomial. The denominator-paired family $d_1=d_2$, $d_3=d_4$ reduces to $(h_1-h_2)d_3=-(h_3-h_4)d_1$. Those calculations are useful because they show no immediate diagonal explosion in the most natural families. They do not prove that a skew family, a high-gcd family, or a near-duplicate denominator family cannot dominate.

The denominator-paired estimate also has hidden assumptions that should be made explicit before it is promoted. If $g=(d_1,d_3)$, $d_1=ga$, and $d_3=gb$ with $(a,b)=1$, the equation becomes $b(h_1-h_2)=-a(h_3-h_4)$. A standard parameterization sets $h_1-h_2=ac$ and $h_3-h_4=-bc$. The coefficient sum then contains terms of the form
$$
\sum_{h_1,h_3,c}
{1\over |h_1|\,|h_1-ac|\,|h_3|\,|h_3+bc|}.
$$
Bounding this by a harmless logarithmic factor requires care near zeros, at truncation endpoints, and when $|ac|$ or $|bc|$ approaches the height $H_D$. The current draft suppresses these endpoint cases. A reliable proof should state the allowed frequency signs, exclude zero frequencies, track the parity restriction, and prove a uniform bound in $a,b,c,H_D$. Until then, the denominator-paired line is best kept as a derived-under-assumptions calculation.

There is also a coefficient normalization issue in the phrase "diagonal contribution." In the pair-swapped family, the absolute contribution is naturally bounded by
$$
\left(\sum_{|h|\le H_D}|\alpha_h|^2 |C_h|^2\right)^2
\left(\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|^2\right)^2
\ll D^2.
$$
This estimate is insensitive to the sign character because the family pairs each frequency with its conjugate. It is still useful as a mass check, but it does not test the sign-preserving mechanism. The semi-diagonal family is different: it keeps four coefficients in a single denominator fiber and therefore measures an additive energy of the $h$-coefficients. If the coefficients were truncated sharply, endpoint artifacts could change the constant and produce larger logarithms. Smooth Vaaler coefficients should keep this controlled, but a proof should specify whether it uses $\ell^2$ convolution, the Hausdorff-Young inequality, or a direct divisor-style count. The current draft asserts bounded $L^4$ norm; the next proof draft should display the exact inequality.

The exact-resonance taxonomy can be organized by denominator partitions. The pair-swapped case corresponds to two identical rational atoms on the positive and negative sides. The semi-diagonal case corresponds to a single denominator block of size four. The denominator-paired case corresponds to two denominator blocks of size two, but with nontrivial frequency differences. Missing cases include three-one denominator patterns, two-one-one patterns with one repeated denominator, and four distinct denominators with a nontrivial rational relation. For four distinct denominators, the cleared equation $N=0$ can still hold because the frequencies vary up to $H_D$. A crude count over $(d_1,d_2,d_3,d_4)$ followed by solving for one $h_i$ may lose too much, since the denominator product is about $D^4$ while the allowed numerator scale is only $H_DD^3$. This tension is exactly where arithmetic structure may help, but the response should not claim that the issue has already been settled.

One possible proof route is to group rational values $h/d$ rather than denominator tuples. Let
$$
r(q)=\sum_{\substack{h,d:\\ h/d=q}}\alpha_h C_h w_D(d)
$$
for rationals $q$ in the dyadic window. Then the exact fourth-moment diagonal is a signed additive energy
$$
\sum_{q_1-q_2+q_3-q_4=0}
r(q_1)\overline{r(q_2)}r(q_3)\overline{r(q_4)}.
$$
This formulation makes the proof obligation clearer: either establish an additive-energy estimate for the rational set $\{h/d\}$ with these weights, or exploit cancellation from the $\chi_4$ factors inside that energy. It also reveals why absolute-value majorization can be dangerous. If one replaces $r(q)$ by $|r(q)|$, all residue-class cancellation disappears and the bound may revert to a larger additive energy problem. A sign-preserving theorem should state where the signs enter and which step saves power or logarithms.

For near-collisions, a practical dyadic decomposition is to split by $|N|\sim T$ after clearing denominators. The phase derivative in $X$ is $N/(4d_1d_2d_3d_4)$, so the band $T\ll D^4/X$ is effectively nonoscillatory in a single $X$-block, while $T\gg D^4/X$ may be accessible to summation by parts or spacing estimates. At the endpoint, $D^4/X\asymp X$, so the nonoscillatory band is quite large. This explains why fourth-moment numerics should report more than the exact $N=0$ mass. A table over logarithmic $T$-bins, with separate signed and absolute totals, will show whether the route is facing a counting problem, a cancellation problem, or both. If the signed totals are small but the absolute totals are large, the route likely needs a cancellation theorem. If both totals are large, the proposed moment is probably the wrong object or needs a sharper weight.

The CRI and fourth-moment routes should also be cross-checked against each other. CRI studies the two residue classes $h\equiv1,3\pmod4$ at second-moment level. The fourth moment studies products of four residue classes through $\chi_4(h_1h_2h_3h_4)$. If CRI numerics show little correlation between $\Sigma_1$ and $\Sigma_3$, then a fourth-moment proof relying on residue-class balancing becomes less plausible, though still logically open. Conversely, if CRI shows strong cancellation, the fourth-moment route should explain that cancellation algebraically in its exact or near-exact bins. This gives a useful consistency test: a successful Round 23 continuation should not treat CRI as a separate slogan, but should connect it to the sign pattern in $\mathcal Q_4$.

Near-collisions are the main unresolved fourth-moment issue. In the exponential sum, exact $N=0$ is not the only dangerous region. Since the phase is $e((X/4)F)$ with
$$

$$
small nonzero $F$ can fail to oscillate over the dyadic block. After clearing denominators, $F=N/(d_1d_2d_3d_4)$. For $d_i\asymp D$, the phase increment is roughly $X N/D^4$. At the endpoint $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, this is about $N/X$. Thus many integers $N$ with $|N|\lesssim X$ may be near-resonant rather than oscillatory. Any fourth-moment route therefore needs a count not just for $N=0$, but for short bands $|N|\le T$ with coefficient weights. $$
we have
$$
\mathcal M_2=8i(\Sigma_1-\Sigma_3).
$$
Therefore a bound on $\Sigma_1\overline{\Sigma_3}$ by itself is not enough. The quantity that matters is the combined identity
$$
|\mathcal M_2|^2=64\left(|\Sigma_1|^2+|\Sigma_3|^2
-2\Re(\Sigma_1\overline{\Sigma_3})\right).
$$
If $\Re(\Sigma_1\overline{\Sigma_3})$ nearly matches half the sum of the two diagonal magnitudes, the signed difference is small. If the cross term is small or has the wrong sign, CRI does not help. Thus the numerical falsification metric should be normalized as
$$
R_{\rm CRI}={|\Sigma_1-\Sigma_3|^2\over |\Sigma_1|^2+|\Sigma_3|^2}.
$$
Values near $1$ indicate little useful cancellation; values substantially below $1$ indicate residue-class interference worth analyzing.

## Toy-Model Computations

Consider a toy model for the CRI statistic where the inner sums $B_h = \sum_d w_D(d) e(hX/(4d))$ are treated as pseudo-random variables. If $B_h$ for $h \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ are uncorrelated with $B_h$ for $h \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, then the cross-term $\Re(\Sigma_1 \overline{\Sigma_3})$ will be small compared to the diagonal terms $|\Sigma_1|^2 + |\Sigma_3|^2$. The phase difference is $X/(2d) \asymp X/D \asymp X^{1/2}$, which wraps around many times, but the arithmetic structure of the rational approximations may preserve correlation. The numerical falsification test $|\Sigma_1 - \Sigma_3|^2 / (|\Sigma_1|^2 + |\Sigma_3|^2)$ will directly measure this correlation.
