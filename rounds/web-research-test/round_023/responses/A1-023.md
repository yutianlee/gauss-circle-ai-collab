### 1. H4-R23: source-normalized finite Vaaler approximation

Use

$$
e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}.
$$

The rendered Vaaler PDF gives the required source map:

The OCR on the rendered pages is imperfect, but the rendered PDF confirms the displayed structure:

- Theorem 6 states the Fourier transform of $J$ as

$$
\widehat J(t)
=
\pi t(1-|t|)\cot(\pi t)+|t|,
\qquad 0<|t|<1,
$$

with support on $|t|\le1$ and evenness/nonnegativity statements.

- Section 7 defines $i_N,j_N,k_N$ as periodic trigonometric polynomials, with $k_N$ the periodic Fejer kernel.

- Theorem 18 gives

$$
|\psi*j_N(x)-\psi(x)|
\le
(2N+2)^{-1}k_N(x).
$$

In repository notation set

$$
H=N,
$$

and

$$
K_H(t)=k_N(t)
=
\sum_{|k|\le H}
\left(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\right)e(kt)
=
\frac1{H+1}
\left(
\frac{\sin \pi(H+1)t}{\sin \pi t}
\right)^2.
$$

Define

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u,
\qquad 0<u<1.
$$

Then

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
\qquad 1\le |h|\le H.
$$

The finite approximation to the floor-compatible sawtooth should be entered as:

**H4-R23.** For every integer $H\ge1$ and every real $t$,

$$
\psi_F(t)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)
+
R_H^F(t),
$$

with

$$
|R_H^F(t)|
\le
\frac1{2H+2}K_H(t).
$$

This is an imported theorem dependency from Vaaler plus an endpoint-convention conversion. The source anchors are now sufficiently exact for the proof draft, but the final commit should still compare the PDF image against the formula to avoid OCR sign errors.

### 2. Floor-compatible endpoint conversion

Vaaler's periodic sawtooth is centered at discontinuities. Write

$$
\psi_C(t)
=
\begin{cases}
t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12,&t\notin\mathbb Z,\\
0,&t\in\mathbb Z.
\end{cases}
$$

The arithmetic identity uses

$$
\psi_F(t)=t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12
$$

for all real $t$, hence

$$
\psi_F(n)=-\frac12.
$$

At nonintegers, $\psi_F=\psi_C$. At integers, the Vaaler polynomial

$$
V_H(t)=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)
$$

satisfies

$$
V_H(n)=0.
$$

Indeed, $e(hn)=1$ and $\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$ while $\alpha_{h,H}$ is purely imaginary for $h>0$, so the $h$ and $-h$ terms cancel.

Also,

$$
K_H(0)=H+1,
$$

and therefore

$$
\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12.
$$

Thus the same Fejer residual majorant covers the floor-compatible half-jump:

$$
|\psi_F(n)-V_H(n)|=\frac12.
$$

This endpoint conversion is part of H4-R23, not an optional convention note.

### 3. Alpha-Conjugacy-R23

For $h>0$,

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}
=
\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right).
$$

Since $\Phi$ is real-valued on $(0,1)$,

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i(-h)}
=
-\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right)
=
\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\boxed{\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.}
$$

The relation $\alpha_{-h,H}=-\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$ is false and should be treated as a regression-test failure in numerical code.

### 4. Phi-Asymmetry-R23

The coefficient function is

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u.
$$

The endpoint limits are

$$
\Phi(0^+)=1,
\qquad
\Phi(1^-)=0.
$$

Moreover,

$$
\Phi(1-u)
=
\pi(1-u)u\cot(\pi(1-u))+(1-u)
=
-\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+1-u.
$$

Thus

$$
\Phi(u)+\Phi(1-u)=1.
$$

Consequently $\Phi(1-u)=\Phi(u)$ only when $\Phi(u)=1/2$, not identically. In particular, assuming symmetry about $u=1/2$ is an algebra error. This should be inserted as a small lemma because it is directly relevant to coefficient implementation.

### 5. R5-Full-R23: Fejer residual product-count bound

State the lemma as follows.

**R5-Full-R23.** Let $X\ge2$ be real, let

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2},
$$

and let $H$ be an integer satisfying

$$
c_0D X^{-1/4}\le H\le C_0D X^{-1/4}
$$

for fixed constants $0<c_0\le C_0$. Let $w_D$ be supported on $d\asymp D$ with $|w_D(d)|\le C_w$. Then for every $\epsilon>0$,

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}
|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon},
$$

and for $\rho\in\{1,3\}$,

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}
|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

After summing over dyadic blocks with bounded overlap, the total Vaaler residual contribution from H3 is

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

#### 5.1 Fejer pointwise bound

The normalized Fejer kernel satisfies

$$
K_H(t)\ll
\min\left(H,\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\right),
$$

where

$$
\|t\|=\min_{m\in\mathbb Z}|t-m|.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

Set

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}.
$$

Since $H\asymp D X^{-1/4}$,

$$
\Delta\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

Use the exact-resonance-safe cap

$$
W_\Delta(u)=
\begin{cases}
1,&u=0,\\
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{u^2}\right),&u\ne0.
\end{cases}
$$

This avoids singular notation at exact Fejer spikes.

#### 5.2 First residual leg

For each $d$, choose $m=m(d)$ to be a nearest integer to $X/d$. In a tie, choose the smaller nearest integer. The tie choice does not affect any estimate.

Then

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
=
\left|\frac Xd-m\right|
=
\frac{|X-md|}{d}.
$$

Since $d\asymp D$,

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
\asymp
\frac{|X-md|}{D}.
$$

Hence

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)
\ll
W_\Delta(X-md).
$$

Group the terms by

$$
n=md.
$$

For large $X$ in the active range, $m>0$ and $n\asymp X$. For small $X$, the total number of terms is bounded by an absolute constant after adjusting the implied constant. The number of representations of a positive integer $n$ as $md$ is at most $\tau(n)$, so

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}
|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll
\sum_{n}
\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n).
$$

By the standard divisor bound

$$
\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon,
$$

and since all relevant $n$ are $O(X+D)\ll X$ for large $X$,

$$
\sum_{n}
\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n)
\ll_\epsilon
X^\epsilon
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}W_\Delta(X-n).
$$

The last sum is

$$
\ll \Delta+1.
$$

Indeed, the central range $|X-n|\le\Delta$ contributes $O(\Delta+1)$, while the dyadic annuli $2^j\Delta<|X-n|\le2^{j+1}\Delta$ contribute

$$
O(2^j\Delta)\cdot O(2^{-2j})
=
O(2^{-j}\Delta).
$$

Thus the first residual leg is

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon(\Delta+1)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Exact resonance is harmless. If $X/d\in\mathbb Z$, then $K_H(X/d)=H+1$, and

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)\asymp1,
$$

which is precisely covered by $W_\Delta(0)=1$.

#### 5.3 Shifted second residual legs

For $\rho\in\{1,3\}$, choose $m=m_\rho(d)$ nearest to

$$
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}.
$$

Then

$$
\left\|
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}
\right\|
=
\frac{|X/d+\rho-4m|}{4}
=
\frac{|X-d(4m-\rho)|}{4d}.
$$

Set

$$
\ell=4m-\rho.
$$

Then

$$
\ell\equiv -\rho\pmod 4.
$$

For large $X$ and active $d\le 2X^{1/2}$,

$$
\ell=\frac Xd+O(1)\gg X^{1/2},
$$

so $\ell>0$. More explicitly, if $X\ge X_0$ for a fixed sufficiently large $X_0$, then $X/d\ge cX^{1/2}$ on the active range and the $O(1)$ nearest-integer error cannot make $\ell$ nonpositive. The finitely many cases $2\le X<X_0$ are absorbed into the implied constant.

Using the Fejer bound,

$$
\frac1H
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll
W_{C\Delta}(X-d\ell),
$$

where $C$ is an absolute constant from the factor $4d$ in the denominator. Replacing $\Delta$ by $C\Delta$ has no effect on the exponent.

Group by

$$
n=d\ell.
$$

The congruence condition $\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4$ only reduces multiplicity, so the number of admissible representations is at most $\tau(n)$. The same argument gives

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}
|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proves the shifted residual estimate.

#### 5.4 Short blocks and dyadic bounded overlap

For $D<X^{1/4}$, do not apply Vaaler. Since $|\psi_F(t)|\le 1/2$ for every noninteger $t$ and $|\psi_F(n)|=1/2$ at integers, each short block contributes $O(D)$. Summing dyadically over $D<X^{1/4}$ gives

$$
\sum_{D<X^{1/4}}O(D)
\ll X^{1/4}
$$

up to a bounded-overlap or logarithmic factor, absorbed into $X^\epsilon$.

For long blocks, use a smooth dyadic partition with bounded overlap. Since R5 uses $|w_D|$, no cancellation or sign of the partition is required. Summing $O(\log X)$ active blocks absorbs into $X^\epsilon$.

### 6. Bridge-R23: conditional theorem

**Bridge-R23.** Assume:

1. H1--H3, the balanced hyperbola and floor-compatible sawtooth reduction;
2. H4-R23, the finite Vaaler approximation with Fejer residual;
3. R5-Full-R23, the total Fejer residual bound;
4. M9-R23, the main-term estimate

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X),\mathcal M_2(D;X)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}
$$

uniformly over active dyadic $D$.

Then

$$
P(X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Proof sketch: H3 gives the two sawtooth legs plus $O(1)$. Short blocks are $O(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$. On active blocks, H4 decomposes each sawtooth into a Vaaler polynomial plus residual. R5-Full bounds all residuals by $O_\epsilon(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$. The remaining polynomial pieces are exactly $\mathcal M_1(D;X)$ and $\mathcal M_2(D;X)$ after dyadic localization. M9 bounds these. The bounded number of dyadic blocks contributes only logarithms, absorbed into $X^\epsilon$.

This bridge is a reduction, not a solution. The theorem cannot be promoted to an exponent result unless M9 is proved.

### 8. M9-Pair-R23: implementation formulas for real weights

Assume $w_D$ is real-valued and the frequency range is symmetric in $h$. Then

$$
A_{-h}(D;X)=\overline{A_h(D;X)},
\qquad
B_{-h}(D;X)=\overline{B_h(D;X)}.
$$

Using $\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$, for $h>0$,

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right).
$$

Therefore

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
\frac4\pi
\sum_{1\le h\le H_D}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\operatorname{Im}A_h(D;X).
$$

For the second leg,

$$
e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=2i\chi_4(h),
$$

so only odd $h$ contribute. A direct pair calculation gives

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
-\frac8\pi
\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\chi_4(h)\operatorname{Re}B_h(D;X).
$$

These are implementation formulas, not the canonical definitions. Numerical code should regress against the raw two-sided complex sums. If complex $w_D$ or asymmetric frequency cutoffs are used, return to the raw definitions.

### 9. M9b-ShiftedF-R23: preferred theorem-extension formulation

The $\chi_4(h)$ form of M9b is algebraically correct but awkward for theorem comparison because $\chi_4(h)$ is a periodic discontinuous weight. The safer formulation keeps the two shifted phases:

$$
e(hX/(4d))(e(h/4)-e(3h/4))
=
e\left(h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac14\right)\right)
-
e\left(h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac34\right)\right).
$$

Write $d=Dz$, $T=X$, and $M=D$. Then

$$
h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac{\rho}{4}\right)
=
\frac{hT}{M}
\left(
\frac1{4z}+\frac{\rho M}{4T}
\right),
\qquad \rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

Thus define

$$
F_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac1{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X}.
$$

The derivatives are

$$
F'_{\rho,D}(z)=-\frac1{4z^2},
$$

$$
F''_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac1{2z^3},
$$

$$
F'''_{\rho,D}(z)=-\frac3{2z^4}.
$$

Therefore

$$
F'F'''-3(F'')^2
=
\frac3{8z^6}-\frac3{4z^6}
=
-\frac3{8z^6}.
$$

The derivative nondegeneracy is uniform on $z\in[1,2]$, and the additive shift does not affect derivatives. However, theorem-level applicability remains open. A theorem used for M9b needs to allow:

1. $F_{\rho,D}$ depending on $D,X$ through a small additive constant;
2. actual Vaaler $h$ weights $\alpha_{h,H_D}$, or a dyadic decomposition that absorbs them with correct prefactors;
3. endpoint height $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$;
4. smooth dyadic $d$ weights;
5. positive/negative frequency pairing or separate positive frequency treatment;
6. no uncontrolled bounded-variation loss from replacing the shifted phase by $\chi_4(h)$.

The equivalent fractional-frequency representation after $h=4q+r$ is

$$
e(hX/(4d))=e((q+r/4)X/d),
\qquad r\in\{1,3\}.
$$

This is useful for finite tests, but the shifted-$F$ formulation is cleaner for comparison with Li--Yang/Bombieri--Iwaniec theorem statements.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

1. **Exact first-leg Fejer resonance.** If $X/d\in\mathbb Z$, then $K_H(X/d)=H+1$ and $(1/H)K_H(X/d)\asymp1$. Such resonances are counted by divisor multiplicity and do not exceed the R5 bound.

2. **Exact shifted resonance.** If

$$
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\in\mathbb Z,
$$

then

$$
X=d(4m-\rho).
$$

The complementary factor $\ell=4m-\rho$ is restricted to a fixed congruence class modulo $4$, so representation multiplicity is still at most $\tau(n)$.

3. **Divisor-rich $X$.** Highly composite $X$ increases exact resonance count only by $\tau(X)\ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon$, not by a power large enough to threaten $X^{1/4+\epsilon}$.

4. **Near-resonance clusters.** The Fejer kernel can be large when $X/d$ is near an integer, but R5 converts this into a sum over $W_\Delta(X-n)$ with total mass $O(\Delta)$.

5. **Endpoint Li--Yang mismatch.** At $D=X^{1/2}$, the Vaaler height is $H=X^{1/4}$. The audited Li--Yang ranges stop below this height, so quoting Li--Yang directly would be a theorem-application error.

6. **Operator-norm character erasure.** If the $\chi_4(d)$ signs enter a matrix estimate only through $U^*KU$ with $U=\operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(d))$, then operator norms, Frobenius norms, Schur bounds, Gershgorin bounds, absolute-value matrices, and pure cyclic traces do not see the character. This blocks a class of proof attempts, not all signed bilinear methods.

7. **H13 dual erasure.** If the B-process-first route transfers $\chi_4(d)$ to $\chi_4(m)$ but the first serious estimate again uses diagonal-unitary norm extraction, then the transform has not preserved useful sign information. H13 therefore needs a non-conjugacy signed statistic after transformation.

8. **$\Phi$ symmetry bug.** Any code or proof using $\Phi(1-u)=\Phi(u)$ is wrong. The correct identity is $\Phi(u)+\Phi(1-u)=1$.

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

### Alternative A: fourth-moment or open-path statistic for $\mathcal M_2$

The standard $h$-Cauchy route for $\mathcal M_2$ loses $\chi_4(h)$ because

$$
|e(h/4)-e(3h/4)|^2
$$

is only an odd-support indicator up to a scalar. A possible alternative is a fourth-moment or open-path statistic that retains the residues of the four $h$ variables before taking absolute values.

Define, schematically,

$$
\Sigma_\rho(D;X)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\sum_d
w_D(d)
e\left(h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac{\rho}{4}\right)\right),
\qquad \rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

Then

$$
\mathcal M_2=4(\Sigma_1-\Sigma_3).
$$

A signed fourth-moment strategy should not estimate $|\Sigma_1|^2+|\Sigma_3|^2$ separately if the useful cancellation is in

$$
|\Sigma_1-\Sigma_3|^2
=
|\Sigma_1|^2+|\Sigma_3|^2
-
2\operatorname{Re}(\Sigma_1\overline{\Sigma_3}).
$$

The exact fourth-moment expansion after clearing denominators contains phase constraints of the form

$$
h_1d_2d_3d_4+h_2d_1d_3d_4
-
h_3d_1d_2d_4-h_4d_1d_2d_3.
$$

A2's next useful task is to classify diagonal, pair-swapped diagonal, semi-diagonal, denominator-paired, and near-collision terms and state exactly what bound on each class would imply

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Falsification test: if the signed cross term

$$
2\operatorname{Re}(\Sigma_1\overline{\Sigma_3})
$$

is numerically too small or has the wrong sign relative to $|\Sigma_1|^2+|\Sigma_3|^2$ across endpoint blocks, this fourth-moment route is unlikely to help without a new structural input.

### Alternative B: H13 endpoint transform as a bounded falsification test

For

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
\sum_d\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),
$$

splitting $d\bmod4$ and applying Poisson formally transfers $\chi_4$ to a dual Gauss factor. The active stationary dual length is

$$
m\asymp \frac{hX}{D^2}.
$$

At maximal height $h\asymp H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$ and $D=X^\delta$,

$$
m\asymp X^{3/4-\delta}.
$$

Only near $\delta=1/2$ does this match the endpoint scale $m\asymp X^{1/4}$.

The leading dual phase is of square-root type,

$$
\Phi(h,m)\asymp \sqrt{Xhm},
$$

and the continuous Hessian determinant is zero. Therefore generic full-rank two-dimensional stationary phase or decoupling is not applicable.

Falsification test: implement H13 only near $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, with stationary amplitude included. If the first useful norm estimate turns the dual character into a diagonal conjugation $U^*KU$, then H13 does not escape Q1-Spectral blindness. If a non-conjugacy signed statistic survives and is smaller than its absolute comparator, H13 may deserve another focused round.
