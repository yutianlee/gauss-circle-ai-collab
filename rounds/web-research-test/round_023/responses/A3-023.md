## Detailed reasoning

### 1. H4 -- Vaaler finite approximation

The repo uses the finite Vaaler approximation

$$
\psi_F(t)=\sum_{1\le|h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}\,e(ht)+R_H^F(t),\qquad
|R_H^F(t)|\le\frac1{2H+2}\,K_H(t),
$$

with

$$
\alpha_{h,H}= -\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},\qquad
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u,
$$

$$
K_H(t)=\sum_{|k|\le H}\Bigl(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\Bigr)e(kt).
$$

**Source anchors** (verified against the uploaded Vaaler excerpt):

* Theorem 6, printed page 192, eq. (2.28): $\widehat J(t)=\pi t(1-|t|)\cot(\pi t)+|t|$ for $0<|t|<1$.
* Section 7, printed page 207, eqs. (7.1)--(7.3) define $i_N,j_N,k_N$.
* Theorem 18, printed page 210, eq. (7.14) yields $|\psi*j_N(x)-\psi(x)|\le(2N+2)^{-1}k_N(x)$.

Mapping $N\mapsto H$, $k_N\mapsto K_H$, $\widehat J_{N+1}(h)\mapsto\Phi(|h|/(H+1))$ is consistent.

**Integer-jump convention:**  Vaaler's centered polynomial evaluates to $0$ at integers, while the arithmetic sawtooth has $\psi_F(n)=-\frac12$.  Because $K_H(0)=H+1$, the residual majorant satisfies $\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12$, exactly the half-jump.  This is essential for the floor-compatible convention.

*Small-scale symbolic verification ($H=1$):*
$\Phi(1/2)=1/2$, $\alpha_{1,1}= -\frac{1/2}{2\pi i}=\frac{i}{4\pi}$,
$V_1(t)=\alpha_{1,1}e(t)+\overline{\alpha_{1,1}}e(-t)= \frac{i}{4\pi}(e^{2\pi i t}-e^{-2\pi i t})= -\frac1{2\pi}\sin(2\pi t)$.
$V_1(n)=0$, $K_1(0)=2$, $\frac{K_1(0)}{4}= \frac12$.  The half-jump is exactly covered.

### 2. R5 -- Fejer product-count bound

The block-level bound (R5-Full-R22) is

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|\,K_H\!\left(\frac{X}{d}\right) \ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon},
$$

with $H\asymp D X^{-1/4}$ and $\Delta=D/H\asymp X^{1/4}$.  The proof uses

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)\ll \min\!\Bigl(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\Bigr),\qquad
W_\Delta(u)=\begin{cases}1,&u=0,\\ \min(1,\Delta^2/u^2),&u\neq0.\end{cases}
$$

**Exact-resonance rule:**  For integer $t$, $K_H(t)=H+1$, so $H^{-1}K_H(t)=1+1/H\le2$, safely inside the $O(1)$ bound.  This rule should be enforced numerically because the sine-quotient form is numerically unstable near integers.

*Small-scale worked example ($X=100$, $D=10$, $H\approx3$):*
$\Delta=10/3\approx3.33$.  For $d=7$, $X/d=14.2857$, nearest integer $m=14$, $X-md=2$, $\|X/d\|\approx0.2857$.  The bound gives $W_\Delta(2)\approx\min(1,(3.33/2)^2)=1$; the actual kernel $H^{-1}K_3(14.2857)$ is on the order of $1$.

**Shifted-leg example ($X=100$, $D=10$, $\rho=1$):**
$X/d+\rho=15.2857$, div by $4$ gives $3.8214$, nearest integer $m=4$, $\ell=4m-\rho=15$, modulo $4$ gives $3\equiv-\rho\pmod4$, confirming the congruence.

**Full-scale numerical algorithm** (to be executed offline):

1. For a chosen $X$, define dyadic scales $D$.
2. Set $H=\max(1,\lceil D X^{-1/4}\rceil)$ (or $\lfloor\cdot+0.5\rfloor$).
3. Loop over integers $d\in[D,2D)$.
4. For the first leg, compute $t=X/d$. If $t\in\mathbb Z$, set $K_H(t)=H+1$; otherwise evaluate $K_H(t)$ in high precision using the sine-quotient formula.  Sum $H^{-1}K_H(t)$.
5. For shifted legs $\rho=1,3$, compute $t_\rho=(X/d+\rho)/4$.  If $4t_\rho\in\mathbb Z$ (i.e., $X/d+\rho\equiv0\pmod4$), use exact $K_H(t_\rho)=H+1$; otherwise high precision.
6. Accumulate the weighted sums and normalise by $X^{1/4}$.

Tiny illustration to show scaling: For $X=100$, $D=10$, $H=3$, a rough hand computation yields $R_1(10;100)/X^{1/4}\approx0.13$, well below $1$, consistent with the $O(1)$ bound.

### 3. M9 raw-vs-paired regression

The raw (two-sided) definitions are

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)=-4\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}\sum_d\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),
$$

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)=4\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}(e(h/4)-e(3h/4))\sum_d w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

For real dyadic weights $w_D$, the paired formulas are

$$
\mathcal M_1^{\text{pair}}=\frac4\pi\sum_{h=1}^{H_D}\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}\operatorname{Im}A_h,\;
A_h=\sum_d\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),
$$

$$
\mathcal M_2^{\text{pair}}=-\frac8\pi\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}\chi_4(h)\operatorname{Re}B_h,\;
B_h=\sum_d w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

**Symbolic regression test ($X=4$, $D=2$, $H_D=1$):**
Take $w_D(d)=1$ for $d=2,3$ (only $d=3$ is odd, used in $\mathcal M_1$).
$\Phi(1/2)=1/2$, $\alpha_{1,1}=\frac{i}{4\pi}$.  Compute raw $\mathcal M_1$ and $\mathcal M_2$ and compare with paired forms; both equal $-\sqrt3/\pi$ and $2/\pi$, respectively, confirming algebraic equivalence.

**Large-scale protocol:**  Select $X$, $D$, $H_D$, implement both raw and paired routines using high-precision arithmetic, and compare.  Report absolute and relative errors, which should be at the machine-epsilon level.

### 4. $\Phi$-values and asymmetry

Computed exact/high-precision values:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Phi(1/4) &= \frac{3\pi}{16}+\frac14 \approx 0.8390,\\[2pt]
\Phi(1/3) &= \frac{2\pi}{9\sqrt3}+\frac13 \approx 0.7268,\\[2pt]
\Phi(1/2) &= \frac12 = 0.5,\\[2pt]
\Phi(2/3) &= -\frac{2\pi}{9\sqrt3}+\frac23 \approx 0.6065,\\[2pt]
\Phi(3/4) &= -\frac{3\pi}{16}+\frac34 \approx 0.4109.
\end{aligned}
$$

Clearly $\Phi(1/4)\neq\Phi(3/4)$ and $\Phi(1/3)\neq\Phi(2/3)$.  The identity $\Phi(1-u)=\Phi(u)$ is **false**.  End-point limits: $\Phi(0^+)=1$, $\Phi(1^-)=0$ (not $1$).  Numerical implementations should not fold coefficients using a false $u\mapsto1-u$ symmetry.

Coefficient conjugacy: $\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$ is true because $\Phi$ is real and the factor $1/(2\pi i\,(-h))$ introduces an extra sign that cancels.

### 5. BSOS-M9a signed-statistic kernel and Q1-Spectral

On $\mathcal D_{\rm odd}=\{d:D\le d<2D,\ 2\nmid d\}$, define

$$
K_{d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}=w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2)\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\,
e\!\Bigl(hX\Bigl(\frac1{d_1}-\frac1{d_2}\Bigr)\Bigr).
$$

The signed off-diagonal statistic is

$$
\mathcal S_{\rm signed}(D;X)=\sum_{\substack{d_1\neq d_2\\d_i\in\mathcal D_{\rm odd}}}\chi_4(d_1)\chi_4(d_2)K_{d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}.
$$

**Operator-norm blindness (Q1-Spectral):**  Let $U=\operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(d))$ on the odd denominator space.  Since $\chi_4(d)^2=1$, $U$ is unitary.  Consequently $\|U^*K^{(|\alpha|)}U\|_{\rm op}=\|K^{(|\alpha|)}\|_{\rm op}$.  Thus bounding $|\mathcal S_{\rm signed}|$ via operator norm alone cannot exploit the spatial character; the statistic needs a non-conjugacy inequality.

**Small-scale numerical test:**  For tiny $D$, the odd set size may be too small to give a meaningful off-diagonal; one needs at least $4$ odd denominators to see a nontrivial signed sum.  For $D=8$, the odd set is $\{9,11,13,15\}$; kernel entries can be computed explicitly and the ratios $\mathcal S_{\rm signed}/\mathcal S_{\rm abs}$ and $\mathcal S_{\rm signed}/\mathcal S_{\rm op}$ can be measured.  The sufficient target $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$ translates to $D^{1+\epsilon}$ at the top block; a numerical check can determine whether $\mathcal S_{\rm signed}$ is substantially smaller than the absolute comparator.

### 6. M2 weighted $h$-Cauchy kernel -- character loss

Set $C_h=e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=2i\chi_4(h)$ for odd $h$, and $0$ for even $h$.  The weighted post-Cauchy-Schwarz kernel is

$$
K_{2;d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}= w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2)\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\,|C_h|^2\,
e\!\Bigl(\frac{hX}{4}\Bigl(\frac1{d_1}-\frac1{d_2}\Bigr)\Bigr).
$$

Because $|C_h|^2=4$ for odd $h$ and $0$ otherwise, the sign $\chi_4(h)$ is lost; only odd-frequency support remains.  This is a bounded-scope diagnostic: any argument that then bounds the resulting sum by an operator norm or absolute-value matrix will be character-blind.

**Toy check ($H_D=3$):**  The kernel sums over $h=\pm1,\pm3$ with the factor $4|\alpha_{h,3}|$.  The original $\mathcal M_2$ contains $\alpha_h C_h$, whose sign varies with $\chi_4(h)$; after squaring, that variation becomes a constant $4$.  Thus the character information is gone.  Numerical implementation for a small $D$ will confirm that the kernel contains only odd-$h$ terms and that the signed off-diagonal statistic of this kernel behaves like the unsigned one.

### 7. M2 $d$-Cauchy diagonal obstruction

If one instead applies Cauchy--Schwarz over the spatial variable $d$, the diagonal term is $\sum_d|w_D(d)|^2\sim D$, and after squaring the overall bound, the diagonal contribution becomes $\asymp D^2$.  At the top block $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, this is $X$, far above the squared target $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$.  Hence the $d$-Cauchy route is blocked without extraordinary off-diagonal cancellation.  This is a proved diagonal-size diagnostic.

**Numerical verification:**  For a small $D=10$, $D^2=100$; the required target for the squared sum is $O(D\log D)$, so the diagonal alone already exceeds it.  No implementation needed beyond this scaling check.

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps

1. **Real dyadic weights:** The paired M9 formulas require real $w_D$.  If complex weights are introduced, revert to the raw two-sided definitions.
2. **Fejer resonance handling:** The R5 proof relies on the pointwise bound and divisor function.  Numerical evaluation should detect integer arguments exactly to avoid floating-point blow-ups.
3. **BSOS sufficient target:** The implication $|\mathcal S_{\rm signed}|\ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}\Rightarrow\mathcal M_1\ll X^{1/4+\epsilon}$ is correct, but the actual size of $\mathcal S_{\rm signed}$ is unknown.
4. **Li--Yang parameter mapping:** The raw endpoint fails, but a full Bombieri--Iwaniec dissection could alter effective parameters; the failure of direct import does not imply impossibility.
5. **H13 stationary phase:** Amplitudes and boundary regimes should be treated uniformly; the leading stationary phase alone is insufficient.
6. **High-precision arithmetic:** For $X$ up to $10^8$, the Fejer sine-quotient evaluation needs high precision near integer arguments.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

- **Exact-resonance spikes:** For $X$ with many divisors, the R5 bound handles each spike as $W_\Delta(0)=1$; the divisor bound $\tau(X)\ll X^\epsilon$ guarantees only $O(X^\epsilon)$ such spikes, so the total sum remains $\ll X^{1/4+\epsilon}$.
- **M2 character loss:** A tiny example ($X=4$, $D=2$, $H_D=1$) shows that the raw $\mathcal M_2$ uses $\chi_4(1)$ sign, while the CS kernel replaces it by constant $4$; the CS bound may be orders of magnitude larger, making the route ineffective.
- **M2 $d$-CS diagonal:** For $D\sim X^{1/2}$, the diagonal alone exceeds the target, blocking that route.
- **Phi symmetry error:** The false identity $\Phi(1-u)=\Phi(u)$ is refuted by explicit computation.
