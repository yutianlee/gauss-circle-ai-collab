## Detailed reasoning

### 1. H4 source-normalized finite Vaaler approximation

Use

$$
e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}.
$$

Vaaler's source is Jeffrey D. Vaaler, "Some extremal functions in Fourier analysis," *Bulletin of the American Mathematical Society* 12(2), 183--216, 1985. The uploaded PDF is the relevant primary source.

The needed source anchors are now stable:

- Theorem 6, printed p. 192, equation (2.28), gives the Fourier transform of $J$:

$$
\widehat J(t)
=
\pi t(1-|t|)\cot(\pi t)+|t|
\qquad
(0<|t|<1),
$$

with $\widehat J(0)=1$ by continuous extension and $\widehat J(t)=0$ for $|t|\ge1$ in the theorem's displayed formula.

- Section 7, printed p. 207, equations (7.1)--(7.3), defines the periodic trigonometric polynomials $i_N,j_N,k_N$. Here $k_N$ is the periodic Fejer kernel:

$$
k_N(x)
=
\sum_{|k|\le N}
\left(1-\frac{|k|}{N+1}\right)e(kx).
$$

- Theorem 18, printed p. 210, equations (7.13)--(7.17), gives the finite periodic approximation. In particular, equation (7.14) gives

$$
|\psi*j_N(x)-\psi(x)|\le (2N+2)^{-1}k_N(x).
$$

Set $H=N$ and define

$$
K_H(t)=k_H(t)
=
\sum_{|k|\le H}
\left(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\right)e(kt)
=
\frac1{H+1}
\left(\frac{\sin \pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}\right)^2.
$$

Define

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u
\qquad
(0<u<1).
$$

Then the repo-normalized Vaaler coefficient is

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
\qquad
1\le |h|\le H.
$$

For $h>0$ this is

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right),
$$

and therefore

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

The finite approximation used in the proof draft is:

$$
\psi_F(t)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)
+
R_H^F(t),
$$

with

$$
|R_H^F(t)|
\le
\frac1{2H+2}K_H(t).
$$

Strictly speaking, Vaaler's periodic sawtooth is centered at discontinuities, while the arithmetic identity uses the floor-compatible point value. The conversion is harmless but should be explicit:

$$
\psi_C(n)=0,
\qquad
\psi_F(n)=-\frac12.
$$

At an integer $n$, the Vaaler polynomial vanishes by positive/negative frequency cancellation:

$$
V_H(n)=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}=0.
$$

Also,

$$
K_H(0)=H+1,
\qquad
\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12.
$$

Thus the Fejer majorant covers the exact half-jump

$$
|\psi_F(n)-V_H(n)|=\frac12.
$$

This is the key endpoint convention preventing a false proof.

### 2. R5-Full residual bound with edge cases

Let $w_D$ be a dyadic weight supported on $d\asymp D$ with bounded overlap in $D$ and $|w_D(d)|\le 1$. In the active range

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2},
$$

take an integer height

$$
H=H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4},
$$

with constants chosen so that $H\ge1$. For small $X$ or for boundary choices giving $H<1$, absorb the contribution into the absolute constant in the final $O_\epsilon(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$ bound.

Set

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

The standard Fejer bound is

$$
K_H(t)\ll \min\left(H,\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\right),
$$

so

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

Define the exact-resonance-safe cap

$$
W_\Delta(u)=
\begin{cases}
1,&u=0,\\
\min(1,\Delta^2/u^2),&u\ne0.
\end{cases}
$$

#### First residual leg

For each $d\asymp D$, choose a nearest integer $m=m(d)$ to $X/d$. If $X/d$ is exactly halfway between two integers, choose the smaller one. The choice affects no estimate because the two possible integers differ by one and both satisfy the same $W_\Delta$ bound up to constants.

Since $d\asymp D$,

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
=
\frac{|X-md|}{d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-md|}{D}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)
\ll
W_\Delta(X-md).
$$

Group by the integer product

$$
n=md.
$$

For active $D\le X^{1/2}$ and sufficiently large $X$, one has $m\asymp X/D>0$; small $X$ is handled separately. The number of representations $n=md$ with $d\asymp D$ is at most $\tau(n)$. Hence

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n).
$$

Using

$$
\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon
$$

and splitting into the central range $|X-n|\le \Delta$ and dyadic annuli

$$
2^j\Delta<|X-n|\le 2^{j+1}\Delta,
$$

gives

$$
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n)
\ll_\epsilon
X^\epsilon
\left(
\Delta+1+\sum_{j\ge0}2^j\Delta\cdot 2^{-2j}
\right)
\ll_\epsilon
X^\epsilon(\Delta+1).
$$

Since $\Delta\asymp X^{1/4}$,

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proof covers integer $X$, noninteger $X$, and exact resonance $X=md$ because $W_\Delta(0)=1$.

#### Shifted second residual legs

For $\rho\in\{1,3\}$, consider

$$
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right).
$$

Choose $m$ nearest to $(X/d+\rho)/4$, with the same deterministic tie rule. Then

$$
\left\|\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right\|
=
\frac{|X/d+\rho-4m|}{4}
=
\frac{|X-d(4m-\rho)|}{4d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-d(4m-\rho)|}{D}.
$$

Let

$$
\ell=4m-\rho.
$$

Then

$$
\ell\equiv -\rho\pmod4.
$$

In the active range and for sufficiently large $X$, $\ell>0$ because

$$
m\asymp \frac{X}{4D}
\ge cX^{1/2}
$$

when $D\le X^{1/2}$. Small $X$ is again absorbed by enlarging the constant. Thus

$$
n=d\ell
$$

is a positive integer product with an additional congruence condition on $\ell$. The congruence restriction can only reduce the divisor multiplicity, so the same divisor-bound proof gives

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

#### Short blocks and dyadic summation

For $D<X^{1/4}$, do not invoke Vaaler. Bound directly with $|\psi_F|\le1/2$. Since the total number of integers in all short dyadic blocks is $O(X^{1/4})$ up to bounded-overlap and logarithmic factors, short blocks contribute

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

There are $O(\log X)$ dyadic blocks in the active range. All logarithmic losses are absorbed into $X^\epsilon$. The residual uses absolute values of the dyadic weights, so no hidden cancellation is needed. This proves R5-Full conditional on H4.

### 3. Conditional bridge theorem

Assume:

1. H1--H3: the balanced hyperbola/sawtooth formula for $P(X)$.
2. H4: the finite Vaaler approximation with Fejer residual.
3. R5-Full: the total Fejer residual contribution is $\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}$.
4. M9: for every dyadic block $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$,

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X),\mathcal M_2(D;X)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Then

$$
P(X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Proof skeleton: apply H3, partition the $d$-sums into short blocks and active dyadic blocks, treat short blocks trivially, apply H4 blockwise on active blocks, use R5-Full for all residuals, and use M9 for all main Vaaler terms. The number of dyadic blocks and all fixed constants are absorbed by $X^\epsilon$.

This is only a conditional reduction. M9 is open.

### 4. Frozen M9-R25

Use actual Vaaler coefficients only:

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}.
$$

The first main term is

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
-4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\sum_{d\asymp D}
\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d).
$$

The second main term is

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\left(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\right)
\sum_{d\asymp D}
w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

Since

$$
C_h:=e(h/4)-e(3h/4)
=
\begin{cases}
2i\chi_4(h),&2\nmid h,\\
0,&2\mid h,
\end{cases}
$$

one may also write

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
8i\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}\chi_4(h)
\sum_{d\asymp D}
w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

The target is

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X),\mathcal M_2(D;X)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Arbitrary-coefficient variants remain stress tests only. They should not be listed as dependencies in the proof skeleton.

### 5. Paired implementation formulas for real weights

Assume $w_D(d)$ is real-valued. Define

$$
A_h(D;X)=\sum_{d\asymp D}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),
$$

and

$$
B_h(D;X)=\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

Then

$$
A_{-h}(D;X)=\overline{A_h(D;X)},
\qquad
B_{-h}(D;X)=\overline{B_h(D;X)}.
$$

Using $\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$, the paired formula for $\mathcal M_1$ is

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
\frac4\pi
\sum_{1\le h\le H_D}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\operatorname{Im} A_h(D;X).
$$

For $\mathcal M_2$,

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
-\frac8\pi
\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\chi_4(h)
\operatorname{Re} B_h(D;X).
$$

These are implementation formulas, not new estimates. Every numerical script should regress the raw two-sided complex definition against these paired real formulas before using paired values.

### 6. Coefficient algebra lemmas

The following should be added to the proof draft and lemma bank.

#### Alpha-Conjugacy-R25

For $1\le h\le H$,

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

The false identity

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}=-\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}
$$

should remain rejected.

#### C-TwoSided-Convention-R25

For

$$
C_h=e(h/4)-e(3h/4),
$$

one has

$$
C_h=
\begin{cases}
2i\chi_4(h),&2\nmid h,\\
0,&2\mid h,
\end{cases}
$$

and

$$
C_{-h}=\overline{C_h}=-C_h
$$

for odd $h$.

#### Phi-Asymmetry-R25

For $0<u<1$,

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u.
$$

Using $\cot(\pi(1-u))=-\cot(\pi u)$,

$$
\Phi(1-u)=1-\Phi(u),
$$

so

$$
\Phi(u)+\Phi(1-u)=1.
$$

In particular, $\Phi(1-u)=\Phi(u)$ only at $u=1/2$. Also,

$$
\Phi(0^+)=1,
\qquad
\Phi(1^-)=0.
$$

These identities are useful for coefficient validation. They do not provide cancellation by themselves.

### 7. M2-CS-Normalizations-R25

Let

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)=4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}\alpha_h C_h B_h.
$$

#### Unweighted $h$-Cauchy diagnostic

A direct unweighted Cauchy step gives a diagonal contribution of size

$$
D H_D
\asymp D^2X^{-1/4}.
$$

At the endpoint $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, this is

$$
X^{3/4},
$$

which is above the squared endpoint target $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$. Thus unweighted $h$-Cauchy is not viable at the endpoint under this normalization.

#### Weighted $|\alpha_h|$ Cauchy diagnostic

Let

$$
L=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}|\alpha_h|\asymp \log H_D.
$$

Then

$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2
\le
16L
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
|\alpha_h|\,|C_h|^2\,|B_h|^2.
$$

The diagonal contribution is acceptable:

$$
\ll D(\log H_D)^2
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}.
$$

However,

$$
|C_h|^2=
\begin{cases}
4,&2\nmid h,\\
0,&2\mid h,
\end{cases}
$$

so this normalization loses the sign of $\chi_4(h)$ and retains only odd-frequency support. This is a bounded-scope diagnostic for that Cauchy route, not a global obstruction to $\mathcal M_2$.

#### $d$-Cauchy diagnostic

Cauchy--Schwarz over the spatial variable can preserve the frequency character longer, but the diagonal scale reaches

$$
D^2
$$

at the top range, hence

$$
D^2\asymp X
$$

when $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, above the squared target $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$. Such a route would require substantial off-diagonal cancellation; it is not currently a proof path.

### 8. M2-Q4-Expansion-R25

Define

$$
\beta_h=\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h,
$$

and

$$
S_2(D;X)=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\beta_h
\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

Then

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)=4S_2(D;X).
$$

A sufficient fourth-moment target is

$$
|S_2(D;X)|^4\ll_\epsilon X^{1+\epsilon},
$$

equivalently

$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^4\ll_\epsilon X^{1+\epsilon}
$$

up to the fixed scalar $4^4=256$.

The exact expansion is

$$
|S_2|^4
=
\sum_{\mathbf h}
\sum_{\mathbf d}
\beta_{h_1}\overline{\beta_{h_2}}
\beta_{h_3}\overline{\beta_{h_4}}
\prod_{j=1}^4 w_D(d_j)
e\left(
\frac{X}{4}
\left(
\frac{h_1}{d_1}
-\frac{h_2}{d_2}
+\frac{h_3}{d_3}
-\frac{h_4}{d_4}
\right)
\right).
$$

Clearing denominators gives the resonance integer

$$
N(\mathbf h,\mathbf d)
=
h_1d_2d_3d_4
-
h_2d_1d_3d_4
+
h_3d_1d_2d_4
-
h_4d_1d_2d_3.
$$

The exact-resonance set $N=0$ and near-collision bands $|N|\sim T$ remain unestimated. Therefore M2-Q4 is only a proposed/falsification target. It should not be promoted to a theorem-level M9 estimate until the diagonal, semi-diagonal, denominator-paired, sign-symmetric, mixed, and near-collision cases are fully classified and bounded.

### 9. CRI-Ratio-Convention-R25

For one-sided paired real M2 computations, define

$$
\Sigma_r^R(D;X)
=
\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\h\equiv r\pmod4}}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\operatorname{Re}
\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)),
\qquad r\in\{1,3\}.

Then

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
-\frac8\pi
\left(
\Sigma_1^R(D;X)-\Sigma_3^R(D;X)
\right).
$$

Define the falsification ratio

$$
R_{\rm CRI}
=
\frac{|\Sigma_1^R-\Sigma_3^R|^2}
{|\Sigma_1^R|^2+|\Sigma_3^R|^2}.
$$

A value near $1$ indicates little useful residue-class interference. A value consistently well below $1$ would justify further proof work. This ratio is not a sufficient theorem by itself, because the whole quantity

$$

=
|\Sigma_1^R|^2+|\Sigma_3^R|^2
-
2\operatorname{Re}(\Sigma_1^R\overline{\Sigma_3^R})

needs to be controlled.

### 10. Li--Yang range and Case A/B audit

Li--Yang define a double exponential sum of the shape

$$
S
=
\sum_{H\le h\le 2H}g(h/H)
\sum_{M\le m\le 2M}G(m/M)
e\left(\frac{hT}{M}F(m/M)\right),
$$

with $F\in C^3([1,2])$, $g,G$ of bounded variation, derivative lower/upper bounds, and the nondegeneracy condition

$$
|F^{(1)}F^{(3)}-3(F^{(2)})^2|\ge C_4^{-1}.
$$

Their Theorem 1.2 gives

$$
R(X),\Delta(X)=O_\epsilon(X^{\theta^*+\epsilon}),
\qquad
\theta^*=0.3144831759741\cdots,
$$

not the conjectural endpoint $1/4$.

The typeset PDF parsing shows an ambiguity in Definition 4.1: the displayed Case A condition includes an apparent $M<T^{-7/16}$ in the definition, while the later application restates the relevant condition as $M<T^{7/16}$. This should be recorded as a source-transcription/typo ambiguity. For theorem import, it is safer to rely on the final reduction range rather than on a possibly mistyped side condition.

The final reduction relevant to the circle/divisor application has

$$
1\le H\le MT^{-\theta^*},
\qquad
1\le M\le T^{1/2},
$$

and asks for

$$
S/H\lesssim_\epsilon T^{\theta^*+\epsilon}.
$$

The paper also lists reciprocal-type $F$ forms including $1/z$, shifts by $\pm 1/4$, and $1/(4z)$ with $\pm M/(4T)$ shifts.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

1. **Endpoint discontinuity obstruction.** At $X/d\in\mathbb Z$, the floor-compatible sawtooth differs from the centered trigonometric value by exactly $1/2$. H4 survives because $K_H(0)/(2H+2)=1/2$. This should be part of every regression test.

2. **Fejer spike obstruction.** If many $d$ make $X/d$ or $(X/d+\rho)/4$ close to an integer, scalar residual heuristics fail. R5 handles this by product counting, not by cancellation. Divisor-rich $X$ should still be tested.

3. **Unweighted $h$-Cauchy obstruction for M2.** The diagonal scale $D H_D$ becomes $X^{3/4}$ at $D=X^{1/2}$, above the squared target. This falsifies that normalization at the endpoint.

4. **Weighted $h$-Cauchy sign-loss obstruction for M2.** The diagonal is acceptable, but $\chi_4(h)$ is replaced by odd support through $|C_h|^2$. This can still prove a bound if the resulting character-blind kernel is strong enough, but it no longer exploits the sign.

5. **Operator-norm blindness for M1.** If $\chi_4(d)$ enters only by diagonal conjugation $U=\operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(d))$, unitarily invariant norm bounds cannot see it. This does not rule out signed open-path or non-conjugacy statistics.

6. **Li--Yang endpoint gap.** At $D=X^{1/2}$, endpoint Vaaler height is $H=X^{1/4}$, while Li--Yang's final record range reaches only $H=X^{0.1855168\cdots}$. This is a hard theorem-import obstruction, not an impossibility theorem.
