## Main claim or direction

The primary analytic bottleneck is the fixed-coefficient main sum `M9`. Standard Cauchy--Schwarz over the frequency variable $h$ for $\mathcal M_2$ replaces the character $C_h$ with $|C_h|^2$, thereby losing the sign of $\chi_4(h)$ and retaining only odd-frequency support. To preserve the character signs before absolute-value majorization, we propose the fourth-moment statistic $\mathcal Q_4(D;X)$ and the Cross-Residue Interference (CRI) statistic as finite, falsifiable objects. The exact resonance taxonomy of $\mathcal Q_4$ suggests that the diagonal and pair-swapped families are compatible with the required $X^{1+\epsilon}$ target, but the denominator-paired singular cases and near-collision bands $|N| \sim T$ require explicit signed or absolute bounds. ## Detailed reasoning: Convention and Definitions

To ensure consistency across algebraic expansions and numerical implementations, we freeze the two-sided frequency convention for the $\mathcal M_2$ main sum.

Let the frequency index range over $1 \le |h| \le H_D$, where $H_D \asymp D X^{-1/4}$ for active dyadic blocks $X^{1/4} \le D \le X^{1/2}$. The actual Vaaler coefficients are defined as:
$$
\alpha_{h,H_D} = -\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H_D+1))}{2\pi i h},
$$
where the function $\Phi(u)$ is given by:
$$
\Phi(u) = \pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u) + u, \qquad 0 < u < 1.
$$
The coefficient conjugacy relation is:
$$
\alpha_{-h,H_D} = \overline{\alpha_{h,H_D}}.
$$
The frequency character factor is defined as:
$$
C_h = e(h/4) - e(3h/4).
$$
For odd $h$, $C_h = 2i\chi_4(h)$. For even $h$, $C_h = 0$. The conjugacy relation for the character factor is:
$$
C_{-h} = e(-h/4) - e(-3h/4) = \overline{e(h/4)-e(3h/4)} = \overline{C_h}.
$$
We define the core sum $S_2(D;X)$ as:
$$
S_2(D;X) = \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} \alpha_{h,H_D} C_h \sum_{d\asymp D} w_D(d) e\left(\frac{hX}{4d}\right).
$$
The second main term is then exactly:
$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X) = 4 S_2(D;X).
$$
We define the fourth-moment statistic $\mathcal Q_4(D;X)$ as:
$$
\mathcal Q_4(D;X) = |S_2(D;X)|^4.
$$
The relationship between the fourth moment of $\mathcal M_2$ and $\mathcal Q_4$ includes the scalar factor $4^4 = 256$:
$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^4 = 256 \mathcal Q_4(D;X).
$$
The required endpoint-strength target for the fourth moment is:
$$
\mathcal Q_4(D;X) \ll_\epsilon X^{1+\epsilon},
$$
which would imply $\mathcal M_2(D;X) \ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}$ by fourth-root extraction.

## Detailed reasoning: Fourth-Moment Expansion and Cleared Denominators

To analyze the potential for sign preservation and cancellation, we expand the fourth moment $\mathcal Q_4(D;X)$ explicitly. Let $\beta_h = \alpha_{h,H_D} C_h$. The expansion is:
$$
\mathcal Q_4(D;X) = S_2 \overline{S_2} S_2 \overline{S_2} = \sum_{\mathbf{h}} \sum_{\mathbf{d}} \beta_{h_1} \overline{\beta_{h_2}} \beta_{h_3} \overline{\beta_{h_4}} \left( \prod_{j=1}^4 w_D(d_j) \right) e\left( \frac{X}{4} \left( \frac{h_1}{d_1} - \frac{h_2}{d_2} + \frac{h_3}{d_3} - \frac{h_4}{d_4} \right) \right),
$$
where the frequency indices $h_1, h_2, h_3, h_4$ range over $1 \le |h_i| \le H_D$ and the spatial indices $d_1, d_2, d_3, d_4$ range over $d_i \asymp D$.

The character signs are retained through the product:
$$
C_{h_1} \overline{C_{h_2}} C_{h_3} \overline{C_{h_4}},
$$
which, on the support of odd frequencies, is a scalar multiple of $\chi_4(h_1)\chi_4(h_2)\chi_4(h_3)\chi_4(h_4)$. This preserves the sign information before any absolute-value majorization is applied.

To classify the resonance configurations, we clear the denominators in the phase argument. The phase is given by:
$$
\frac{X}{4 d_1 d_2 d_3 d_4} N,
$$
where the cleared-denominator integer $N$ is defined as:
$$
N = h_1 d_2 d_3 d_4 - h_2 d_1 d_3 d_4 + h_3 d_1 d_2 d_4 - h_4 d_1 d_2 d_3.
$$
The exact resonance configurations correspond to $N=0$, while the near-collision bands correspond to small non-zero values of $|N|$.

## Detailed reasoning: Exact Resonance Taxonomy ($N=0$)

We classify the exact resonance configurations ($N=0$) into distinct families and assign a rigorous status to the mass contribution of each family.

**Family 1: Exact Diagonal.** `[DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS]`
This family is defined by $h_1 = h_2$, $d_1 = d_2$ and $h_3 = h_4$, $d_3 = d_4$.
The number of terms in this family is bounded by $(H_D D)^2$. The weight per term is $|\alpha_{h_1}|^2 |\alpha_{h_3}|^2 \sim h_1^{-2} h_3^{-2}$ away from endpoint taper effects. Summing over $h_1$ and $h_3$ yields $O(1)$, and summing over $d_1$ and $d_3$ yields $D^2$. The total weighted mass is $\asymp D^2$ under bounded dyadic weights. Since $D \le X^{1/2}$, the mass is bounded by $X$, which is compatible with the target $X^{1+\epsilon}$.

**Family 2: Pair-Swapped Diagonal.** `[DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS]`
This family is defined by $h_1 = h_4$, $d_1 = d_4$ and $h_3 = h_2$, $d_3 = d_2$.
By symmetry with Family 1, the total weighted mass is $\asymp D^2 \ll X$, which is compatible with the target $X^{1+\epsilon}$.

**Family 3: Semi-Diagonal.** `[DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS]`
This family is defined by $d_1 = d_2 = d_3 = d_4 = d$.
The resonance condition reduces to $N = (h_1 - h_2 + h_3 - h_4)d^3 = 0$, which implies $h_1 - h_2 + h_3 - h_4 = 0$. The number of frequency solutions is bounded by $H_D^3$. The weight per term is $(|h_1| |h_2| |h_3| |h_4|)^{-1}$. Summing over the constrained frequency hyperplane yields $O(\log^3 H_D)$. Summing over $d$ yields $D$. The total weighted mass is $\asymp D \log^3 H_D$. Since $D \le X^{1/2}$, this mass is bounded by $X^{1/2+\epsilon} \ll X^{1+\epsilon}$.

**Family 4: Denominator-Paired.** `[DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS]`
This family is defined by $d_1 = d_2$ and $d_3 = d_4$, with $d_1 \ne d_3$.
The resonance condition reduces to $N = (h_1 - h_2)d_3^2 + (h_3 - h_4)d_1^2 = 0$, which implies $(h_1 - h_2)d_3^2 = (h_4 - h_3)d_1^2$. Let $g = \gcd(d_1, d_3)$, and write $d_1 = g u$, $d_3 = g v$ with $\gcd(u,v)=1$. The condition becomes $(h_1 - h_2)v^2 = (h_4 - h_3)u^2$. This requires $h_1 - h_2 = k u^2$ and $h_4 - h_3 = k v^2$ for some integer $k$. The number of choices for $d_1, d_3$ is $D^2$. The number of choices for the frequencies is constrained by the parameter $k$. The weighted sum requires careful bounding of the $1/h$ coefficients over this arithmetic progression, but under standard divisor bounds, the mass is expected to be $\ll_\epsilon X^{1+\epsilon}$.

**Family 5: Mixed Denominator Patterns.** `[DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS]`
This family is defined by $d_1 = d_4$ and $d_2 = d_3$, with $d_1 \ne d_2$.
By symmetry with Family 4, the resonance condition reduces to $(h_1 - h_4)d_2^2 = (h_2 - h_3)d_1^2$. The same parameterization and divisor-bound arguments apply, yielding an expected mass $\ll_\epsilon X^{1+\epsilon}$.

**Family 6: Sign-Symmetric Variants.** `[PROVED]`
These variants arise from the transformation $h_i \to -h_i$. Since we have frozen the two-sided convention $1 \le |h| \le H_D$, these variants are automatically covered by the preceding family classifications and do not introduce new mass scales.

**Family 7: Unclassified Residue.** `[ASSUMED]`
This family consists of configurations where $N=0$ but no two denominators are equal. This requires counting solutions to the full Diophantine equation $h_1 d_2 d_3 d_4 - h_2 d_1 d_3 d_4 + h_3 d_1 d_2 d_4 - h_4 d_1 d_2 d_3 = 0$. While heuristically sparse due to the high degree of the polynomial and the strict bounds on $h_i$, a rigorous bound requires a dedicated lattice-point counting lemma. We assume this mass is $\ll_\epsilon X^{1+\epsilon}$, but it remains an open proof gap.

## Detailed reasoning: Denominator-Paired Singular Cases

Within the Denominator-Paired family (Family 4), singular cases arise when the parameter $k$ approaches zero or when the frequencies approach the truncation boundaries.

Recall the parameterization: $h_1 - h_2 = k u^2$ and $h_4 - h_3 = k v^2$.
If $k = 0$, then $h_1 = h_2$ and $h_3 = h_4$. This collapses exactly to Family 1 (Exact Diagonal), which is already proved to be bounded by $D^2 \ll X$.

If $k \ne 0$, the frequencies are constrained by the truncation boundaries $h_i \in [-H_D, H_D] \setminus \{0\}$. This implies that $|k u^2| \le 2H_D$ and $|k v^2| \le 2H_D$. Since $u$ and $v$ are derived from $d_1/g$ and $d_3/g$, large values of $u$ or $v$ severely restrict the possible values of $k$. Specifically, if $u^2 > 2H_D$, no non-zero $k$ can satisfy the boundary condition. This truncation effect naturally suppresses the number of solutions when the greatest common divisor $g$ is small. The singular cases where $g$ is large (i.e., $d_1$ and $d_3$ are highly composite and share many factors) need a divisor-function bound, with expected loss at most $X^\epsilon$.

## Detailed reasoning: Near-Collision Bands and Heuristic Evaluation

The near-collision bands correspond to small non-zero values of $|N|$. We define the bands as $|N| \sim T$, where $T$ ranges up to the non-oscillatory scale. The phase is $\frac{X N}{4 d_1 d_2 d_3 d_4}$. For the phase to be non-oscillatory (i.e., $\ll 1$), the relevant range is $|N| \lesssim D^4 / X$. At the endpoint $D \asymp X^{1/2}$, this gives $|N| \lesssim X$.

We evaluate the $X^{5/4}$ absolute near-collision heuristic proposed in the human strategy note. `[HEURISTIC]`
The uniform-fiber model assumes that the total number of states is uniformly distributed across the possible values of $N$. The total number of states is $\sim H_D^4 D^4 \sim (D X^{-1/4})^4 D^4 = D^8 X^{-1}$. The maximum value of $|N|$ is $\sim H_D D^3 \sim D^4 X^{-1/4}$. Under the uniform assumption, the average number of states per $N$ is $\sim (D^8 X^{-1}) / (D^4 X^{-1/4}) = D^4 X^{-3/4}$.
The uniform model assumes an average weight per state of $H_D^{-4} \sim D^{-4} X$.
The absolute mass per $N$ is then $(D^4 X^{-3/4}) \times (D^{-4} X) = X^{1/4}$.
Summing over the non-oscillatory band $|N| \le D^4/X$, the total absolute mass is $\sim X^{1/4} \times (D^4/X) = D^4 X^{-3/4}$.
At the endpoint $D = X^{1/2}$, this yields $X^{5/4}$, which exceeds the target $X^{1+\epsilon}$.

However, this heuristic ignores several key structural features:
1. **Coefficient Weights:** The actual weights are $|\alpha_{h_1} \alpha_{h_2} \alpha_{h_3} \alpha_{h_4}| \sim (|h_1| |h_2| |h_3| |h_4|)^{-1}$. This heavily favors configurations with small frequencies. The uniform-fiber model's assumption of an average weight $H_D^{-4}$ drastically overestimates the mass of typical states and underestimates the mass of states with small $h$.
2. **GCD Structure:** The integer $N$ is not uniformly distributed. The multiplicative structure of $d_1 d_2 d_3 d_4$ can create clustering at specific values of $N$ that share prime factors with the denominators.
3. **Parity:** The character factor $C_h$ restricts the support to odd frequencies, eliminating all even-frequency configurations.
4. **Signs:** The heuristic estimates *absolute* mass. The actual fourth moment retains the signs $C_{h_1} \overline{C_{h_2}} C_{h_3} \overline{C_{h_4}}$, which may provide substantial cancellation within each band $|N| \sim T$.

Therefore, the $X^{5/4}$ heuristic is a warning against absolute majorization, not a proved lower bound. A3 should measure the signed and absolute masses empirically to determine the true behavior.

## Detailed reasoning: Cross-Residue Interference (CRI) Statistic

To isolate the cancellation between residue classes, we formalize the Cross-Residue Interference (CRI) statistic under the two-sided convention.

Define the residue-restricted sums:
$$
\Sigma_r(D;X) = \sum_{\substack{1\le |h|\le H_D\\h\equiv r\pmod4}} \alpha_{h,H_D} \sum_{d\asymp D} w_D(d) e\left(\frac{hX}{4d}\right), \qquad r \in \{1,3\}.
$$
For odd $h$, the character factor is $C_h = e(h/4) - e(3h/4)$.
If $h \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, $C_h = e(1/4) - e(3/4) = i - (-i) = 2i$.
If $h \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, $C_h = e(3/4) - e(9/4) = -i - i = -2i$.
If $h \equiv -1 \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, $C_h = -2i$.
If $h \equiv -3 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, $C_h = 2i$.
Thus, $C_h = 2i$ for $h \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ and $C_h = -2i$ for $h \equiv 3 \pmod 4$.

The core sum $S_2(D;X)$ can be written as:
$$
S_2(D;X) = 2i \Sigma_1(D;X) - 2i \Sigma_3(D;X) = 2i (\Sigma_1(D;X) - \Sigma_3(D;X)).
$$
The second main term is:
$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X) = 4 S_2(D;X) = 8i (\Sigma_1(D;X) - \Sigma_3(D;X)).
$$
The squared magnitude is:
$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2 = 64 |\Sigma_1 - \Sigma_3|^2 = 64 \left( |\Sigma_1|^2 + |\Sigma_3|^2 - 2\operatorname{Re}(\Sigma_1 \overline{\Sigma_3}) \right).
$$
We define the CRI falsification ratio as:
$$
R_{\rm CRI} = \frac{|\Sigma_1 - \Sigma_3|^2}{|\Sigma_1|^2 + |\Sigma_3|^2}.
$$
`[DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS]`
This ratio satisfies $0 \le R_{\rm CRI} \le 2$. Values near $0$ indicate strong constructive interference (cancellation) between the residue classes. Values near $1$ indicate neutral behavior (orthogonal residue classes). Values near $2$ indicate adverse behavior. The CRI statistic is a falsification metric; a theorem-level sufficient condition would require proving that $|\Sigma_1 - \Sigma_3|^2 \ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}$, which necessitates controlling the cross-term $2\operatorname{Re}(\Sigma_1 \overline{\Sigma_3})$.

## Claim ledger and proof-status calibration

This section records the precise status of the claims above so that no diagnostic statement is accidentally promoted into a theorem-level estimate.

1. **Two-sided coefficient convention.** `[PROVED]`
For $1\le |h|\le H_D$, the identity
$$
\alpha_{-h,H_D}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H_D}}
$$
follows directly from the real-valued function $\Phi(|h|/(H_D+1))$ and the factor $1/h$. This statement is algebraic and does not depend on any analytic estimate.

2. **Character factor convention.** `[PROVED]`
The identity
$$
C_h=e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=2i\chi_4(h)
$$
holds for odd $h$, while $C_h=0$ for even $h$. The relation $C_{-h}=\overline{C_h}$ also follows algebraically. This justifies using the two-sided convention in the fourth-moment expansion.

3. **Fourth-moment expansion.** `[PROVED]`
The expansion of $\mathcal Q_4(D;X)=|S_2(D;X)|^4$ into an eight-variable sum is an exact algebraic identity once the dyadic weights and coefficient convention are fixed. The cleared-denominator integer
$$
N=h_1d_2d_3d_4-h_2d_1d_3d_4+h_3d_1d_2d_4-h_4d_1d_2d_3
$$
is also exact.

4. **Displayed diagonal mass.** `[DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS]`
The estimate $\asymp D^2$ for the exact diagonal and pair-swapped diagonal assumes bounded dyadic weights, the standard $|\alpha_h|\ll 1/|h|$ coefficient model, and no unexpected endpoint amplification from the taper $\Phi(|h|/(H_D+1))$. These assumptions are natural, but the statement should remain a family-bound rather than a full exact-resonance theorem.

5. **Semi-diagonal mass.** `[DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS]`
The semi-diagonal estimate reduces to bounding
$$
\sum_{\substack{1\le |h_i|\le H_D\\h_1-h_2+h_3-h_4=0}}
\frac{1}{|h_1h_2h_3h_4|}.
$$
A crude convolution estimate gives polylogarithmic growth, so the expected contribution is $D(\log H_D)^O(1)$. The exact exponent of the logarithm is not important for the endpoint target, but the convolution should be written in proof-draft form before promotion.

6. **Denominator-paired family.** `[DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS]`
The parameterization with $d_1=gu$, $d_3=gv$ and
$$
h_1-h_2=ku^2,\qquad h_4-h_3=kv^2
$$
is correct for the displayed denominator-paired subfamily. The bound remains incomplete because endpoint cases with small induced frequencies and large shared gcd require separate coefficient summation.

7. **Four-distinct-denominator exact resonances.** `[ASSUMED]`
The unclassified residue contains configurations with four distinct denominators satisfying a rational relation. No bound is proved here. This is a genuine gap, not a cosmetic leftover category.

8. **Near-collision absolute mass.** `[HEURISTIC]`
The $X^{5/4}$ top-block scale follows from a uniform-fiber and typical-frequency model. It does not prove that the actual weighted absolute mass has that size. A3 should measure both the exact $\Phi$-weighted mass and the proxy $1/|h|$-weighted mass.

9. **Near-collision signed mass.** `[CONJECTURED]`
The useful theorem would be a signed band estimate substantially below the absolute model. This response does not prove such a theorem; it only defines the statistic that would test it.

10. **CRI identity.** `[PROVED]`
Under the two-sided convention and the residue split stated above,
$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)=8i(\Sigma_1-\Sigma_3)
$$
and the displayed squared-magnitude identity follows exactly.

11. **CRI as theorem route.** `[HEURISTIC]`
CRI is a falsification metric. A low value of $R_{\rm CRI}$ would identify useful residue-class cancellation, but by itself it is not a proof of the endpoint estimate unless accompanied by absolute scale bounds for $|\Sigma_1|^2+|\Sigma_3|^2$.

12. **H13 comparison.** `[CONJECTURED]`
The H13 endpoint test is relevant only as a comparison module. A transform that sends the character into a diagonal unitary conjugation would be character-blind under operator norms, but the exact post-transform kernel still needs to be written before this is a theorem-level rejection.

## Boundary-case audit for exact resonance families

The exact-resonance taxonomy should be checked against five boundary mechanisms before it is inserted into the lemma bank.

**Boundary 1: zero induced frequencies.** In the two-sided convention, the original summation excludes $h=0$. However, parameterizations such as $h_2=h_1-ku^2$ can produce $h_2=0$ even when $h_1$ and $k$ are nonzero. These tuples should not be counted in the family mass. A proof-grade denominator-paired estimate should insert indicators
$$
1_{\{1\le |h_1|\le H_D\}}1_{\{1\le |h_1-ku^2|\le H_D\}}
$$
and the analogous indicators for $h_3,h_4$. This is the clean way to avoid importing non-existent zero-frequency terms.

**Boundary 2: endpoint taper.** The coefficient
$$
|\alpha_{h,H_D}|=\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H_D+1))}{2\pi |h|}
$$
is smaller near $|h|=H_D$ than the proxy $1/(2\pi |h|)$, because $\Phi(u)$ tapers as $u\to1^-$. Any numerical or theoretical family mass should be computed twice: with exact $\Phi$ and with the proxy. If the two values differ materially in a bin, endpoint tapering is part of the mechanism and should be recorded.

**Boundary 3: low-frequency dominance.** The proxy $H_D^{-4}$ used in the $X^{5/4}$ heuristic assumes typical frequencies $|h_i|\asymp H_D$. Exact resonances and near-collisions may instead concentrate at $|h_i|=O(1)$, where the weight is much larger. A3 should report the distribution of
$$
\max_i |h_i|,\qquad \min_i |h_i|
$$
inside $N=0$ and inside the first near-collision band. This directly tests the typical-frequency model.

**Boundary 4: gcd-rich denominators.** The denominator-paired relation becomes less restrictive when $d_1$ and $d_3$ have a large common factor. In the notation $d_1=gu$, $d_3=gv$, large $g$ makes $u$ and $v$ small, hence permits more nonzero $k$. The proof therefore needs a sum over $g,u,v$ with dyadic constraints $gu\asymp D$, $gv\asymp D$, $\gcd(u,v)=1$, and frequency restrictions $|ku^2|,|kv^2|\le 2H_D$. This is the exact place where a divisor or lattice-point estimate should enter.

**Boundary 5: four-distinct denominator relations.** The unclassified residue is not automatically sparse. For fixed denominators, the equation
$$
h_1d_2d_3d_4-h_2d_1d_3d_4+h_3d_1d_2d_4-h_4d_1d_2d_3=0
$$
is one linear relation in four bounded integer frequencies. A crude count gives $O(H_D^3)$ possible frequency quadruples for each denominator quadruple, which is far too large before weights and arithmetic structure are used. The needed improvement should use divisibility, coprimality, or spacing in the coefficients $d_2d_3d_4$, $d_1d_3d_4$, $d_1d_2d_4$, $d_1d_2d_3$. Until such a lemma is supplied, this residue remains a real gap.
