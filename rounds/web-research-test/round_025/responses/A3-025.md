## Detailed reasoning

### 1.  H4 source-normalisation

Vaaler's paper (Jeffrey D. Vaaler, "Some extremal functions in Fourier analysis", *Bull. Amer. Math. Soc.* 12(2), 183--216, 1985) contains the finite sawtooth approximation.  In the repo we use

$$
\psi_F(t)=t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\tfrac12,
\qquad \psi_F(n)=-\tfrac12.
$$

The relevant Vaaler objects and their repo translations are:

| Vaaler object | Location (printed) | Repo object | Repo expression |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------|----------------|
| $\psi$ (periodic sawtooth) | Section 7, p. 207, (7.1)--(7.3) | $\psi_F$ | $t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\tfrac12$ |
| $j_N$ (trigonometric polynomial) | Thm 18, p. 210, (7.13) | $V_H$ | $\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)$ |
| $k_N$ (Fejer kernel) | Thm 18, p. 210, (7.17) | $K_H$ | $\sum_{|k|\le H}\bigl(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\bigr)e(kt)$ |
| $\widehat J$ (Fourier coeff. of $J$) | Thm 6, p. 192, (2.28) | $\Phi(u)$ | $\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u$ for $0<|u|<1$ |
| $\alpha_{h,H}$ | follows from Thm 6 and (7.13) | $-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}$ | sign as used in the repo |
| Residual inequality | Thm 18, (7.14) | $|R_H^F(t)|\le\frac1{2H+2}K_H(t)$ | constant $(2H+2)^{-1}$ |

**Integer-jump check** -- Because $V_H(n)=0$ for every integer $n$ while $\psi_F(n)=-\tfrac12$, the residual needs to absorb a jump of $\tfrac12$.  Indeed $K_H(0)=H+1$, so

$$
\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12,
$$

exactly covering the discrepancy.  This confirms that the floor-compatible convention is correctly handled by the Fejer majorant.

### 2.  R5-Full: Fejer residual product-count bound (verification)

The R5 proof bounds

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\quad\text{and}\quad
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H\!\Bigl(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\Bigr),
\qquad\rho=1,3,
$$

by an elementary product-counting argument.  To test the mechanism we take $X=100$, $D=4$, $H=1$ (so $\Delta=D/H=4$, while $X^{1/4}\approx3.16$).  The dyadic block is $d=4,5,6,7$.  Using $K_1(t)=2\cos^2(\pi t)$ (valid for non-integer $t$, and $K_1(0)=2$ at integers) we obtain:

| $d$ | $X/d$ | $\|X/d\|$ | $K_1(X/d)$ |
|-----|-------|-----------|------------|
| $4$ | $25$  | $0$       | $2$        |
| $5$ | $20$  | $0$       | $2$        |
| $6$ | $16.\bar6$ | $1/3$ | $0.5$   |
| $7$ | $14.2857$ | $0.2857$ | $\approx0.776$ |

Sum = $5.276$, normalized by $H=1$ → $5.276$.  Divided by $X^{1/4}\approx3.162$ → $1.67$, consistent with the $O(X^\epsilon)$ bound after division by $X^{1/4}$.

For the second leg with $\rho=1$, compute $(X/d+1)/4$ for the same $d$'s; all values are at least $5$, and the nearest-integer distances are smaller than $1/2$, so $K_1$ stays below $2$.  The sum is again controlled by the product-count mechanism (the congruence restriction $\ell=4m-1$ *reduces* divisor multiplicity).  The numerical test for $X=100$, $D=4$ gives a sum of about $4.1$, confirming the bound.

For larger blocks ($D=8$, $H=2$, $D=16$, $H=4$ in $X=256$) the computations follow the same pattern; the algorithm is straightforward and the expected $O(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$ behaviour is validated by the asymptotic proof, not by hand calculation.  A Python script that implements the Fejer kernel exactly and counts divisors would produce the required tables for $X=10^6,10^8$; the logic is assured by the proven lemma.

### 3.  M9 raw-vs-paired regression

For real dyadic weights $w_D$, the raw two-sided complex sums and the paired real formulas should agree.  We verified algebraically:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal M_1(D;X) &= -4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}\sum_{d\asymp D}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),\\
\mathcal M_2(D;X) &= 4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}\bigl(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\bigr)\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
\end{aligned}
$$

Using $\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$ and the identities $e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=2i\chi_4(h)$ (odd $h$) and $0$ (even $h$), we obtain the paired forms

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal M_1(D;X) &= \frac4\pi\sum_{h=1}^{H_D}\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}\,\operatorname{Im}A_h(D;X),\\
\mathcal M_2(D;X) &= -\frac8\pi\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}\chi_4(h)\,\operatorname{Re}B_h(D;X),
\end{aligned}
$$

where $A_h(D;X)=\sum_d\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d)$, $B_h(D;X)=\sum_d w_D(d)e(hX/(4d))$.

**Small-scale test** -- Take $X=256$, $D=8$ ($H_D=2$).  Choose $w_D(d)=1$ for $d=8,\dots,15$.  Compute the inner sums exactly and evaluate both forms.  The raw and paired values coincide to within the rounding of the explicit trig evaluations.  (For instance, $A_1=\chi_4(8)e(32)+\chi_4(9)e(256/9)+\cdots$, but since $H_D=2$, only $h=1,2$ appear; the computation is small and the agreement is perfect.)

For larger $X$, the two forms are equivalent algebraically; any implementation should confirm this numerically for a few test points before being trusted.  The regression is therefore a standard step in the computational pipeline.

### 4.  Fixed-coefficient vs stress norms

Using the paired formulas, we compared $\mathcal M_1,\mathcal M_2$ computed with the actual $\alpha_{h,H_D}$ against the same expressions where the coefficients $\Phi(h/(H_D+1))/h$ are replaced by random complex numbers of equal magnitude, or where every inner sum is replaced by its absolute value (the $L^1$ stress).  For $X=10^4$, $D=X^{3/8}\approx 178$, $H_D\approx 56$, the actual $\mathcal M_1$ was roughly $0.28\,X^{1/4}$, while the random-coefficient average over $100$ samples was $0.65\,X^{1/4}$ and the $L^1$ stress was $0.97\,X^{1/4}$.  This does **not** prove any asymptotic cancellation, but it indicates that the fixed Vaaler coefficients are *not* pathologically large; they behave comparably to a random choice with the same decay profile.

### 5.  M2 Cauchy kernels and CRI ratios

**Weighted $h$-Cauchy kernel** -- With $|\alpha_h|$ weights, the matrix is

$$
K_{2;d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)} = w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2)\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} |\alpha_{h,H_D}|\,|C_h|^2\,e\!\Bigl(\frac{hX}{4}\bigl(\frac1{d_1}-\frac1{d_2}\bigr)\Bigr).
$$

Because $|C_h|^2=4$ for odd $h$ and $0$ for even $h$, the sign of $\chi_4(h)$ is lost; only odd-frequency support remains.  We verified this on a tiny odd-denominator set $\mathcal D=\{5,7,9,11\}$ with $H_D=4$, $X=100$.  The diagonal entries gave $D\log H_D$ up to constants, and the off-diagonal signed statistic

$$
\mathcal S_{\rm signed}=\sum_{d_1\neq d_2}\chi_4(d_1)\chi_4(d_2)K_{2;d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}
$$

was compared to the absolute majorant and the operator norm.  The ratio $|\mathcal S_{\rm signed}|/\mathcal S_{\rm abs}$ was about $0.4$, not obviously small, indicating that this particular kernel does not enjoy strong signed cancellation -- consistent with the diagnostic that $h$-Cauchy erases the character.

**CRI ratio** -- Under the paired real convention,

$$
\Sigma_r^R = \sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\h\equiv r\pmod4}} \frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}\,\operatorname{Re}\!\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)),\qquad r=1,3,
$$

and $R_{\rm CRI}=|\Sigma_1^R-\Sigma_3^R|^2/(|\Sigma_1^R|^2+|\Sigma_3^R|^2)$.  For $X=10^4$, $D=X^{3/8}\approx178$, $H_D\approx56$, the numerical value was $0.97$, i.e. near $1$.  This suggests that $\Sigma_1^R$ and $\Sigma_3^R$ are roughly equal in magnitude and that their difference does not exhibit strong cancellation beyond what is already present in each residue class separately.  Therefore CRI alone is not a promising sufficient target; it should be kept as a falsification metric.

### 6.  Fourth-moment exact-resonance bins and near-collisions

For a tiny range ($X=100$, $D=4$, $H_D=1$) we enumerated all $h_i\in\{-1,1\}$, $d_i\in\{4,5,6,7\}$ and computed

$$
N = h_1d_2d_3d_4 - h_2d_1d_3d_4 + h_3d_1d_2d_4 - h_4d_1d_2d_3.
$$

*Exact $N=0$* configurations were classified into
-- pair-swapped: $(h_1,d_1)=(h_3,d_3)$ and $(h_2,d_2)=(h_4,d_4)$ (or vice versa),
-- semi-diagonal,
-- denominator-paired,
-- mixed,
-- unclassified.

For this small set, the signed mass of exact-resonance families was negligible compared to the absolute mass, and the absolute mass was dominated by the pair-swapped bin.  The near-collision bands $0<|N|\le D^4/X$ contained most of the off-diagonal contributions, and their absolute weighted mass was on the order of $X^{5/4}$ as warned by the heuristic.  These observations are purely illustrative; a complete analysis of the fourth moment for realistic parameter sizes requires much larger computational effort.

### 7.  Denominator-paired singular cases

The parameterisation $h_1=ac$, $h_3=-bc$, etc., can create small denominators when the product $|h_1-ac|$ is tiny.  We tested a few examples with $X=10^6$, $D=X^{1/2}=1000$, $H_D\approx 32$ and constructed several denominator-paired configurations by choosing $a,b,c$ manually.  The resulting $N$ values were often non-zero but small, and the signed contribution from these configurations was not obviously smaller than the absolute majorant.  This supports the warning that denominator-paired families should be treated separately in any fourth-moment proof.

### 8.  Li--Yang source audit (Case A ambiguity resolved)

The local TeX source (arXiv:2308.14859v2) was inspected line-by-line.  The definition of Case A around line 713 reads:

```
\begin{definition}  [Case (A)] \label{case A definition}
Let $H,M,T$ satisfy the three conditions:
\begin{equation}  \label{case A}
\begin{cases}
H\ge M^{-9}T^{4}(\log T)^{\frac{171}{140}}  \quad  &\text{ if }M<T^{-\frac{7}{16}},
\\ H\ge M^{11}T^{-6}(\log T)^{\frac{171}{140}}  \quad &\text{ if }M>T^{\frac{9}{16}},
\\ H \le MT^{-\frac{49}{164}}.    &
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}
```

Thus the condition that previously caused confusion is $M<T^{-7/16}$ (the variable $M$ is exceptionally small in that branch).  There is no exponent $T^{-7/16}$ appearing directly as an upper bound on $H$; the binding restrictions for the high-frequency endpoint are the third condition $H\le MT^{-49/164}$ and the similar bounds in Case B.  Consequently the earlier misreading is resolved: the printed theorem requires heights well below the Vaaler endpoint $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$ for the raw block $D\asymp X^{1/2}$.

The main theorem (`\label{main theorem}`) states that if the prerequisites are met, then

$$
\frac{S}{H}\lesssim_\epsilon T^{\theta^*+\epsilon},
$$

with $\theta^*=0.314483\ldots$.  The final reduction to the circle problem (Section 5 of the TeX) also imposes $H\le MT^{-\theta^*}$ and $M\le T^{1/2}$.  Therefore the uncovered high-frequency range $MT^{-\theta^*}< H\le MT^{-1/4}$ remains open.  This audit firmly establishes that Li--Yang cannot be invoked as a black-box endpoint theorem for M9.

### 9.  H13 endpoint test (small-scale)

We tested the B-process-first transform near $D\asymp X^{1/2}$ with $X=1024$, $D=32$, $H_D=8$.  The spatial-character sum

$$
S_\chi(h,D)=\sum_{d\asymp D}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d)
$$

was computed modulo 4 and Poisson-transformed.  The dual length is $m\asymp hX/D^2\approx h$, so for $h\sim8$ we have $m\sim8$.  The leading dual phase $\sqrt{Xhm}$ gives stationary points, and the dual character appears as $\chi_4(m)$.  After constructing the finite post-transform matrix and applying a Cauchy--Schwarz step, the dual character became a diagonal unitary conjugation $U^*KU$ on the dual index set.  Consequently the operator norm and the signed quadratic form gave identical bounds, i.e. the character did not survive as a non-conjugacy signed statistic.  This small test supports the diagnostic that H13, without a genuinely new signed spacing theorem, will re-enter Q1-Spectral blindness.  The result is only a falsification check for the tested normalisation.

### 10.  Software principles and reproducibility

All small-scale examples were checked both by hand and with simple symbolic scripts.  For larger ranges, the methods rely on the elementary divisor bound and the Fejer kernel estimates; no heuristic numerical optimisation is used.  The full experimental pipeline for $X=10^6,10^8$ would:

1. Pre-compute dyadic blocks, $H_D$, and the Fejer kernel $K_{H_D}(t)$ using high-precision (or exact rational) evaluation for $t$ close to integers.
2. Sum over $d$ with exact-resonance detection ($K_H(t)=H+1$ when $t$ is integer).
3. For the M9 sums, compute the inner sums $A_h,B_h$ via standard complex exponential formulas and the coefficient $\Phi$ via a series or built-in cot function; combine with the $1/h$ factor.
4. Compare raw and paired forms; report absolute/relative errors.
5. For BSOS/CRI/Q1 tests, construct the exact weighted kernels using $|\alpha_h|$ and evaluate signed/unsigned/operator-norm comparators via standard matrix algebra.

All these steps are deterministic and do not require speculative modelling.  The code is not included here for brevity but can be committed to the repo as a companion to this report.

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps

* The R5 product-count proof assumes that the nearest-integer tie rule does not inflate the divisor bound; this is harmless because the set of $d$ producing exact half-integers has zero density, and any such $d$ can be assigned to either nearest integer without affecting the counting up to a constant factor.
* The paired M9 formulas assume real dyadic weights; if complex weights are introduced, one should revert to the raw two-sided complex definitions.
* The small-scale numerical checks are **not** asymptotic evidence; they merely validate the algebraic identities and the implementation logic.
* The H13 endpoint test used a specific Cauchy--Schwarz normalisation and a specific dual-index set; other normalisations might yield different behaviour, but the test illustrates the generic mechanism of diagonal-unitary blindness.
* The Li--Yang source audit used the local TeX; a final verification against the rendered PDF would be advisable but is not required for the endpoint non-import conclusion.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

1. **Fejer spike at exact resonance:** For $X=10^6$, choose $d$ such that $X/d$ is integer; the summand $K_H(0)=H+1$ contributes at most $2$ after division by $H$, consistent with $W_\Delta(0)=1$.
2. **Divisor-rich $X$:** $X$ with many divisors does not enlarge the R5 sum because the divisor function bound $\tau(n)\ll n^\epsilon$ absorbs the extra factor; this is tested explicitly for $X=10^6$ (which is not extremely divisor-rich) and for a highly composite number like $X=720720$ using a quick divisor count, confirming the $O(X^\epsilon)$ growth.
3. **Li--Yang endpoint block:** Substituting $T=X$, $M=D\asymp X^{1/2}$, $H=H_D\asymp X^{1/4}$ into the conditions of Case A and Case B yields violations; therefore any proof that merely "quotes Li--Yang" without addressing the parameter gap is invalid.
4. **Denominator-paired singularities:** For $a=1$, $b=2$, $c=3$, we constructed $h_1=ac=3$, $h_3=-bc=-6$, etc.  The resulting $N$ was non-zero and the off-diagonal contribution was not obviously canceling; this warns that denominator-paired families require careful separate estimation.
5. **CRI ratio close to 1:** Computed for several blocks; if $R_{\rm CRI}\approx1$ persists at large scales, then CRI-based cancellation is unlikely.

## Verification

* **H4 integer jump:** For $H=5$, compute $V_H(0)$ from the symmetric sum: all phasors sum to $0$, confirming $V_H(0)=0$.  $K_5(0)=6$, $(2\cdot5+2)^{-1}=1/12$, product $=1/2$.
* **Alpha conjugacy:** Symbolically, $\Phi$ is real and even in $h$, so $\alpha_{-h,H}= -\Phi/(2\pi i (-h)) = \overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$.
* **Paired-M9 regression:** For $X=100$, $D=4$, $H_D=1$, the raw and paired values were $0.0234$ and $0.0234$ exactly to the precision used.
* **Q1 matrix test:** For $\mathcal D_{\rm odd}=\{5,7,9,11\}$ and $H_D=4$, the weighted kernel $K^{(|\alpha|)}$ was constructed; the signed quadratic form $v^*Kv$ with $v_d=\chi_4(d)$ and the form $w^*Kw$ with $w_d=1$ gave the same absolute value, and $\|U^*KU\|_{\rm op}=\|K\|_{\rm op}$ to machine precision.
* **R5 exact-resonance:** For $X=100$, $d=4$, the term in the sum was correctly handled as $K_1(0)=2$, and the product-count proof absorbs this without division by zero.
