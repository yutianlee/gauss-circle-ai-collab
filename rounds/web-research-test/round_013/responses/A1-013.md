## Detailed reasoning

### 1. Source-normalized H4 statement, with remaining source gap

Use

$$
e(t)=e^{2\pi i t},
\qquad
\psi(t)=t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12.
$$

The Vaaler approximation needed in this repo is the following.

For $H\ge1$, define

$$
K_H(t)
=
\sum_{|k|\le H}
\left(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\right)e(kt)
=
\frac{1}{H+1}
\left(\frac{\sin \pi(H+1)t}{\sin \pi t}\right)^2.
$$

For $0<u<1$, define

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u.
$$

Set

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
\qquad
1\le |h|\le H.
$$

Then the required H4 form is

$$
\psi(t)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)
+
R_H(t),
$$

with

$$
|R_H(t)|\le \frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(t).
$$

The coefficient sign is consistent with the nonendpoint Fourier expansion

$$
\psi(t)=-\sum_{h\ne0}\frac{e(ht)}{2\pi i h}
$$

at nonintegral $t$. Since $\Phi(u)\to1$ as $u\to0^+$, the Vaaler coefficients are smoothed versions of the ordinary Fourier coefficients. Also

$$
|\alpha_{h,H}|\ll \frac1{|h|}.
$$

At integer $t=n$,

$$
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(hn)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}
=0
$$

by the pairing $h,-h$, whereas the floor-compatible convention gives

$$
\psi(n)=-\frac12.
$$

The residual majorant covers this exactly because

$$
K_H(0)=H+1
$$

and hence

$$
\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12.
$$

This endpoint check is essential. The trigonometric polynomial is centered at the midpoint convention at jumps; the floor-compatible sawtooth is not. The discrepancy is not a mistake, but it must be covered by the residual.

Source status: the article metadata and citation are verified from Project Euclid's listing, and an accessible MathOverflow discussion identifies Vaaler's paper and points to p. 206 for the sawtooth polynomial. However, direct full-text access to Project Euclid and AMS failed in this run, so I would keep H4 as "external theorem dependency; exact page/equation source-normalization pending." The formula above is the one to be checked by A3 against the primary PDF.

### 2. R5-Full: complete residual proof conditional on H4

Let $X\ge2$, let

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2},
$$

and choose an integer height

$$
H=H_D
$$

satisfying

$$
c_0DX^{-1/4}\le H\le C_0DX^{-1/4}
$$

with fixed positive constants $c_0,C_0$. In the proof draft one can take

$$
H_D=\max(1,\lfloor C DX^{-1/4}\rfloor)
$$

for a fixed $C$; all constants below are unaffected.

Let $w_D$ be a dyadic weight supported on

$$
D\le d\le 2D
$$

with

$$
|w_D(d)|\le C_w.
$$

Only boundedness and bounded overlap are needed for the residual estimate; smoothness is not needed here.

The Fejer kernel satisfies

$$
K_H(t)\ll \min\left(H,\frac{1}{H\|t\|^2}\right),
$$

where

$$
\|t\|=\min_{m\in\mathbb Z}|t-m|.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac{1}{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

#### First leg

We need to bound

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d).
$$

For each $d$, choose $m=m(d)$ to be a nearest integer to $X/d$. Ties may be resolved arbitrarily; the bound only uses the distance to the nearest integer. Since $D\le X^{1/2}$ and $X$ is large, $X/d\gg X^{1/2}$, so $m$ is positive. Small $X$ can be absorbed into the implicit constant.

For $d\asymp D$,

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
=
\left|\frac Xd-m\right|
=
\frac{|X-md|}{d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-md|}{D}.
$$

Set

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}.
$$

With $H\asymp DX^{-1/4}$,

$$
\Delta\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

Then

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-md|^2}\right).
$$

Group terms by

$$
n=md.
$$

For a fixed positive integer $n$, the number of representations $n=md$ with $d\asymp D$ is at most $\tau(n)$. Also, since $|X-md|\le d/2\ll D\le X^{1/2}$ for the nearest-integer choice, all such $n$ lie in a fixed interval $n\asymp X$ for $X$ sufficiently large. Extending the summation to all positive integers only enlarges the bound. Thus

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right).
$$

Using

$$
\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon\ll_\epsilon X^\epsilon
$$

for $n\asymp X$, it remains to bound

$$
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right).
$$

This is uniform in real $X$. Indeed, the terms with $|X-n|\le \Delta$ contribute $O(\Delta+1)$, and the dyadic tail gives

$$
\sum_{|X-n|>\Delta}\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}
\ll
\Delta^2\int_\Delta^\infty u^{-2}\,du
\ll \Delta.
$$

Thus

$$
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right)
\ll \Delta+1
\ll X^{1/4}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This covers both integer and noninteger $X$. If $X$ is an integer and $n=X$ occurs, the summand is interpreted as $\min(1,\infty)=1$, exactly matching the Fejer spike at an integer.

#### Shifted second legs

For $\rho\in\{1,3\}$, we must bound

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right).
$$

Choose $m=m(d,\rho)$ nearest to

$$
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}.
$$

Then

$$
\left\|
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}
\right\|
=
\left|
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}-m
\right|
=
\frac{|X+\rho d-4md|}{4d}
=
\frac{|X-d(4m-\rho)|}{4d}.
$$

Let

$$
\ell=4m-\rho.
$$

Then

$$
\ell\equiv -\rho \pmod 4.
$$

For large $X$, $\ell>0$ because $X/d\gg1$. Any finite small-$X$ or $\ell\le0$ edge is absorbed into constants. As before,

$$
\left\|
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}
\right\|
\asymp
\frac{|X-d\ell|}{D}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-d\ell|^2}\right).
$$

Group by

$$
n=d\ell.
$$

For fixed $n$, the number of admissible representations with $d\asymp D$ and

$$
\ell\equiv -\rho\pmod 4
$$

is at most $\tau(n)$. The congruence restriction only reduces the count. Hence

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proves R5 on a long dyadic block.

#### Zero mode and signs

The Fejer kernel includes the zero Fourier mode. Its contribution is

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
\ll
\frac{D}{H}
\asymp X^{1/4},
$$

which is consistent with the product-count proof. Since $K_H$ is real and even, the $k>0$ and $k<0$ nonzero modes are already included. No separate signed-frequency argument is needed for the residual.

#### Short blocks

For

$$
D<X^{1/4},
$$

do not apply Vaaler. In H3 each sawtooth term satisfies

$$
|\psi(t)|\le \frac12.
$$

The total number of denominators in all short dyadic blocks is $O(X^{1/4})$. Therefore the contribution of all short blocks to both legs is

$$
O(X^{1/4})
$$

up to an absolute constant.

#### Dyadic summation

Take a smooth or sharp dyadic partition of the denominator range

$$
1\le d\le y=\lfloor X^{1/2}\rfloor.
$$

Assume bounded overlap and $|w_D|\ll1$. There are $O(\log X)$ long blocks. R5 gives

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}
$$

per block, and the logarithm is absorbed into $X^\epsilon$. Thus all Vaaler residuals from both H3 legs contribute

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proves R5-Full, conditional on H4.

### 3. Bridge theorem

Assume H1--H3. Then for $X\ge1$,

$$
P(X)
=
-4\sum_{d\le y}\chi_4(d)\psi(X/d)
+
4\sum_{d\le y}
\left[
\psi\left(\frac{X/d+1}{4}\right)
-
\psi\left(\frac{X/d+3}{4}\right)
\right]
+
O(1),
$$

with

$$
y=\lfloor X^{1/2}\rfloor.
$$

Split the denominator range into short blocks $D<X^{1/4}$ and long blocks $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$.

The short blocks contribute

$$
O(X^{1/4})
$$

because $|\psi|\le1/2$.

On each long block, apply H4 with

$$
H_D\asymp DX^{-1/4}.
$$

The first leg main term on a block is

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
-4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\sum_{d\asymp D}
\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d).
$$

The second leg main term is

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\left(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\right)
\sum_{d\asymp D}
w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

All residuals from the long blocks are

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}
$$

by R5-Full.

If M9 holds, i.e.

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X),\mathcal M_2(D;X)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}
$$

for every long dyadic block, then summing over $O(\log X)$ blocks gives

$$
\sum_D \mathcal M_1(D;X)+\sum_D \mathcal M_2(D;X)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Therefore

$$
P(X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This proves the bridge theorem.

### 4. M9 definitions and stress-test appendix

The official M9 target must use the actual Vaaler coefficients:

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}.
$$

It should not be replaced by arbitrary bounded $u_h/h$ unless explicitly labelled as a stronger stress test.

For dyadic $h$-subblocks $H_0\le H_D$, one may define stress-test quantities

$$
\mathcal B_1(H_0,D;X)
=
\sum_{h\asymp H_0}u_h
\sum_{d\asymp D}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),
\qquad |u_h|\le1,
$$

and analogous $\mathcal B_2$. But these are stronger than M9. The proof needs only the fixed coefficient sum over $1\le |h|\le H_D$ with the Vaaler weights.

This distinction is not cosmetic. An arbitrary-coefficient bound is closer to an $L^1$ or operator-norm stress norm and may erase the coefficient structure that M9 could exploit.

### 5. M9b theorem-extension problem: best formulation

The current state records M9b as a fractional-frequency theorem-extension problem after splitting $h\bmod4$. That is valid as one representation, but it is not the best representation for comparison with Li--Yang. The natural formulation keeps the phase-shifted second-leg expression before converting it into a $\chi_4(h)$ frequency character.

Indeed,

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}
\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)
\left[
e\left(h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac14\right)\right)
-
e\left(h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac34\right)\right)
\right].
$$

Write $d=Dz$ and $T=X$, $M=D$. Then

$$
h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac{\rho}{4}\right)
=
\frac{hT}{M}
\left(
\frac{1}{4z}+\frac{\rho M}{4T}
\right),
\qquad \rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

Thus the natural phase functions are

$$
F_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac{1}{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X},
\qquad \rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

Their derivatives are the same as those of $1/(4z)$:

$$
F_{\rho,D}'(z)=-\frac{1}{4z^2},
$$

$$
F_{\rho,D}''(z)=\frac{1}{2z^3},
$$

$$
F_{\rho,D}'''(z)=-\frac{3}{2z^4}.
$$

Hence

$$
F_{\rho,D}'F_{\rho,D}'''-3(F_{\rho,D}'')^2
=
\frac{3}{8z^6}-\frac{3}{4z^6}
=
-\frac{3}{8z^6}\ne0.
$$

The constant shift $\rho D/(4X)$ does not affect the derivative hypotheses. Therefore the clean M9b theorem-extension problem is:

**M9b-shifted-$F$ target.** Prove or cite an endpoint theorem for Li--Yang/Bombieri--Iwaniec sums of the form

$$
S_{\rho}
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\sum_{d\asymp D}
w_D(d)
e\left(\frac{hX}{D}F_{\rho,D}(d/D)\right),
$$

with

$$
F_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac{1}{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X},
\qquad \rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

This formulation avoids a periodic $\chi_4(h)$ weight and avoids the fractional-frequency representation

$$
e((q+\beta)X/d).
$$

The fractional-frequency representation remains a useful stress formulation:

$$
h=4q+r,\qquad r\in\{1,3\},
$$

which gives

$$
e(hX/(4d))=e((q+r/4)X/d).
$$

However, if one rewrites it as

$$
e(qX/d)e(\beta X/d),
$$

then $e(\beta X/d)$ becomes a highly oscillatory $d$-weight, not a bounded-variation weight with uniform constants. That formulation is therefore worse for black-box Li--Yang comparison.

Conclusion: use the shifted-$F$ formulation as the official M9b theorem-extension problem. Keep the fractional-frequency version as an equivalent algebraic representation and numerical stress test.

### 6. Li--Yang subrange map

Li--Yang define double sums of the form

$$
S
=
\sum_{H\le h\le 2H}g(h/H)
\sum_{M\le m\le 2M}G(m/M)
e\left(\frac{hT}{M}F(m/M)\right),
$$

with bounded-variation weights and derivative hypotheses on $F$. The uploaded Li--Yang TeX audit records their Case A and Case B restrictions and their final circle/divisor reduction. Their arXiv abstract confirms the paper's method: Bombieri--Iwaniec, a new first-spacing estimate, and Huxley's second-spacing results.

For M9, substitute

$$
T=X,
\qquad
M=D=X^\delta,
\qquad
1/4\le\delta\le1/2.
$$

The endpoint Vaaler height is

H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}=X^{\delta-1/4}.

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps

1. **H4 source gap.** The residual proof rests on H4. If the source theorem has a different normalization, the constants are harmless, but the coefficient formula and endpoint convention must be corrected.

2. **Local height integer choice.** The statement $H_D\asymp DX^{-1/4}$ hides integer rounding. This is harmless for $D\ge X^{1/4}$ if $H_D\ge1$, but the proof draft should specify one choice, for example $H_D=\max(1,\lfloor cDX^{-1/4}\rfloor)$.

3. **Dyadic partition signs.** If the partition weights are not nonnegative, R5 still works after replacing $w_D$ by $|w_D|$. The proof draft must explicitly do this.

4. **Nearest-integer tie rules.** Ties do not matter, but the proof should say so.

5. **Second-leg positivity of $\ell=4m-\rho$.** For $X$ large, $\ell>0$ on the active range. For small $X$ or edge cases, absorb into constants. The proof draft should include this sentence.

6. **Product range $n\asymp X$.** The product-count proof uses $n\asymp X$ to apply $\tau(n)\ll X^\epsilon$. This follows for large $X$ because $|X-n|\ll D\le X^{1/2}$, but it should be stated.

7. **Li--Yang lower ranges.** The Li--Yang final reduction includes lower restrictions on $H$. Low $H$ for M9 should not be assumed covered. A separate low-frequency treatment is needed.

8. **M9b representation.** If M9b is represented as a $\chi_4(h)$ coefficient, a periodic $h$-weight appears and may violate bounded-variation hypotheses. The shifted-$F$ representation avoids this, and should be adopted for theorem comparison.

9. **Fixed Vaaler coefficients.** $\alpha_{h,H}$ are not arbitrary coefficients. A proof that takes absolute values over $h$ too early will likely lose the only special structure available.

10. **No endpoint theorem from stationary phase alone.** H13 or C2 stationary phase transforms individual sums/integrals. It does not imply cancellation in the resulting double sum.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

### Fejer spike obstruction

R5 shows Fejer spikes are controlled by product counting. The worst alignment corresponds to many representations of products near $X$:

$$
md\approx X
$$

or

$$
d(4m-\rho)\approx X.
$$

The multiplicity is at most $\tau(n)$, so exact product alignment cannot exceed

$$
X^{1/4+\epsilon}
$$

after the Fejer normalization. This removes the earlier worry that square or divisor-rich $X$ might create a residual catastrophe.

### M9 operator-norm blindness

If the spatial character in M9a enters only as diagonal conjugation

$$
U=\operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(d)),
$$

then

$$
\|U^*KU\|_{\rm op}=\|K\|_{\rm op}.
$$

The uploaded state records Q1-Spectral as a proved diagnostic for operator-norm-only methods. This means a Cauchy--Schwarz or large-sieve step that reduces M9a to an unsigned operator norm cannot exploit $\chi_4(d)$.

This is not a global obstruction. It only says that the method must preserve signs in a non-conjugacy statistic or signed bilinear form before norm extraction.

### M9b periodic-weight obstruction

If M9b is written as

$$

$$

then $\chi_4(h)$ is a periodic weight with total variation $\asymp H$ over an interval of length $H$. A theorem requiring bounded variation uniformly in $H$ cannot accept it directly.

The shifted-phase formulation avoids this obstruction.

This is a theorem-application obstruction, not a no-go theorem for new spacing methods.

## Verification

### Phi special values

For numerical implementation,

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u.
$$

The key values are:

$$
\Phi(1/2)=1/2,
$$

because $\cot(\pi/2)=0$.

Also,

$$
\Phi(1/4)
=
\pi\cdot\frac14\cdot\frac34\cdot1+\frac14
=
\frac{3\pi}{16}+\frac14.
$$

And

$$
\Phi(3/4)
=
\pi\cdot\frac34\cdot\frac14\cdot(-1)+\frac34
=
-\frac{3\pi}{16}+\frac34.
$$

Thus

$$
\Phi(1/4)\ne \Phi(3/4).
$$

Any M9 numerical implementation assuming symmetry around $1/2$ is wrong.

### Endpoint convention

At integer $n$,

$$
V_H(n)=0
$$

by coefficient pairing, while

$$
\psi(n)=-1/2.
$$

The residual bound gives

$$
|R_H(n)|\le \frac{1}{2H+2}K_H(0)=1/2.
$$

Thus the H4 convention is compatible with H3's floor-compatible sawtooth.

### R5 real-$X$ summability

The kernel

$$
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{|X-n|^2}\right)
$$

is uniformly summable for real $X$, including $X$ arbitrarily close to an integer. If $X$ is exactly an integer, one term contributes $1$, not infinity. Therefore R5 is stable under the jump-discontinuity tests.

### M9b derivative nondegeneracy

For

$$

one has

$$

$$

This is bounded away from $0$ on compact subintervals of $(0,\infty)$, uniformly in $D,X,\rho$.

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

### H13 feasibility note

H13 applies Poisson summation modulo $4$ to the M9a spatial-character sum before Cauchy--Schwarz:

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
\sum_d \chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d).
$$

The expected transform is

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
c
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}\chi_4(n)
\int_{\mathbb R}
w_D(u)e(hX/u-nu/4)\,du,
$$

with a convention-dependent constant $c$.

Stationary phase is active for one sign of $n$. Writing $n=-m$ with $m>0$, the phase is

$$
hX/u+mu/4
$$

up to sign convention. The stationary point satisfies

$$
u_0\asymp \sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}},
$$

so the dual length is

$$
m\asymp \frac{hX}{D^2}.
$$

At maximal Vaaler height

$$
h\asymp H_D\asymp DX^{-1/4}
$$

and with $D=X^\delta$,

$$
m\asymp X^{3/4-\delta}.
$$

Range table:

| $\delta$ | $D$ | $h_{\max}$ | H13 dual length $m$ |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| $1/4$ | $X^{1/4}$ | $X^0$ | $X^{1/2}$ |
| $3/8$ | $X^{3/8}$ | $X^{1/8}$ | $X^{3/8}$ |
| $1/2$ | $X^{1/2}$ | $X^{1/4}$ | $X^{1/4}$ |

Thus H13 is best balanced near $D=X^{1/2}$. It lengthens the dual variable for smaller $D$.

The leading dual phase is of square-root type:

$$
\Phi(h,m)\asymp \sqrt{Xhm}.
$$

Its Hessian is degenerate:

$$
\det \nabla^2 \Phi=0.
$$

Therefore H13 cannot be used with generic full-rank two-dimensional stationary phase or generic full-rank decoupling. It would need a discrete spacing theorem adapted to the square-root phase and the dual $\chi_4(m)$ character.

### H13 falsification checklist

H13 should be deprioritized if any of the following occurs:

1. The exact modulo-$4$ Poisson calculation produces only a residue selector that becomes a diagonal unitary under the first Cauchy--Schwarz step, with no signed statistic surviving.

2. The stationary-phase boundary terms are as large as the original M9a target in the endpoint range.

3. The transformed dual length

$$
m\asymp hX/D^2
$$

creates a longer and no easier sum for most $D<X^{1/2}$, with no special gain near $D=X^{1/2}$.

4. The first spacing or large-sieve step converts the dual $\chi_4(m)$ into a conjugacy-invariant operator norm, triggering the Q1-Spectral blindness mechanism.

5. The square-root phase degeneracy forces the same Bombieri--Iwaniec second-spacing obstruction without any new signed cancellation.

### Cross-residue interference as a sign-preserving test

A possible non-operator-norm statistic for M9a is to split

$$
S_\chi(h,D)
=
S_1(h,D)-S_3(h,D),
$$

where

$$
S_r(h,D)=
\sum_m w_D(4m+r)e(hX/(4m+r)),
\qquad r\in\{1,3\}.
$$

Then test the cross-residue statistic

$$
\mathcal C(D;X)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
S_1(h,D)\overline{S_3(h,D)}.
$$

This is not yet a lemma. It is a falsification object. If numerically and symbolically it behaves like the absolute-value matrix or a conjugacy-invariant trace, deprioritize it. If it shows systematic cancellation tied to residue interference, formulate a signed bilinear target.

### Mellin--Perron and signed Fourier

Do not pivot to these as main routes. They remain comparison modules.

Signed Fourier truncation formally preserves $\chi_4$ in the high-frequency tail, but near discontinuities it likely recreates small-denominator product-counting problems. Mellin--Perron preserves arithmetic at the Dirichlet-series level but likely reconstructs Hardy--Voronoi--Bessel phases after functional equation. Neither currently replaces M9.
