### 1. H4-R22: finite Vaaler approximation with floor-compatible residual

Use

$$
e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}.
$$

The relevant source is Jeffrey D. Vaaler, "Some extremal functions in Fourier analysis," *Bulletin of the American Mathematical Society* 12(2), 183--216, 1985. Project Euclid and AMS list the paper and bibliographic data. The source anchors in the uploaded Vaaler PDF are:

- Theorem 6, equation (2.28), for the Fourier transform of $J$:
$$
\widehat J(t)=\pi t(1-|t|)\cot(\pi t)+|t|,
\qquad 0<|t|<1.
$$
The parsed PDF gives this as equation (2.28).

- Section 7, equations (7.1)--(7.3), for $i_N,j_N,k_N$, where $k_N$ is the periodic Fejer kernel.

- Theorem 18, printed page 210, equations (7.13)--(7.17), especially the residual inequality
$$
|\psi*j_N(x)-\psi(x)|\le (2N+2)^{-1}k_N(x).
$$
This is equation (7.14).

Set $H=N$ and define

$$
K_H(t)=k_N(t)
=
\sum_{|k|\le H}
\left(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\right)e(kt)
=
\frac1{H+1}
\left(\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}\right)^2.
$$

Define

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u,
\qquad 0<u<1.
$$

The Vaaler coefficients in repo notation are:

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
\qquad 1\le |h|\le H.
$$

Thus the finite approximation is:

$$
\psi_F(t)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)
+
R_H^F(t),
$$

with

$$
|R_H^F(t)|\le \frac1{2H+2}K_H(t).
$$

This is H4-R22.

The endpoint convention is essential. Vaaler's periodic sawtooth is centered at discontinuities:

$$
\psi_C(t)=
\begin{cases}
t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12,&t\notin\mathbb Z,\\
0,&t\in\mathbb Z.
\end{cases}
$$

The arithmetic formula uses $\psi_F(n)=-1/2$. At integers, the Vaaler polynomial has value $0$, because positive and negative Fourier modes cancel. Also,

$$
K_H(0)=H+1,
\qquad
\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12.
$$

Therefore the same Fejer majorant covers the floor-compatible half-jump:

$$
|\psi_F(n)-V_H(n)|=\frac12.
$$

This closes the endpoint convention gap for H4, subject only to final proofreading of the exact page/equation labels in the proof draft.

### 2. Algebra check for the Vaaler coefficient conjugacy

For $h>0$,

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}
=
\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right),
$$

because $1/i=-i$. Since $\Phi$ is real-valued on $(0,1)$,

$$
\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}
=
-\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right).
$$

For $-h$,

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i(-h)}
=
-\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right).
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.}
$$

The relation $\alpha_{-h,H}=-\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$ is false and should remain a regression error flag.

### 3. Explicit check that $\Phi(1-u)\ne \Phi(u)$

The function is

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u.
$$

At $u=1/3$,

$$
\Phi(1/3)
=
\pi\frac13\frac23\cot(\pi/3)+\frac13
=
\frac{2\pi}{9\sqrt3}+\frac13.
$$

At $u=2/3$,

$$
\Phi(2/3)
=
\pi\frac23\frac13\cot(2\pi/3)+\frac23
=
-\frac{2\pi}{9\sqrt3}+\frac23.
$$

Numerically,

$$
\Phi(1/3)\approx 0.73638,
\qquad
\Phi(2/3)\approx 0.26362.
$$

Thus

$$
\Phi(1/3)\ne \Phi(2/3).
$$

All arguments using $\Phi(1-u)=\Phi(u)$ should be rejected. The correct evenness is in the Fourier transform variable $t\mapsto -t$ through $|t|$, not in $u\mapsto 1-u$ on $(0,1)$.

### 4. R5-Full-R22: Fejer residual product-count bound

**Lemma R5-Full-R22.** Let $X\ge2$ be real. Let

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2},
$$

and let $H$ be an integer satisfying

$$
c_0D X^{-1/4}\le H\le C_0D X^{-1/4}
$$

for fixed constants $0<c_0\le C_0$. Let $w_D$ be supported on $d\asymp D$ and satisfy $|w_D(d)|\le C_w$. Then, for every $\epsilon>0$,

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_{\epsilon,C_0,c_0,C_w} X^{1/4+\epsilon},
$$

and for $\rho\in\{1,3\}$,

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_{\epsilon,C_0,c_0,C_w} X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Consequently, assuming H4-R22, the total Vaaler residual over all active dyadic blocks and both sawtooth legs is

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

#### Fejer bound

The Fejer kernel satisfies

$$
K_H(t)\ll \min\left(H,\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\right),
$$

where

$$
\|t\|=\min_{m\in\mathbb Z}|t-m|.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

Set

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}.
$$

Since $H\asymp D X^{-1/4}$,

$$
\Delta\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

Define the exact-resonance-safe weight

$$
W_\Delta(u)=
\begin{cases}
1,&u=0,\\
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{u^2}\right),&u\ne0.
\end{cases}
$$

The value $W_\Delta(0)=1$ is required. It prevents singular notation in exact resonance cases such as $X/d\in\mathbb Z$ or $(X/d+\rho)/4\in\mathbb Z$.

#### First residual leg

For each $d\asymp D$, choose an integer $m=m(d)$ nearest to $X/d$. If there is a tie, choose either nearest integer by a fixed convention, for example the smaller one. The proof uses only

$$
\left|\frac Xd-m\right|=\left\|\frac Xd\right\|.
$$

Then

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
=
\frac{|X-md|}{d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-md|}{D},
$$

because $d\asymp D$. Hence

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)
\ll
W_\Delta(X-md).
$$

If $X-md=0$, then $K_H(X/d)=K_H(0)=H+1$, so

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)=1+\frac1H\le 2,
$$

which is covered by the constant multiple of $W_\Delta(0)=1$.

Group by the integer

$$
n=md.
$$

In the active range and for $X$ sufficiently large, $m>0$ because $X/d\gg X^{1/2}$. Small $X$ is handled separately below. For fixed $n$, the number of representations $n=md$ with integer $d$ is at most $\tau(n)$. Also, since $m$ is nearest to $X/d$,

$$
|X-n|\le \frac d2\ll D\le X^{1/2},
$$

so $n\asymp X$ for large $X$. Thus

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n).
$$

Using

$$
\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon
$$

and $n\asymp X$, it remains to bound

$$
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z} W_\Delta(X-n).
$$

The central region $|X-n|\le \Delta$ contributes $O(\Delta+1)$. The dyadic annulus

$$
2^{j-1}\Delta<|X-n|\le 2^j\Delta
$$

contributes

$$
O(2^j\Delta)\cdot O(2^{-2j})=O(2^{-j}\Delta).
$$

Summing over $j\ge1$ gives

$$
\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z} W_\Delta(X-n)\ll \Delta+1.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_\epsilon
X^\epsilon(\Delta+1)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

#### Shifted residual legs

For $\rho\in\{1,3\}$, set

$$
t_\rho(d)=\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}.
$$

Choose $m=m(d,\rho)$ nearest to $t_\rho(d)$, with ties broken by a fixed convention. Then

$$
\left\|t_\rho(d)\right\|
=
\frac{|X/d+\rho-4m|}{4}
=
\frac{|X-d(4m-\rho)|}{4d}.
$$

Let

$$
\ell=4m-\rho.
$$

Then

$$
\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4.
$$

For large $X$ in the active range, $\ell>0$. Indeed, $t_\rho(d)\gg X/d\gg X^{1/2}$ when $D\le X^{1/2}$, up to harmless absolute constants; for bounded $X$ we use the small-$X$ reduction below. Thus $n=d\ell$ is a positive integer and the congruence restriction on $\ell$ only reduces the number of factorizations.

The same Fejer bound gives

$$
\frac1H
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll
W_\Delta(X-d\ell).
$$

Group by

$$
n=d\ell,
\qquad
\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4.
$$

For fixed $n$, the number of representations is at most $\tau(n)$, with an additional congruence restriction. Therefore the same argument gives

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

#### Zero Fejer mode

The $k=0$ mode of $K_H$ contributes

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
\ll \frac{D}{H}
\asymp X^{1/4}.
$$

This is already included in the Fejer kernel estimate, but it is useful to record separately because it fixes the natural local height $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$.

#### Short blocks

For $D<X^{1/4}$, do not apply Vaaler. Since $|\psi_F(t)|\le 1/2$ for all real $t$, except that the same bound holds at integers with $\psi_F(n)=-1/2$, the total contribution from all denominators $d<X^{1/4}$ is

$$
O(X^{1/4})
$$

per sawtooth leg. The two shifts in the second leg do not change this.

#### Small $X$

For $1\le X<X_0$ with $X_0$ fixed sufficiently large, all sums contain $O_{X_0}(1)$ terms, so the desired bound holds after increasing the implicit constant. For $0<X<1$, the original lattice count is bounded and should be handled separately before invoking dyadic notation.

#### Dyadic overlap

Use a bounded-overlap partition of unity on $1\le d\le y$. The number of active dyadic blocks is $O(\log X)$. Each active residual block is $\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}$; after reducing $\epsilon$ in the block estimate, the logarithm is absorbed into $X^\epsilon$.

### 5. Bridge-R22: conditional reduction to M9

**Bridge-R22.** Assume:

1. H1--H3: the balanced floor-compatible sawtooth identity,
2. H4-R22: finite Vaaler approximation with Fejer residual,
3. R5-Full-R22: Fejer residual product-count bound,
4. M9-R22: fixed-coefficient main-term estimates,

then

$$
P(X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Proof sketch. Split both sawtooth legs in H3 into dyadic denominator blocks. Short blocks $D<X^{1/4}$ contribute $O(X^{1/4})$. On active blocks, apply H4 with $H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$. The residual terms are bounded by R5-Full-R22. The main trigonometric polynomial terms are exactly $\mathcal M_1(D;X)$ and $\mathcal M_2(D;X)$ below, and are bounded by M9-R22. Summing $O(\log X)$ dyadic blocks gives the claimed $X^{1/4+\epsilon}$ bound. The $O(1)$ residual in H3 is negligible.

This is a conditional theorem. It is not a proof of the conjectural Gauss circle bound because M9-R22 is open.

### 6. M9-R22: fixed-coefficient main-term target

For $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$ and

$$
H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4},
$$

define

$$
A_h(D;X)=
\sum_d\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),
$$

and

$$
B_h(D;X)=
\sum_d w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

The first main term is

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
-4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}A_h(D;X).
$$

The second main term is

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\left(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\right)
B_h(D;X).
$$

Since

$$
e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=2i\chi_4(h),
$$

this may also be written as

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
8i
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}\chi_4(h)B_h(D;X).
$$

The required estimate is:

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal M_1(D;X),\mathcal M_2(D;X)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}
}
$$

uniformly for all active dyadic $D$.

Status: open. The coefficients are the actual Vaaler coefficients. Arbitrary-coefficient versions remain stress tests only.

### 7. M9-Pair-R22: paired implementation formulas for real weights

Assume $w_D$ is real-valued. Then

$$
A_{-h}(D;X)=\overline{A_h(D;X)},
\qquad
B_{-h}(D;X)=\overline{B_h(D;X)}.
$$

Using $\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$, for $h>0$ one obtains

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
\frac4\pi
\sum_{1\le h\le H_D}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\operatorname{Im}A_h(D;X).
$$

For the second term, $e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=0$ for even $h$ and equals $2i\chi_4(h)$ for odd $h$. Pairing $h$ and $-h$ gives

\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
-\frac8\pi
\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\chi_4(h)
\operatorname{Re}B_h(D;X).
$$

Status: implementation lemma only. Every numerical implementation should regress raw two-sided complex sums against these paired formulas. If $w_D$ is complex-valued, these paired formulas need modification.

### 8. M9b-ShiftedF-R22 theorem-extension formulation

For M9b, the safer theorem-comparison form avoids treating $\chi_4(h)$ as a rough periodic weight. Use

$$
e(hX/(4d))(e(h/4)-e(3h/4))
=
e\left(h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac14\right)\right)
-

$$

After writing $d=Dz$,

$$
h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac{\rho}{4}\right)
=

\left(

\right).
$$

Thus define

F_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac1{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X},
\qquad

Derivative nondegeneracy is unchanged by the additive constant. Indeed,

F'_{\rho,D}(z)=-\frac1{4z^2},
\qquad

so

F'_{\rho,D}F'''_{\rho,D}-3(F''_{\rho,D})^2

### 9. M2-CS-Character-Blindness-R22

Let

$$
C_h=e(h/4)-e(3h/4).
$$

Then

$$
C_h=
\begin{cases}
2i\chi_4(h),&2\nmid h,\\
0,&2\mid h,
\end{cases}
$$

and

$$
|C_h|^2=
\begin{cases}
4,&2\nmid h,\\
0,&2\mid h.
\end{cases}
$$

#### Normalization 1: Cauchy--Schwarz over $h$ on the combined M2 sum

Write

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h B_h(D;X).
$$

Let

$$
L=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\ll\log X.
$$

Then

$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2
\le
16L
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\,|C_h|^2\,|B_h(D;X)|^2.
$$

After expansion, the sign $\chi_4(h)$ is gone; only the odd-$h$ restriction remains through $|C_h|^2$.

Status: proved diagnostic under this normalization. It is not a global no-go theorem.

#### Normalization 2: shifted-$F$ pieces separated first

Define

$$
B_{\rho,h}(D;X)=
e(h\rho/4)\sum_d w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)),
\qquad
\rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

For a fixed $\rho$, Cauchy--Schwarz over $h$ gives

$$
\sum_h |\alpha_h||B_{\rho,h}|^2.
$$

But

$$
|B_{\rho,h}|^2
=
\sum_{d_1,d_2}w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2)
e\left(h\frac X4\left(\frac1{d_1}-\frac1{d_2}\right)\right),
$$

because $e(h\rho/4)\overline{e(h\rho/4)}=1$. Thus the shift cancels in the square. If the two shifted pieces are recombined before Cauchy--Schwarz, the same loss appears as $|C_h|^2$.

Status: proved diagnostic under standard $h$-Cauchy--Schwarz. It shows that this common route is character-blind for $\mathcal M_2$.

### 10. CS-d-Diagonal-Obstruction-R22

A different attempt is to preserve the frequency character by applying Cauchy--Schwarz over $d$.

For M2, write schematically

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
4\sum_d w_D(d)
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h e(hX/(4d)).
$$

Cauchy--Schwarz over $d$ gives

$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2
\ll
D
\sum_{d\asymp D}
\left|
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h e(hX/(4d))
\right|^2.
$$

Expanding the square, the diagonal contribution $h_1=h_2$ is

$$
D
\sum_{d\asymp D}
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
|\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h|^2.
$$

Since $C_h$ is nonzero only for odd $h$ and $|C_h|=2$ there,

$$
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}|\alpha_{h,H_D}C_h|^2
\asymp
\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}\frac1{h^2}
\asymp 1.
$$

Therefore the diagonal size under this normalization is

$$
\asymp D^2.
$$

At the endpoint $D\asymp X^{1/2}$, this is $\asymp X$, whereas the target for $|\mathcal M_2|^2$ is $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$. Thus any Cauchy--Schwarz-over-$d$ proof that does not exploit substantial negative off-diagonal cancellation in the expanded square is too large by a factor $X^{1/2-o(1)}$ at the top dyadic range.

Status: proved diagonal-size calculation under the displayed normalization; diagnostic, not a theorem excluding all $d$-Cauchy strategies.
