## Detailed reasoning

### M2 Cauchy-Schwarz Normalizations and Character Blindness

We are tasked with rewriting the $\mathcal M_2$ Cauchy--Schwarz derivation with fixed normalizations to demonstrate exactly where the sign of $\chi_4(h)$ is lost. The raw fixed-coefficient main sum is:
$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X) = 4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} \alpha_{h,H_D} C_h \sum_{d\sim D} w_D(d)e\left(\frac{hX}{4d}\right),
$$
where $C_h = e(h/4)-e(3h/4) = 2i\chi_4(h)$, and $\alpha_{h,H_D} = -\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H_D+1))}{2\pi i h}$.

**Normalization 1: Cauchy--Schwarz weighted by $|\alpha_{h,H_D}|$.**
We split the outer sum using the weight $|\alpha_{h,H_D}|^{1/2}$.
$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2 \le 16 \left( \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} |\alpha_{h,H_D}| \right) \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} |\alpha_{h,H_D}| |C_h|^2 \left| \sum_{d\sim D} w_D(d)e\left(\frac{hX}{4d}\right) \right|^2.
$$
- **Scalar factor:** $16 L$, where $L = \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} |\alpha_{h,H_D}| \asymp \log H_D$.
- **Exact kernel:** Expanding the square over $d_1, d_2$, the kernel is
$$
K_{2;d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)} = w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2) \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} |\alpha_{h,H_D}| |C_h|^2 e\left(\frac{hX}{4}\left(\frac{1}{d_1}-\frac{1}{d_2}\right)\right).
$$
- **Diagonal size:** For $d_1 = d_2$, the exponential is $1$. The sum over $h$ is $\sum |\alpha_h| |C_h|^2 \asymp \log H_D$. Summing over $d \sim D$ gives a diagonal contribution of $\mathcal S_{\rm diag} \asymp D \log H_D$. Multiplied by the scalar factor $16L$, the total diagonal bound is $D (\log H_D)^2 \ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}$, which is acceptable.
- **Off-diagonal target:** To achieve $|\mathcal M_2|^2 \ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}$, the off-diagonal sum must satisfy $\mathcal S_{\rm off} \ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon} / L \asymp X^{1/2+\epsilon}$.
- **Character loss:** The term $|C_h|^2 = |2i\chi_4(h)|^2 = 4|\chi_4(h)|^2$ appears directly in the kernel. Since $|\chi_4(h)|^2 = 1$ for odd $h$ and $0$ for even $h$, the alternating sign of $\chi_4(h)$ is removed in this kernel, leaving only an odd-$h$ restriction.

**Normalization 2: Cauchy--Schwarz with unweighted $h$ sum.**
We apply Cauchy--Schwarz directly without extracting $|\alpha_h|$ into the scalar factor.
$$
|\mathcal M_2(D;X)|^2 \le 16 \left( \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} 1 \right) \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} |\alpha_{h,H_D}|^2 |C_h|^2 \left| \sum_{d\sim D} w_D(d)e\left(\frac{hX}{4d}\right) \right|^2.
$$
- **Scalar factor:** $16 H_D \asymp D X^{-1/4}$.
- **Exact kernel:**
$$
K_{2;d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|^2)} = w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2) \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} |\alpha_{h,H_D}|^2 |C_h|^2 e\left(\frac{hX}{4}\left(\frac{1}{d_1}-\frac{1}{d_2}\right)\right).
$$
- **Diagonal size:** For $d_1 = d_2$, the sum over $h$ is $\sum |\alpha_h|^2 |C_h|^2 \asymp \sum 1/h^2 \asymp 1$. Summing over $d \sim D$ gives $\mathcal S_{\rm diag} \asymp D$. Multiplied by the scalar factor $16 H_D$, the total diagonal bound is $D H_D \asymp D^2 X^{-1/4} \le X^{3/4}$. This exceeds the squared target $X^{1/2+\epsilon}$ at the endpoint, showing that Normalization 2 suffers from diagonal blowup.
- **Character loss:** Again, $|C_h|^2$ appears, erasing the sign of $\chi_4(h)$.

**Conclusion:** Of these two Cauchy--Schwarz paths, Normalization 1 is the better candidate. It still requires a theorem capable of bounding the odd-$h$ restricted, character-blind reciprocal kernel $K_{2;d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}$ at endpoint height $H_D \asymp D X^{-1/4}$.

### M2-NonConjugacy-FourthMoment-R22 Definition and Classification

To avoid the $|C_h|^2$ positivity trap, we define an explicit finite fourth-moment statistic. Let $S_h = \sum_{d\sim D} w_D(d) e(hX/(4d))$. We wish to bound $\mathcal M_2 = 4 \sum_h \alpha_h C_h S_h$.
We expand the fourth moment $\mathcal S_{4th} = \left| \sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D} \alpha_h C_h S_h \right|^4$:
$$
\mathcal S_{4th} = \sum_{h_1,h_2,h_3,h_4} \alpha_{h_1}\alpha_{h_2}\overline{\alpha_{h_3}\alpha_{h_4}} C_{h_1}C_{h_2}\overline{C_{h_3}C_{h_4}} S_{h_1}S_{h_2}\overline{S_{h_3}S_{h_4}}.
$$
Crucially, $C_{h_1}C_{h_2}\overline{C_{h_3}C_{h_4}} = 16 \chi_4(h_1)\chi_4(h_2)\chi_4(h_3)\chi_4(h_4)$, which preserves the character signs.
Expanding the $S_{h_i}$ terms yields an 8-variable sum over $h_1,h_2,h_3,h_4$ and $d_1,d_2,d_3,d_4$:
$$
\mathcal S_{4th} = 16 \sum_{\mathbf{h}} \sum_{\mathbf{d}} \left( \prod_{i=1}^2 \alpha_{h_i}\chi_4(h_i)w_D(d_i) \right) \left( \prod_{j=3}^4 \overline{\alpha_{h_j}}\chi_4(h_j)w_D(d_j) \right) e\left( \frac{X}{4} \left( \frac{h_1}{d_1} + \frac{h_2}{d_2} - \frac{h_3}{d_3} - \frac{h_4}{d_4} \right) \right).
$$
**Target Scale:** To imply $\mathcal M_2 \ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}$, we require $\mathcal S_{4th} \ll_\epsilon X^{1+\epsilon}$.

**Classification of Configurations:**
1. **Diagonal ($h_1=h_3, h_2=h_4$ and permutations):**
The contribution is bounded by $2 \left( \sum_h |\alpha_h|^2 |C_h|^2 |S_h|^2 \right)^2$.
One must determine if this diagonal exceeds $X^{1+\epsilon}$.
$\sum_h |\alpha_h|^2 |S_h|^2 = \sum_h |\alpha_h|^2 \sum_{d_1, d_2} w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2) e\left(\frac{hX}{4}\left(\frac{1}{d_1}-\frac{1}{d_2}\right)\right)$.
The $d_1=d_2$ sub-diagonal is $\sum_h |\alpha_h|^2 \sum_d w_D(d)^2 \asymp D \sum 1/h^2 \asymp D$.
If the $d_1 \neq d_2$ off-diagonal exhibits square-root cancellation such that the total sum is $\ll_\epsilon D X^\epsilon$, then the $h$-diagonal of the fourth moment is $\ll_\epsilon D^2 X^\epsilon \le X^{1+\epsilon}$.
Thus, the diagonal is acceptable *conditional* on $L^2$ square-root cancellation of $S_h$.
2. **Semi-diagonal ($h_1+h_2 = h_3+h_4$):**
This restricts the 4-variable $h$ sum to 3 degrees of freedom. The phase becomes highly structured. If $d_1=d_3$ and $d_2=d_4$, the phase vanishes exactly when $h_1+h_2 = h_3+h_4$. The number of such $h$-solutions is $\asymp H_D^3$. The contribution is $H_D^3 D^2 \times (1/H_D^4) \asymp D^2 / H_D \asymp D X^{1/4} \le X^{3/4} \ll X^{1+\epsilon}$. This is also acceptable.
3. **Off-diagonal (generic $\mathbf{h}, \mathbf{d}$):**
This requires a true 8-variable signed spacing theorem.

**Conditional Implication:** [DERIVED-UNDER-ASSUMPTIONS] If the off-diagonal and semi-diagonal configurations of $\mathcal S_{4th}$ are bounded by $X^{1+\epsilon}$, and if $\sum_h |\alpha_h|^2 |S_h|^2 \ll_\epsilon D X^\epsilon$, then $\mathcal M_2(D;X) \ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}$.

### CRI-M2-Statistic-R22 Definition and Implication

The Cross-Residue Interference (CRI) statistic attempts to capture cancellation between $h \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ and $h \equiv 3 \pmod 4$ before squaring destroys the sign.
Let $\Sigma_1 = \sum_{h \equiv 1} \alpha_h S_h$ and $\Sigma_3 = \sum_{h \equiv 3} \alpha_h S_h$.
Then $\mathcal M_2 = 8i (\Sigma_1 - \Sigma_3)$.
$|\mathcal M_2|^2 = 64 \left( |\Sigma_1|^2 + |\Sigma_3|^2 - 2 \Re(\Sigma_1 \overline{\Sigma_3}) \right)$.
We define the finite CRI statistic as:
$$
\mathcal S_{CRI} = \Sigma_1 \overline{\Sigma_3} = \sum_{h_1 \equiv 1} \sum_{h_3 \equiv 3} \alpha_{h_1} \overline{\alpha_{h_3}} \sum_{d_1, d_3} w_D(d_1)w_D(d_3) e\left( \frac{X}{4} \left( \frac{h_1}{d_1} - \frac{h_3}{d_3} \right) \right).
$$
**Target Scale:** We require $|\mathcal M_2|^2 \ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}$.
**Implication:** Bounding $\mathcal S_{CRI} \ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}$ is *insufficient* to bound $|\mathcal M_2|^2$ because it does not control the positive definite terms $|\Sigma_1|^2$ and $|\Sigma_3|^2$. For CRI to become a proof lemma, one would need an asymptotic expansion where $\Re(\mathcal S_{CRI})$ cancels the main diagonal terms of $|\Sigma_1|^2 + |\Sigma_3|^2$.
Therefore, `CRI-M2-Statistic-R22` is strictly a **falsification statistic**. If numerical tests show that $\mathcal S_{CRI}$ is positive or fails to cancel the diagonals of $\Sigma_1, \Sigma_3$, the CRI route is falsified.

### Direct Signed Bilinear Forms for M1

To avoid the operator-norm extraction trap for $\mathcal M_1$, one can expand $|\mathcal M_1|^2$ directly without applying Cauchy--Schwarz over $h$.
$$
|\mathcal M_1|^2 = 16 \sum_{1\le |h_1|,|h_2|\le H_D} \alpha_{h_1} \overline{\alpha_{h_2}} \sum_{d_1, d_2} \chi_4(d_1)\chi_4(d_2) w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2) e\left(X\left(\frac{h_1}{d_1} - \frac{h_2}{d_2}\right)\right).
$$
We group the terms by the rational collision parameter $\Delta = h_1 d_2 - h_2 d_1$.
The phase becomes $e\left( \frac{X \Delta}{d_1 d_2} \right)$.
Using the `Q1-Ext` character factorization, for exact collisions $\Delta = 0$, we have $h_1 d_2 = h_2 d_1$. Let $h_1/d_1 = h_2/d_2 = q/r$ in lowest terms. Then $d_1 = r c_1, d_2 = r c_2$, and $\chi_4(d_1)\chi_4(d_2) = \chi_4(r)^2 \chi_4(c_1 c_2)$.
This formulation keeps the sum over $h_1, h_2$ outside any absolute values, preserving the highly oscillatory coefficients $\alpha_{h_1} \overline{\alpha_{h_2}}$ and the spatial character $\chi_4(d_1)\chi_4(d_2)$.

### Q1-Spectral and H12 Bounded Scope

The scope of the character-blindness diagnostics should remain bounded to prevent route-closing overclaims.
**Q1-Spectral-R22:**
Let $\mathcal V_D = \ell^2(\mathcal D_{\rm odd})$ where $\mathcal D_{\rm odd} = \{d: D \le d < 2D, 2\nmid d\}$.
Define the diagonal operator $U = \text{diag}(\chi_4(d))$. Since $\chi_4(d) \in \{\pm 1\}$ for odd $d$, $U$ is a unitary involution ($U^* U = I$).
For any matrix $K$ acting on $\mathcal V_D$, the operator norm is unitarily invariant:
$$
\|U^* K U\|_{\operatorname{op}} = \sup_{\|v\|=1} \|U^* K U v\| = \sup_{\|w\|=1} \|K w\| = \|K\|_{\operatorname{op}},
$$
where $w = Uv$ and $\|w\| = \|v\|$ because $U$ is unitary.
[PROVED] **Blocked Methods:** This strictly blocks any proof path that factors the spatial character into $U^* K U$ and subsequently applies the operator norm, Frobenius norm, spectral radius, Schur/Gershgorin bounds, or absolute-value matrix majorization.
[PROVED] **Unblocked Methods:** This does *not* block direct signed bilinear forms (as defined in the previous section), open-path moments, non-conjugacy traces, residue interference statistics, or any new signed spacing theorem that evaluates the quadratic form $v^* (U^* K U) v$ without passing to a supremum over all vectors.

**H12-Trace-R22:**
[PROVED] For pure cyclic traces, $\operatorname{Tr}((U^* K U)^m) = \operatorname{Tr}(U^* K^m U) = \operatorname{Tr}(K^m U U^*) = \operatorname{Tr}(K^m)$.
This blocks pure conjugacy-invariant cyclic trace statistics. It explicitly does *not* block open-path moments (e.g., $v^* (U^* K U)^m v$) or cross-residue statistics.

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

**Exploration: Non-Conjugacy Statistic for $\mathcal M_2$ via Residue Pairing**

The primary obstacle for $\mathcal M_2$ is that standard Cauchy--Schwarz over $h$ squares the character factor $C_h = e(h/4)-e(3h/4) = 2i\chi_4(h)$, resulting in $|C_h|^2 = 4 \cdot \mathbf{1}_{2\nmid h}$, which destroys the alternating sign. To bypass this, we explore a non-conjugacy statistic that pairs $h \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ against $h \equiv 3 \pmod 4$ *before* squaring.

Let $S_h = \sum_{d\sim D} w_D(d) e(hX/(4d))$. We split the main sum into residue classes:
$$
\mathcal M_2 = 4 \sum_{h \equiv 1 \pmod 4} \alpha_h (2i) S_h + 4 \sum_{h \equiv 3 \pmod 4} \alpha_h (-2i) S_h = 8i (\Sigma_1 - \Sigma_3),
$$
where $\Sigma_1 = \sum_{h \equiv 1} \alpha_h S_h$ and $\Sigma_3 = \sum_{h \equiv 3} \alpha_h S_h$.

Instead of bounding $|\Sigma_1|$ and $|\Sigma_3|$ separately (which would require character-blind estimates), we expand the square of the difference:
$$
|\mathcal M_2|^2 = 64 \left( |\Sigma_1|^2 + |\Sigma_3|^2 - 2 \Re(\Sigma_1 \overline{\Sigma_3}) \right).
$$
The cross-term is the Cross-Residue Interference (CRI) statistic:
$$
\mathcal S_{CRI} = \Sigma_1 \overline{\Sigma_3} = \sum_{h_1 \equiv 1} \sum_{h_3 \equiv 3} \alpha_{h_1} \overline{\alpha_{h_3}} S_{h_1} \overline{S_{h_3}}.
$$
Expanding $S_{h_1}$ and $\overline{S_{h_3}}$:
$$
\mathcal S_{CRI} = \sum_{h_1 \equiv 1} \sum_{h_3 \equiv 3} \alpha_{h_1} \overline{\alpha_{h_3}} \sum_{d_1, d_3} w_D(d_1)w_D(d_3) e\left( \frac{X}{4} \left( \frac{h_1}{d_1} - \frac{h_3}{d_3} \right) \right).
$$
**Mechanism of Action:**
Notice that the coefficients in $\mathcal S_{CRI}$ are $\alpha_{h_1} \overline{\alpha_{h_3}}$. Because $h_1 \equiv 1$ and $h_3 \equiv 3$, we are explicitly multiplying terms with opposite character signs. The product $C_{h_1} \overline{C_{h_3}} = (2i)(\overline{-2i}) = (2i)(2i) = -4$. This negative sign is preserved in the expansion of $|\mathcal M_2|^2$, appearing as $-2 \Re(-4 \dots) = +8 \Re(\dots)$. Wait, the expansion is $|\Sigma_1 - \Sigma_3|^2$. The cross term is $-2 \Re(\Sigma_1 \overline{\Sigma_3})$.
If $\mathcal S_{CRI}$ has a specific asymptotic expansion, its main term can exactly cancel the diagonal main terms of $|\Sigma_1|^2$ and $|\Sigma_3|^2$.

**Target Scale and Falsification:**
For this route to succeed, we do not merely need $\mathcal S_{CRI} \ll X^{1/2+\epsilon}$. We need the *combination* $|\Sigma_1|^2 + |\Sigma_3|^2 - 2 \Re(\mathcal S_{CRI}) \ll X^{1/2+\epsilon}$.
**Falsification Test:** A3 should compute $|\Sigma_1|^2$, $|\Sigma_3|^2$, and $2 \Re(\mathcal S_{CRI})$ for $D \asymp X^{1/4}, X^{3/8}, X^{1/2}$. If $2 \Re(\mathcal S_{CRI})$ is negative, or if it is positive but fails to cancel the bulk of $|\Sigma_1|^2 + |\Sigma_3|^2$, then the CRI statistic does not provide the necessary cancellation, and this specific non-conjugacy route is falsified.
