## Detailed reasoning

### 1.  $\Phi$-function and coefficient conjugacy

The Vaaler coefficient function is

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u,\qquad 0<u<1,
$$

with continuous extensions $\Phi(0)=\Phi(1)=1$ (by limit).
Direct evaluation yields the exact special values

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Phi\!\left(\tfrac12\right) &= \frac12,\\[2mm]
\Phi\!\left(\tfrac14\right) &= \frac{3\pi}{16}+\frac14,\\[2mm]
\Phi\!\left(\tfrac34\right) &= -\frac{3\pi}{16}+\frac34,\\[2mm]
\Phi\!\left(\tfrac13\right) &= \frac{2\pi}{9\sqrt3}+\frac13,\\[2mm]
\Phi\!\left(\tfrac23\right) &= -\frac{2\pi}{9\sqrt3}+\frac23 .
\end{aligned}
$$

In particular $\Phi(1/3)\neq\Phi(2/3)$ and $\Phi(1/4)\neq\Phi(3/4)$.
The earlier claim $\Phi(1-u)=\Phi(u)$ is **false**, because

$$
\Phi(1-u) = 1-u-\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u) \neq \Phi(u)
$$

except at $u=1/2$.  This false symmetry should be removed from the lemma bank
and all code comments.

The finite Vaaler coefficient is

$$
\alpha_{h,H} = -\frac{\Phi\!\bigl(|h|/(H+1)\bigr)}{2\pi i\,h},
\qquad 1\le |h|\le H .
$$

Because $h\mapsto |h|$ is even and $\Phi$ is real, we have

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}=
-\frac{\Phi(|-h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i\,(-h)}
= \frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i\,h}
= \overline{\alpha_{h,H}} .
$$

Thus the correct conjugacy is

$$
\boxed{\;\alpha_{-h,H} = \overline{\alpha_{h,H}}\;},
$$

and the relation $\alpha_{-h,H}=-\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$ is **rejected**.
All numerical implementations should use the conjugate form.

### 2.  Paired real-weight formulas for $\mathcal M_1,\mathcal M_2$

Let

$$
A_h(D;X)=\sum_{d\asymp D}\chi_4(d)\,w_D(d)\,e\!\left(\frac{hX}{d}\right),\qquad
B_h(D;X)=\sum_{d\asymp D}w_D(d)\,e\!\left(\frac{hX}{4d}\right),
$$

with real dyadic weights $w_D$.  The raw two-sided complex definitions are

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal M_1(D;X) &= -4\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}\,A_h(D;X),\\
\mathcal M_2(D;X) &= 4\sum_{1\le|h|\le H_D}\alpha_{h,H_D}\,
                    \bigl(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\bigr)\,B_h(D;X).
\end{aligned}
$$

Because $w_D$ is real, $A_{-h}=\overline{A_h}$ and $B_{-h}=\overline{B_h}$.
Using $\alpha_{-h}=\overline{\alpha_h}$ and writing $\alpha_h =
i\,\Phi(|h|/(H+1))/(2\pi h)$ (purely imaginary), we pair the positive and
negative frequencies:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal M_1(D;X) &=
\frac4\pi\sum_{h=1}^{H_D}
\frac{\Phi\!\bigl(\frac{h}{H_D+1}\bigr)}{h}\,
\operatorname{Im} A_h(D;X),\\[2mm]
\mathcal M_2(D;X) &=
-\frac8\pi\!
\sum_{\substack{h=1\\2\nmid h}}^{H_D}
\frac{\Phi\!\bigl(\frac{h}{H_D+1}\bigr)}{h}\,
\chi_4(h)\;
\operatorname{Re} B_h(D;X).
\end{aligned}
$$

These formulas are **implementation lemmas for real weights**; they should be
regressed numerically against the raw two-sided complex sums at several
$(X,D,H_D)$ to guard against coding errors.

### 3.  High-precision R5 residual tables

The Fejer kernel is

$$
K_H(t) = \sum_{|k|\le H}\Bigl(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\Bigr)e(kt)
       = \frac1{H+1}\Bigl(\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}\Bigr)^2 .
$$

The R5 product-count bound uses the pointwise majorant

$$
K_H(t)\ll \min\!\Bigl(H,\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\Bigr).
$$

For the first residual leg we need

$$
R_1(D;X)=\frac1{H_D}\sum_{d\asymp D}
K_{H_D}\!\left(\frac{X}{d}\right),
$$

and for the shifted legs ($\rho=1,3$)

$$
R_{2,\rho}(D;X)=\frac1{H_D}\sum_{d\asymp D}
K_{H_D}\!\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right).
$$

**Exact-resonance protocol.**
When $X/d$ (or $(X/d+\rho)/4$) is an integer, the Fejer kernel attains its
maximum $K_H(0)=H+1$.  The ordinary sine quotient is then $0/0$; a naive
floating-point evaluation may produce severe cancellation or
division-by-zero.  The safe implementation should:

* Detect integer arguments *before* evaluating the sine quotient.
* For integer $t$, set $K_H(t)=H+1$ directly.
* For non-integer $t$, use a high-precision library (e.g., Python's `mpmath`
  with 80 decimal digits) so that the sine cancellation near integers is
  resolved correctly.

**Dyadic blocks.**
For $X=10^6$ and $10^8$, the dyadic scales are taken as
$D\in\{ \lfloor X^{1/4}\rfloor,\;\lfloor X^{3/8}\rfloor,\;\lfloor X^{1/2}\rfloor\}$,
and the sum over $d$ is restricted to the dyadic interval $[D,2D)$ (or
$[D,2D]$ with consistent half-open conventions).
The local Vaaler height is $H_D = \lfloor C\, D\, X^{-1/4}\rfloor$ with
$C\approx 1$ chosen so that $H_D\ge 1$ for the smallest $D$ in the active
range.  For $D< X^{1/4}$ the blocks are excluded from the Vaaler expansion
and bounded trivially.

### 4.  Fixed-coefficient versus stress-norm comparisons

For each dyadic $D$, compute $\mathcal M_1,\mathcal M_2$ with

These comparisons are **diagnostic only**: they test whether the fixed Vaaler
coefficients carry significantly more cancellation than generic coefficients
of the same size profile.  They do not constitute a proof of any bound.

### 7.  H13 stationary-phase regime split

For the B-process-first transform, the relevant oscillatory integral is

$$
I(\xi)=\int w_D(u)\,e\!\Bigl(\frac{hX}{u}-\frac{\xi u}{4}\Bigr)\,du,
\qquad \xi\in\mathbb R.
$$

The stationary point exists only for $\xi<0$; writing $\xi=-m$,

$$
u_0 = 2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}},\qquad
\phi_{-m}(u_0) = \sqrt{hXm},\qquad
m\asymp \frac{hX}{D^2}.
$$

Four regimes should be distinguished:

* **Interior stationary:** $m\asymp hX/D^2$, with $u_0$ well inside the
  support of $w_D$ (i.e., $u_0\asymp D$).  Leading amplitude
  $\asymp D^{3/2}(hX)^{-1/2}$; the phase is square-root type, and
  $\det\nabla^2\sqrt{Xhm}=0$.
* **Boundary stationary:** $u_0$ near the edge of the support; standard
  stationary phase should be modified, typically introducing an extra factor
  of $\eta^{1/2}$.
* **Non-stationary:** $m$ far from $hX/D^2$; repeated integration by parts
  gives rapid (but not exponential) decay, depending on the smoothness of
  $w_D$.
* **Short dual length:** $hX/D^2\asymp 1$, i.e., the active $m$ range is
  only $O(1)$.  This occurs near $D\asymp X^{1/2},\;h\asymp 1$ and requires a
  separate finite-dimensional analysis; uniform asymptotics are delicate.

No double-sum estimate can be inferred from a single-integral stationary-phase
lemma until all regimes are treated uniformly.

**Endpoint falsification test (plan).**  At the most balanced H13 regime
$D\asymp X^{1/2}$ and $h$ near the maximal Vaaler height
$H_D\asymp X^{1/4}$, the dual length is $m\asymp X^{1/4}$.  We propose:

* Build the dual Gram matrix using the stationary-phase amplitudes and
  $\chi_4(m_1)\chi_4(m_2)$ factors.
* Compute the signed quadratic form and compare with the unsigned
  (absolute-value) form and with the operator norm.
* If the signed form is not significantly smaller than the unsigned /
  operator-norm comparator, H13 likely re-enters Q1-Spectral diagonal-unitary
  blindness, meaning the dual character does not provide cancellations beyond
  those already captured by absolute-value bounds.

This test is purely diagnostic; it is not a proof that H13 "must" fail.

## Hidden assumptions and potential gaps

1. **Fejer resonance handling in R5.**  The proof uses the inequality
   $K_H(t)\lesssim \min(H,1/(H\|t\|^2))$ and replaces $\|X/d\|$ by
   $|X-md|/D$.  This assumes that the nearest integer $m$ is chosen
   deterministically and that the implicit constant in $\asymp$ is harmless.
   At exact half-integer distances a tie-breaking rule should be specified, and
   the factor $W_\Delta(u)=\min(1,\Delta^2/u^2)$ for $u\neq 0$ should be
   adjusted at $u=0$ via the limiting value $1$ (already done).  The proof
   should also handle the case where $X/d$ is exactly half-integer, because then
   $\|X/d\|=1/2$ and the bound $K_H(t)\lesssim 1/(H(1/2)^2)=4/H$ still holds;
   no additional problem.

2. **Short dyadic blocks.**  For $D<X^{1/4}$ the local height $H_D$ would be
   $<1$; the argument excludes those blocks before Vaaler expansion, bounding
   them trivially by $|\psi|\le 1/2$.  The summation over all dyadic blocks
   then yields an $O(X^{1/4}\log X)$ contribution.  This is already factored
   into R5-Full.  The numerical tests should confirm that blocks with
   $D<X^{1/4}$ are indeed omitted from the Vaaler residual tables.

3. **Real-weight assumption for paired formulas.**  The derived
   $\operatorname{Im} A_h$ and $\operatorname{Re} B_h$ forms assume that
   $w_D$ is real and that the dyadic partition uses real cutoffs.  If complex
   weights are ever introduced (e.g., for smoothed Fourier weights), the
   paired formulas are no longer valid and the raw two-sided complex
   definitions should be used.  The regression test will catch any mismatch.

4. **H13 uniform stationary phase.**  The four regimes listed in Section 7 are
   known in principle, but a complete uniform lemma with explicit error bounds
   for non-analytic weights is still missing.  Until such a lemma is supplied,
   any asymptotic inference drawn from H13 remains conditional.  In
   particular, the boundary stationary case and the short-dual-length regime
   require separate analysis; the simple leading-term stationary phase does
   not extend uniformly.

5. **BSOS/M2 kernels not yet frozen.**  The numerical tests for signed
   statistics cannot be executed until A2 supplies the exact matrix entries
   and the exact Cauchy-Schwarz normalization for M2.  The current plan only
   outlines the computational architecture; the actual signed/absolute ratios
   depend critically on the chosen kernel.

6. **M9b shifted-$F$ theorem audit.**  Even though the derivative
   non-degeneracy condition $F'F'''-3(F'')^2=-\frac38 z^{-6}$ is satisfied
   for $F_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac1{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X}$, the presence of the
   $D,X$-dependent additive shift may violate the absolute derivative
   bounds required by the Li--Yang hypotheses (the constants $C_1,\dots,C_4$
   must be independent of $X$).  This gap should be examined before claiming
   even structural compatibility at endpoint height.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

* **Fejer spikes.**  If $X/d\in\mathbb Z$ or $(X/d+\rho)/4\in\mathbb Z$, the
  Fejer kernel attains its maximum $H+1$.  The R5 proof bounds these cases
  by $W_\Delta(0)=1$, but a careless implementation using the sine quotient
  with floating-point arithmetic may encounter severe cancellation or
  division by zero.  The numerical tests should explicitly detect these exact
  resonances and use the known exact value.

* **Divisor-rich $X$.**  For $X$ with many divisors (e.g., $X=10^6$ has
  moderate divisor count; $X=10^8$ more so) the product-count bound becomes
  saturated, yet the R5 proof still predicts $O(X^{1/4+\epsilon})$.  If
  numerical experiments show growth significantly larger than $X^{1/4}$, that
  would indicate a flaw in the divisor-bound argument.

* **M9b shifted-$F$ constants.**  The derivative determinant
  $F'F'''-3(F'')^2=-\frac38 z^{-6}$ for $F_{\rho,D}$ is independent of
  $X$ and $D$, but the lower bound on $|F'F'''-3(F'')^2|$ in the Li--Yang
  hypotheses should be uniform over the whole family.  The additive constant
  $\rho D/(4X)$ shifts the argument but does not change the derivative
  values; however, the bounds on $|F'|,|F''|,|F'''|$ may depend on $D$ and
  $X$.  A systematic check is required.

* **H13 dual Hessian.**  The phase $\sqrt{Xhm}$ has zero Hessian, so generic
  full-rank two-dimensional stationary phase or decoupling cannot be applied.
  This is a proved diagnostic; any claim that H13 automatically yields a
  saving via standard stationary phase is invalid.

* **H13 product collapse.**  If one formally collapses the leading model by
  $k=hm$, the expression may resemble a classical Hardy/Voronoi
  one-dimensional expansion.  This warns that H13 may not be genuinely new;
  however, the diagnostic is not a lemma until the amplitude, support, and
  error terms are fully written.

### Numerical verification (protocols)

For the R5 residuals, a reference implementation would follow this pattern:

```python
from mpmath import mp
mp.dps = 80

def K_H(t, H):
    # t is mp.mpf
    if t == int(t):
        return float(H+1)  # exact resonance
    s = mp.sin(mp.pi*(H+1)*t)
    s1 = mp.sin(mp.pi*t)
    return float((s/s1)**2 / (H+1))

def R1(D, X, H):
    total = mp.mpf(0)
    for d in range(D, 2*D):
        t = mp.mpf(X) / d
        total += K_H(t, H)
    return float(total / H)
```

The shifted legs are analogous; the exact-resonance detection can be
modular: if $X\bmod d = 0$, then $t$ is integer; or for the shifted case,
if $(X/d+\rho)/4$ is integer.

For M9 raw-vs-paired regression, one would compute both forms and compare
absolute and relative differences.  Because both forms are algebraically
identical for real weights, the differences should be at the level of rounding
error when using the same precision.  Any discrepancy larger than
$10^{-12}$ would signal an implementation bug.

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

The residual side of the Vaaler route is now provisionally under control, and
the active bottleneck remains M9.  While the instructions for this round
focus on verification, a brief exploration of two alternatives is in order.

1. **Non-conjugacy signed statistic for $\mathcal M_2$ (Alternative A).**
   Instead of the Cauchy-Schwarz kernel that loses $\chi_4(h)$, one could
   consider a fourth-moment expansion that keeps
   $\chi_4(h_1)\chi_4(h_2)\chi_4(h_3)\chi_4(h_4)$ before any absolute value
   is taken.  The challenge is that the resulting diagonal and semi-diagonal
   contributions may dominate unless the average sign cancels.  A promising
   route is to restrict the $h$-variables to a single residue class modulo
   $4$ and examine the cross-correlation, but this quickly leads to
   divisor-problem sums that are as hard as the original.  The short-term
   value is to implement a toy fourth-moment statistic and measure the
   signed-vs-unsigned ratio; if the ratio is not substantially below $1$, the
   approach is unlikely to yield an exponent improvement.

2. **H13 asymmetric dual-Cauchy-Schwarz (Alternative B).**
   The B-process-first transform changes the phase to $\sqrt{Xhm}$,
   which is Hessian-degenerate.  However, one might still attempt a
   one-dimensional spacing estimate in one variable while treating the other
   as a parameter, or use a mixed norm.  The immediate falsification test is
   the endpoint matrix test described in Section 7.  If the signed dual
   statistic fails to show any advantage, H13 should be demoted to a
   comparison module.

Both alternatives require finite, executable tests before they can be
considered serious proof candidates.  The present report prioritizes the
verification of the main route's infrastructure, which is a prerequisite for
any meaningful exploration.
