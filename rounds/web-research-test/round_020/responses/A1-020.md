## Detailed reasoning

### 1. H4-R20: finite Vaaler approximation with floor-compatible residual

Use the Fourier convention

$$
e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}.
$$

Vaaler defines periodic trigonometric polynomials $i_N,j_N,k_N$ in Section 7, printed page 207, equations (7.1)--(7.3); $k_N$ is identified with the periodic Fejer kernel. Vaaler Theorem 18, printed page 210, states that the trigonometric polynomial $\psi*j_N$ satisfies equations (7.13)--(7.15), with the residual bound

$$
|\psi*j_N(x)-\psi(x)|\le (2N+2)^{-1}k_N(x)
$$

as equation (7.14). Vaaler Theorem 6 gives the coefficient function through the Fourier transform formula for $J$,

$$
\widehat J(t)=\pi t(1-|t|)\cot(\pi t)+|t|,
\qquad 0<|t|<1.
$$

The project's proof draft should quote this as Theorem 6, printed page 192, equation (2.28), with the caveat that OCR sometimes places the displayed formula across the page break into printed page 193. The metadata and theorem anchors are stable, but the final repo citation should be checked against the rendered page image before commit.

The notation translation is:

| Vaaler notation | Repo notation | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| $N$ | $H$ | Vaaler truncation degree equals repo Fourier height. |
| $\psi*j_N$ | $V_H$ | Vaaler trigonometric approximation to the centered sawtooth. |
| $k_N$ | $K_H$ | Periodic Fejer kernel. |
| $\widehat J_{N+1}(h)$ | $\Phi(|h|/(H+1))$ | Scaled Fourier coefficient from Theorem 6. |
| $(2N+2)^{-1}k_N$ | $(2H+2)^{-1}K_H$ | Residual majorant. |

The Fejer kernel is

$$
K_H(t)
=
\sum_{|k|\le H}
\left(1-\frac{|k|}{H+1}\right)e(kt)
=
\frac1{H+1}
\left(\frac{\sin\pi(H+1)t}{\sin\pi t}\right)^2.
$$

For $0<u<1$, set

$$
\Phi(u)=\pi u(1-u)\cot(\pi u)+u.
$$

Then the repo Vaaler coefficients are

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
\qquad
1\le |h|\le H.
$$

Thus the proof-draft statement is:

**H4-R20.** For every integer $H\ge1$ and every real $t$,

$$
\psi_F(t)
=
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}
\alpha_{h,H}e(ht)
+
R_H^F(t),
$$

where

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(|h|/(H+1))}{2\pi i h},
$$

and

$$
|R_H^F(t)|\le \frac1{2H+2}K_H(t).
$$

### 2. Endpoint convention and coefficient pairing

Vaaler's periodic sawtooth is centered at discontinuities. Write

$$
\psi_C(t)=
\begin{cases}
t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12,&t\notin\mathbb Z,\\
0,&t\in\mathbb Z.
\end{cases}
$$

The arithmetic identity uses

$$
\psi_F(t)=t-\lfloor t\rfloor-\frac12
$$

for all real $t$, so

$$
\psi_F(n)=-\frac12,\qquad \psi_C(n)=0.
$$

For integer $n$,

$$
V_H(n)=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H}\alpha_{h,H}=0
$$

because the $h$ and $-h$ terms cancel. Also

$$
K_H(0)=H+1,
\qquad
\frac{K_H(0)}{2H+2}=\frac12.
$$

Thus Vaaler's residual majorant covers the floor-compatible half-jump exactly:

$$
|\psi_F(n)-V_H(n)|=\frac12.
$$

For coefficient pairing, since $\Phi$ is real-valued,

$$
\alpha_{h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i h}
=
\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right)
\qquad (h>0),
$$

and

$$
\alpha_{-h,H}
=
-\frac{\Phi(h/(H+1))}{2\pi i(-h)}
=
-\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H+1}\right)
=
\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

Therefore the correct relation is

$$
\boxed{\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.}
$$

The relation $\alpha_{-h,H}=-\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}$ is an algebra error and must not be used in code or proof text.

### 3. R5-Full-R20: Fejer residual product-count bound

**Lemma R5-Full-R20.** Let $X\ge2$ be real. Let

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2},
$$

and let $H$ be an integer satisfying

$$
c_0D X^{-1/4}\le H\le C_0D X^{-1/4}
$$

for fixed constants $0<c_0\le C_0$. Let $w_D$ be a dyadic weight supported on $d\asymp D$ with $|w_D(d)|\le C_w$. Then, for every $\epsilon>0$,

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon},
$$

and, for $\rho\in\{1,3\}$,

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Consequently, assuming H4-R20, the full Vaaler residual from both sawtooth legs contributes

$$
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}
$$

after dyadic summation.

#### Proof: Fejer kernel estimate

The standard Fejer bound is

$$
K_H(t)\ll \min\left(H,\frac1{H\|t\|^2}\right),
$$

where

$$
\|t\|=\min_{m\in\mathbb Z}|t-m|.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac1H K_H(t)
\ll
\min\left(1,\frac1{H^2\|t\|^2}\right).
$$

Define

$$
\Delta=\frac{D}{H}\asymp X^{1/4},
$$

and define the exact-resonance-safe weight

$$
W_\Delta(u)=
\begin{cases}
1,&u=0,\\
\min\left(1,\frac{\Delta^2}{u^2}\right),&u\ne0.
\end{cases}
$$

#### First residual leg

For each $d\asymp D$, choose $m=m(d)$ to be a nearest integer to $X/d$. If there is a tie, choose the smaller nearest integer. Then

$$
\left\|\frac Xd\right\|
=
\left|\frac Xd-m\right|
=
\frac{|X-md|}{d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-md|}{D}.
$$

Hence

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)
\ll
W_\Delta(X-md).
$$

Put

$$
n=md.
$$

For a fixed integer $n$, the number of factorizations $n=md$ with $d\asymp D$ is at most $\tau(n)$. Since $m$ is nearest to $X/d$, all relevant $n$ lie in $X+O(D)$; for large $X$ this is $n\asymp X$. Extending the sum harmlessly,

$$
\frac1H\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|K_H(X/d)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n).
$$

Using the pointwise divisor bound

$$
\tau(n)\ll_\epsilon n^\epsilon,
$$

the central range $|X-n|\le\Delta$ contributes $O_\epsilon(\Delta X^\epsilon)$, and the dyadic annulus

$$
2^{j-1}\Delta<|X-n|\le2^j\Delta
$$

contributes

$$
O_\epsilon\left(X^\epsilon (2^j\Delta)2^{-2j}\right)
=
O_\epsilon(2^{-j}\Delta X^\epsilon).
$$

Summing over $j$ gives

$$
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n)
\ll_\epsilon
\Delta X^\epsilon
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

Exact resonance is safe: if $X/d\in\mathbb Z$, then $K_H(X/d)=K_H(0)=H+1$, so

$$
\frac1H K_H(X/d)\le 1+\frac1H\le2,
$$

and $W_\Delta(0)=1$ captures the correct scale up to an absolute constant.

#### Shifted residual legs

For $\rho\in\{1,3\}$, choose $m=m(d,\rho)$ nearest to

$$
\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}.
$$

Then

$$
\left\|\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right\|
=
\frac{|X-d(4m-\rho)|}{4d}
\asymp
\frac{|X-d(4m-\rho)|}{D}.
$$

Set

$$
\ell=4m-\rho.
$$

Then $\ell$ is an integer satisfying

$$
\ell\equiv-\rho\pmod4.
$$

For $X$ sufficiently large in the active range, $\ell>0$ because $X/d\gg X^{1/2}$ and $m\asymp X/(4d)$. Bounded small-$X$ exceptions are absorbed into the constant. The product is

$$
n=d\ell,
$$

with a congruence restriction on $\ell$ that can only reduce multiplicity. Therefore

$$
\frac1H
\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
K_H\left(\frac{X/d+\rho}{4}\right)
\ll
\sum_{n\asymp X}\tau(n)W_\Delta(X-n)
\ll_\epsilon
X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

#### Zero Fejer mode, frequency signs, dyadic weights, and short blocks

The zero Fejer mode contributes

$$
\frac1{2H+2}\sum_{d\asymp D}|w_D(d)|
\ll
\frac{D}{H}
\asymp
X^{1/4},
$$

which is included in the Fejer-kernel bound. Positive and negative Fejer frequencies are already included in $K_H$; no separate sign argument is needed for residuals. Residual estimates use $|w_D|$ because $K_H$ is nonnegative. Bounded overlap of the dyadic partition contributes only logarithmic loss, absorbed in $X^\epsilon$.

For $D<X^{1/4}$, do not apply Vaaler. Since $|\psi_F(t)|\le1/2$, the total contribution of all short blocks is

$$
\ll \#\{d\le X^{1/4}\}
\ll X^{1/4}.
$$

This completes the proof of R5-Full-R20, conditional only on H4 and the divisor bound.

### 5. M9-R20: official fixed-coefficient main targets

The official main terms use the actual Vaaler coefficients, not arbitrary $u_h$.

Let $w_D$ be a real dyadic weight supported on $d\asymp D$, unless explicitly stated otherwise. Let

$$
H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}.
$$

Define

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
-4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\sum_d
\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),
$$

and

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
4\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}
\left(e(h/4)-e(3h/4)\right)
\sum_d
w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

Since

$$
e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=2i\chi_4(h),
$$

one can also write

$$
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
8i\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
\alpha_{h,H_D}\chi_4(h)
\sum_d
w_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

The open target is:

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal M_1(D;X),\mathcal M_2(D;X)
\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}
}
$$

uniformly over

$$
X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}.
$$

Arbitrary-coefficient versions and termwise $L^1$ norms are stress tests only.

### 6. M9-Pair-R20: paired real implementation formulas

This is an implementation lemma, not a replacement for the raw definitions. It requires real $w_D$ and symmetric positive/negative frequency pairing. If $w_D$ is complex or the frequency cutoff is not symmetric, this lemma must be rederived.

For real $w_D$, define

$$
A_h(D;X)=\sum_d\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d),
$$

and

$$
B_h(D;X)=\sum_dw_D(d)e(hX/(4d)).
$$

Then

$$
A_{-h}=\overline{A_h},
\qquad
B_{-h}=\overline{B_h},
\qquad
\alpha_{-h,H}=\overline{\alpha_{h,H}}.
$$

For $\mathcal M_1$,

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
-4\sum_{1\le h\le H_D}
\left(\alpha_{h,H_D}A_h+\overline{\alpha_{h,H_D}A_h}\right)
=
-8\sum_{1\le h\le H_D}\operatorname{Re}(\alpha_{h,H_D}A_h).
$$

Since, for $h>0$,

$$
\alpha_{h,H_D}
=
\frac{i}{2\pi h}\Phi\left(\frac{h}{H_D+1}\right),
$$

we obtain

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal M_1(D;X)
=
\frac4\pi
\sum_{1\le h\le H_D}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\operatorname{Im}A_h(D;X).
}
$$

For $\mathcal M_2$, only odd $h$ contribute because $e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=0$ for even $h$. For odd $h$,

$$
e(h/4)-e(3h/4)=2i\chi_4(h).
$$

Pairing $h$ and $-h$ gives

$$
\boxed{
\mathcal M_2(D;X)
=
-\frac8\pi
\sum_{\substack{1\le h\le H_D\\2\nmid h}}
\frac{\Phi(h/(H_D+1))}{h}
\chi_4(h)
\operatorname{Re}B_h(D;X).
}
$$

Every numerical implementation should compute both the raw two-sided complex sums and these paired real formulas, then report absolute and relative differences.

### 7. M9b-ShiftedF-R20: theorem-extension checklist

The safer comparison form for $\mathcal M_2$ is not to treat $\chi_4(h)$ as a rough periodic bounded-variation weight. Instead use

$$
e(hX/(4d))(e(h/4)-e(3h/4))
=
e\left(h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac14\right)\right)
-
e\left(h\left(\frac{X}{4d}+\frac34\right)\right).
$$

Writing $d=Dz$ and taking $T=X$, $M=D$, this has Li--Yang-type phase

$$
e\left(\frac{hT}{M}F_{\rho,D}(z)\right),
$$

where

$$
F_{\rho,D}(z)
=
\frac1{4z}+\frac{\rho D}{4X},
\qquad
\rho\in\{1,3\}.
$$

The derivative checks are uniform on $z\in[1,2]$:

$$
F'_{\rho,D}(z)=-\frac1{4z^2},
\qquad
F''_{\rho,D}(z)=\frac1{2z^3},
\qquad
F'''_{\rho,D}(z)=-\frac3{2z^4}.
$$

Thus

$$
F'F'''-3(F'')^2
=
\frac3{8z^6}-\frac3{4z^6}
=
-\frac3{8z^6}.
$$

The additive constant $\rho D/(4X)$ does not alter derivative nondegeneracy.

A theorem sufficient for M9b must allow all of the following:

1. $F_{\rho,D}$ may depend on $D,X$ through an additive constant, uniformly in $X^{1/4}\le D\le X^{1/2}$.
2. The $h$-weights are the actual Vaaler weights, or can be handled by dyadic partial summation without losing the endpoint.
3. The endpoint height

$$
H\asymp D X^{-1/4}
$$

is allowed.
4. Positive and negative frequencies can be paired or the theorem is stable under the paired real formulation.
5. Smooth dyadic denominator weights $w_D$ are allowed.
6. The theorem treats the fractional-frequency shift formulation rather than absorbing $\chi_4(h)$ as a high-variation periodic weight.

This is a theorem-extension problem, not an available black-box theorem.

\mathcal D_{\rm odd}=\{d:D\le d<2D,\ 2\nmid d\},
\qquad
\mathcal V_D=\ell^2(\mathcal D_{\rm odd}),
$$

and

$$
U=\operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(d))_{d\in\mathcal D_{\rm odd}}.
$$

Since $d$ is odd,

$$
\chi_4(d)\in\{\pm1\},
\qquad
U^*U=I.
$$

Therefore for every matrix $K$ on $\mathcal V_D$,

$$
\boxed{\|U^*KU\|_{\operatorname{op}}=\|K\|_{\operatorname{op}}.}
$$

This blocks only methods where the character enters solely as diagonal unitary conjugation and the next step uses operator norm, spectral radius, Frobenius norm, Schur/Gershgorin, absolute-value matrices, or pure conjugacy-invariant norms.

It does not block direct signed bilinear estimates, open-path statistics, non-conjugacy residue forms, cross-residue interference statistics, or estimates that exploit $\chi_4$ before norm extraction.

The pure cyclic trace identity is similarly narrow. For integer $m\ge1$,

$$
\operatorname{Tr}((U^*KU)^m)
=
\operatorname{Tr}(U^*K^mU)
=
\operatorname{Tr}(K^m).
$$

Thus pure cyclic traces are character-blind under diagonal conjugation. This does not block expressions such as $v^*(U^*KU)^mv$ or $\operatorname{Tr}(KU)$, which are not conjugacy-invariant in the same way.

### 10. BSOS-M9a-R20: conditional signed-statistic framework

Freeze the $|\alpha_h|$-weighted Cauchy--Schwarz normalization for $\mathcal M_1$.

Let

$$
L_D=\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}|\alpha_{h,H_D}|.
$$

Since $|\alpha_{h,H_D}|\asymp 1/|h|$ away from harmless endpoint constants,

$$
L_D\asymp\log H_D.
$$

Cauchy--Schwarz gives

$$
|\mathcal M_1(D;X)|^2
\le
16L_D
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
|\alpha_{h,H_D}|
\left|
\sum_{d\in\mathcal D_{\rm odd}}
\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d)
\right|^2.
$$

Define the kernel

$$
K_{d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}
=
w_D(d_1)w_D(d_2)
\sum_{1\le |h|\le H_D}
|\alpha_{h,H_D}|
e\left(hX\left(\frac1{d_1}-\frac1{d_2}\right)\right).
$$

Then the second factor is

$$
\mathcal S_{\rm diag}+\mathcal S_{\rm signed},
$$

where

$$
\mathcal S_{\rm diag}
=
\sum_{d\in\mathcal D_{\rm odd}}K_{d,d}^{(|\alpha|)}
\asymp D\log H_D,
$$

and

$$
\mathcal S_{\rm signed}
=
\sum_{\substack{d_1,d_2\in\mathcal D_{\rm odd}\\d_1\ne d_2}}
\chi_4(d_1)\chi_4(d_2)
K_{d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}.
$$

The diagonal contribution to $|\mathcal M_1|$ is therefore

$$
\ll \sqrt{L_D\mathcal S_{\rm diag}}
\asymp
D^{1/2}\log H_D,
$$

which is acceptable because $D\le X^{1/2}$ and logarithms are absorbed in $X^\epsilon$.

A sufficient signed off-diagonal target is

$$
\boxed{
|\mathcal S_{\rm signed}|\ll_\epsilon X^{1/2+\epsilon}.
}
$$

Indeed, then

$$
|\mathcal M_1(D;X)|^2
\ll_\epsilon
(\log X)(X^{1/2+\epsilon}),
$$

so

$$
\mathcal M_1(D;X)\ll_\epsilon X^{1/4+\epsilon}.
$$

This is only a conditional implication. No estimate for $\mathcal S_{\rm signed}$ is proved here.

For falsification, define

$$
\mathcal S_{\rm abs}
=
\sum_{\substack{d_1,d_2\in\mathcal D_{\rm odd}\\d_1\ne d_2}}
\left|K_{d_1,d_2}^{(|\alpha|)}\right|,
$$

and

$$
\mathcal S_{\rm op}
=
|\mathcal D_{\rm odd}|
\left\|K_{\rm off}^{(|\alpha|)}\right\|_{\operatorname{op}}.
$$

If numerically

$$
|\mathcal S_{\rm signed}|
\asymp
\mathcal S_{\rm abs}
\quad\text{or}\quad
|\mathcal S_{\rm signed}|
\asymp
\mathcal S_{\rm op}
$$

in endpoint regimes, this particular BSOS statistic is unlikely to give the needed saving. If it is consistently much smaller, it becomes a candidate for a proof attempt.

The analogous BSOS framework for $\mathcal M_2$ is still missing. It must be built from the shifted-$F$ formulation, not by reusing the M9a kernel.

### 11. H13-Falsification-R20: endpoint checklist only

H13 is exploratory. It should remain a falsification checklist unless a complete post-transform signed theorem is written.

For M9a, set

$$
f(u)=w_D(u)e(hX/u).
$$

Splitting modulo $4$ and applying Poisson summation under $e(t)=e^{2\pi i t}$ gives formally

$$
\sum_d\chi_4(d)f(d)
=
\frac14\sum_{k\in\mathbb Z}
\left(e(k/4)-e(3k/4)\right)
\widehat f(k/4)
=
\frac{i}{2}\sum_{k\in\mathbb Z}\chi_4(k)\widehat f(k/4),
$$

where

$$
\widehat f(\xi)=\int_{\mathbb R}w_D(u)e(hX/u-\xi u)\,du.
$$

The phase is

$$
\phi_k(u)=\frac{hX}{u}-\frac{k u}{4}.
$$

Stationary points occur only for $k<0$. Writing $k=-m$ with $m>0$,

$$
\phi_{-m}(u)=\frac{hX}{u}+\frac{m u}{4},
$$

and

$$
u_0=2\sqrt{\frac{hX}{m}}.
$$

The leading phase is

$$
\phi_{-m}(u_0)=\sqrt{hXm}.
$$

The active dual length is

$$
m\asymp\frac{hX}{D^2}.
$$

At maximal Vaaler height $h\asymp H_D\asymp D X^{-1/4}$ and $D=X^\delta$,

$$
m\asymp X^{3/4-\delta}.
$$

Thus near $\delta=1/2$, the dual length is $X^{1/4}$, comparable to the original endpoint frequency length. For smaller $D$, the dual length grows.

The continuous dual phase

$$
\Phi(h,m)=\sqrt{Xhm}
$$

has Hessian determinant zero, so generic full-rank two-dimensional stationary phase or full-rank decoupling cannot be applied directly.

H13 passes a first sanity check only if, after transformation and the first intended spacing step, the dual character $\chi_4(m)$ survives in a non-conjugacy signed statistic. It fails as a sign-preserving route at that step if the expression becomes

$$
v^*U^*KUv,
\qquad
U=\operatorname{diag}(\chi_4(m)),
$$

and is then bounded by an operator norm or absolute-value matrix.

## Counterexample or obstruction search

1. **Exact Fejer resonances.** If $X/d\in\mathbb Z$ or $(X/d+\rho)/4\in\mathbb Z$, the proof must use $W_\Delta(0)=1$ and exact $K_H(0)=H+1$. Floating-point sine evaluations near integers can produce false spikes or false cancellations.

## Divergent alternatives and 20% exploration

### Signed Fourier truncation

A signed Fourier truncation keeps the formal tails

$$
\sum_{|h|>H_D}\frac1h
\sum_{d\asymp D}\chi_4(d)w_D(d)e(hX/d)
$$

and the analogous second-leg tail with the frequency character. This preserves signs longer than the Fejer majorant, but it replaces R5 by a high-frequency tail estimate. No current argument bounds that tail at $X^{1/4+\epsilon}$ without reintroducing absolute-value or endpoint reciprocal-sum difficulties. Therefore it does not change the active target: M9 remains the bottleneck.

A quick falsification test is to truncate at $H_D$ and compute the signed tail for square and near-square $X$. If the tail behaves like the Fejer residual or the $L^1$ stress norm, signed truncation is unlikely to improve the route.

### Mellin--Perron comparison

The Mellin--Perron formulation uses the Dirichlet series

$$
4\zeta(s)L(s,\chi_4)
$$

for the two-square coefficients. It preserves arithmetic structure at the series level, but after truncation, contour shifting, and functional equations, it is expected to reconstruct Hardy/Voronoi/Bessel-type oscillatory sums. No current contour estimate replaces M9 with an easier theorem.

A quick falsification test is to write a smoothed Perron kernel with truncation height $T_P$, apply the functional equation, and compare the resulting dual length and phase with the existing Bessel/Voronoi scale. If the dual length is again $X^{1/2}$ at endpoint precision, the method is a comparison module, not a pivot.

Neither alternative should replace the balanced hyperbola/Vaaler route in the next round.
